# 第007-2章：最后面的人，最前面的心

## 典故引入

### 第1页

美国独立战争快结束时，华盛顿的军营里很危险。士兵多年拿不到军饷，有人想用武力逼国会让步。

愤怒像干草堆，只差一点火星。

### 第2页

华盛顿没有带兵冲进去压人。他独自走进会场，面对那些按着佩剑的军官。

读信读到一半，他拿出老花眼镜，慢慢戴上。大家忽然看见：这位将军也老了，也为战争付出了身体。

### 第3页

那一刻，刀剑没有再往前。许多军官低下头，兵变的火慢慢熄了。

华盛顿没有把威风摆在前面。他把疲惫和诚恳摆在前面，反而守住了国家的路。

### 第4页

印度的甘地，也不是用高台和刀剑走在前面。英国殖民政府不许印度人自己取盐，他就决定走到海边。

他穿着粗布，拄着拐杖，从修行所出发，一步一步向大海走去。

### 第5页

二十四天里，路越走越长，跟随的人越来越多。到海边时，他弯腰拾起一小撮盐。

一个弯腰的动作，比许多愤怒的口号更响。把自己放低，许多人反而看见了方向。

### 第6页

南非的曼德拉，被关在罗本岛监狱二十七年。白天，他在采石场工作，石灰岩的反光刺伤了眼睛。

如果只按个人痛苦来说，他有太多理由把仇恨放在最前面。

### 第7页

可是出狱以后，他没有让国家跟着自己的伤口一起燃烧。他邀请旧日狱卒参加就职典礼，也推动真相与和解。

他没有忘记伤害，却把南非的未来放在自己的痛苦前面。

### 第8页

中国的范仲淹写《岳阳楼记》，也把自己的心放到很远很大。

他写洞庭湖的阴晴风雨，不只是在写一座楼，也是在写一个人怎样看天下。

### 第9页

他留下“先天下之忧而忧，后天下之乐而乐”。忧的时候站在前面，享乐的时候退到后面。

这句话听起来像吃亏，却让他的名字和胸怀留了很久。

### 第10页

这些故事都不是要孩子去逞英雄。第七章说的“外其身”，不是乱冒险。

它是说：在重要关头，不让“我先舒服、我先安全、我先有名”挡住该做的事。

### 第11页

华盛顿面对怒火，甘地面对帝国，曼德拉面对旧怨，范仲淹面对天下忧乐。

他们的“后”，不是缩在角落；他们的“先”，也不是抢第一名。

### 第12页

真正的先，是先承担。真正的后，是把奖赏、舒服和名声往后放一放。

人若只是喊“我很无私”，其实私心已经在台上敲锣了。

### 第13页

“无私”不是心里空空，没有爱，也没有愿望。

它是有更大的愿望：让国家不乱，让人能取盐，让仇恨少一点，让天下人少一些忧。

## 回扣

### 第14页

所以老子说，圣人后其身而身先。一个人把自己放在后面，大家反而愿意跟着他走。

因为人们看得见：他不是来占便宜，而是来承担。

### 第15页

老子又说，外其身而身存。一个人不把自己那点得失看得最大，反而保存了更深的自己。

华盛顿保存了信任，甘地保存了尊严，曼德拉保存了共同生活的可能。

### 第16页

“故能成其私”也不是教人绕弯子求私利。

它是在说：当一个人不追着自己的小私跑，生命里真正珍贵的东西，反而可能保留下来。

### 第17页

在孩子的生活里，这也能练习。排队时让更小的人先走，分东西时先看谁更需要，遇到难事时不把责任推开。

这些事不大，却能把心慢慢养宽。

### 第18页

第七章像一句反过来的话：最后面的人，可能有最前面的心。

阿禾在本子上写：把自己放小一点，不是消失；是给更大的事情让出路。

# Chapter 007-2: The Last Person, the Foremost Heart

## Allusion

### Page 1

Near the end of the American War of Independence, Washington's army camp was in danger. Soldiers had gone unpaid for years, and some wanted to pressure Congress with force.

Anger was like dry grass, waiting for a spark.

### Page 2

Washington did not march in with threats. He entered the meeting alone and faced officers whose hands were near their swords.

While reading a letter, he paused and put on his glasses. Suddenly they saw that their general had grown old too, and that war had cost him his body.

### Page 3

At that moment, the swords did not move forward. Many officers lowered their heads, and the fire of mutiny slowly went out.

Washington did not place power in front. He placed weariness and sincerity in front, and the country's road was preserved.

### Page 4

Gandhi in India also did not walk in front with a high platform or a sword. The colonial law forbade Indians to gather their own salt, so he chose to walk to the sea.

In rough cloth, with a walking stick, he left his ashram and moved step by step toward the ocean.

### Page 5

For twenty-four days, the road grew longer, and more people joined. At the shore, he bent down and picked up a small bit of salt.

That bending motion spoke louder than many angry slogans. By lowering himself, he helped many people see a direction.

### Page 6

Nelson Mandela in South Africa spent twenty-seven years in prison on Robben Island. By day, he worked in a stone quarry, where white limestone glare damaged his eyes.

If he had put personal pain first, he had many reasons to place hatred in front.

### Page 7

But after leaving prison, he did not make the country burn with his wound. He invited former jailers to his inauguration and supported truth and reconciliation.

He did not forget harm, but he placed South Africa's future before his private hurt.

### Page 8

In China, Fan Zhongyan wrote "The Record of Yueyang Tower" with a heart that looked far and wide.

He wrote of the wind, rain, brightness, and darkness over Dongting Lake. He was not only writing about a tower; he was writing about how a person sees the world.

### Page 9

He left the thought: be first to worry for the world, and last to enjoy its joy. Stand in front for sorrow; step behind for pleasure.

It sounds like loss, yet his name and breadth of heart lasted a long time.

### Page 10

These stories do not tell children to chase danger. Chapter Seven's "treating the self as outside" is not reckless risk.

It means that at an important moment, "my comfort, my safety, my fame" should not block what must be done.

### Page 11

Washington faced anger. Gandhi faced an empire. Mandela faced old hatred. Fan Zhongyan faced the worries and joys of all under heaven.

Their "behind" was not hiding in a corner. Their "front" was not grabbing first place.

### Page 12

True front means carrying first. True behind means putting reward, comfort, and fame a little farther back.

If someone only shouts, "I am selfless," private desire may already be beating a drum on stage.

### Page 13

"No private ends" does not mean an empty heart with no love and no wish.

It means having a larger wish: less disorder, more dignity, less hatred, and fewer worries for the people.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

So Laozi says the sage puts himself behind and is found in front. When someone puts himself behind, others may be willing to follow.

They can see that he has not come to take advantage. He has come to carry.

### Page 15

Laozi also says the sage treats himself as outside, and so he is preserved. When a person does not make small gain and loss the biggest thing, a deeper self is preserved.

Washington preserved trust, Gandhi preserved dignity, and Mandela preserved the possibility of living together.

### Page 16

"Private ends are realised" does not teach people to seek selfish gain by a clever turn.

It means that when a person stops chasing the small private self, what is truly precious in life may remain.

### Page 17

Children can practice this too. In line, let someone younger go first. When sharing, look first at who needs more. When trouble comes, do not push the task away.

These things are small, but they slowly widen the heart.

### Page 18

Chapter Seven speaks like a sentence turned around: the person at the very back may have the foremost heart.

Ahe wrote in his notebook: Making myself smaller is not disappearing. It is making room for something larger to pass.
