# 第000-2章：青牛走到关口

## 第1页

第二天一早，阿禾又坐在大树下等老人。

他翻着本子问："老子骑青牛走了以后，那些话是怎么留下来的？"

老人说："这就要讲到一个关口。"

## 第2页

"这是传说部分。"老人先提醒。

"传说不能当作历史，但常常藏着人们想说的意思。"

大家点点头，围得更近了。

## 第3页

传说里，老子骑青牛到了函谷关。

那是周朝西边的重要关口。出了关，就离开了周朝的地界。

守关的人叫尹喜，也被尊称为关尹子。

## 第4页

尹喜不是普通守门人。

传说他会"望气"，能看云气的颜色和形状。

有一天，他看见东方有紫色云气慢慢飘来，心里想：有圣人要来了。

## 第5页

"紫气东来！"阿禾说，"我听过。"

老人点头："后来这成了成语，意思是好事将近，贵客要到。"

小鸟抬头看云，好像也想看出一点紫色。

## 第6页

果然，一位白发老人骑着青牛来了。

尹喜没有为难他，只是诚恳地请求："您要出关远去，能不能留下最重要的话？"

老子停了下来。

## 第7页

老人说："老子本不爱多说。"

"他知道，真正的道理很难被嘴说完。说出来的，只能像手指指月亮。"

"可是尹喜诚心相请，他就写了。"

## 第8页

"写了多少？"小石头问。

老人伸出五根手指："大约五千个字。后来的人把它分成八十一章，方便慢慢读。"

写完后，老子骑上青牛，出了关。

## 第9页

"后来他去哪儿了？"小兔问。

老人摇头："没有人能确定。有人说他继续往西，有人说他融进云里。"

"能确定的是，那五千个字留下来了。"

## 第10页

阿禾想象黄昏的关门。

青牛慢慢走远，尹喜手里抱着一卷竹简。

一个人走了，一本书的路才刚开始。

## 第11页

那时没有印刷机。

竹简会断，绳子会松，字也可能被虫蛀掉。

每多一本，都要有人用手一字一字抄下来。

## 第12页

"抄书会不会抄错？"阿禾问。

"会。"老人说，"眼睛一花，就可能多一笔、少一句。"

"同一本书，传久了，就会长出不同的样子。"

## 第13页

有时，连避讳也会改变字。

比如早期有些本子写"恒"，后来因皇帝名字要避开，就常写成"常"。

所以我们今天读到的是一条流过许多年代的河。

## 第14页

"那最早的样子还找得到吗？"小石头问。

老人说："很难。但考古帮我们看见了更早的水。"

"1973 年，马王堆出土了帛书《老子》。"

## 第15页

帛书写在丝绸上，已经埋了两千多年。

后来，郭店又出土了更早的竹简，不过只留下部分章节。

阿禾写下：*同一条河，在不同地方，颜色会不一样。*

# Chapter 000-2: The Blue-Green Ox Reaches the Pass

## Page 1

Early the next morning, Ahe waited again beneath the big tree.

He opened his notebook and asked, "After Laozi rode away on the blue-green ox, how were his words left behind?"

"For that," said the old man, "we must speak of a pass."

## Page 2

"This part is legend," the old man said first.

"A legend is not the same as history. But it often carries what people wanted to say."

Everyone nodded and leaned closer.

## Page 3

In the legend, Laozi rode his blue-green ox to Hangu Pass.

It was an important western gate of the Zhou realm. Once through it, one had left Zhou territory.

The keeper of the pass was named Yin Xi. He was also respectfully called Master Yin of the Pass.

## Page 4

Yin Xi was not an ordinary gatekeeper.

Legend says he could "watch the vapors," reading the colors and shapes of clouds.

One day, he saw purple vapor drifting from the east. His heart stirred: a sage was coming.

## Page 5

"Zi qi dong lai!" Ahe said. "Purple vapor from the east. I have heard that."

The old man nodded. "Later it became an idiom. It means good fortune is near, or an honored guest is arriving."

Little Bird looked up, trying to find purple in the clouds.

## Page 6

Sure enough, an old man with white hair came riding a blue-green ox.

Yin Xi did not trouble him. He asked sincerely, "You are going beyond the pass. Could you leave your most important words?"

Laozi stopped.

## Page 7

"Laozi did not love too much speaking," said the old man.

"He knew that true understanding cannot be finished by the mouth. Spoken words are like a finger pointing to the moon."

"But Yin Xi asked with sincerity. So Laozi wrote."

## Page 8

"How much did he write?" Little Stone asked.

The old man held up five fingers. "About five thousand characters. Later people divided them into eighty-one chapters, so they could be read slowly."

When it was done, Laozi mounted the ox and passed through the gate.

## Page 9

"Where did he go afterward?" Little Rabbit asked.

The old man shook his head. "No one knows for certain. Some say he kept going west. Some say his story dissolved into clouds."

"What we can say is this: the five thousand characters remained."

## Page 10

Ahe imagined the pass at dusk.

The blue-green ox moved slowly away. Yin Xi held a roll of bamboo slips in his arms.

One person had left. The journey of a book had just begun.

## Page 11

There were no printing presses then.

Bamboo slips could snap. Cords could loosen. Worms could eat the written strips.

Every new copy had to be written by hand, character by character.

## Page 12

"Could copying make mistakes?" Ahe asked.

"Yes," said the old man. "A tired eye might add a stroke or miss a phrase."

"When one book travels for a long time, it grows different faces."

## Page 13

Sometimes even taboo changed a word.

Some early texts used *heng*. Later, because an emperor's name used that character, many copies wrote *chang* instead.

So the text we read today is a river that has passed through many ages.

## Page 14

"Can we still find its earliest shape?" Little Stone asked.

"It is difficult," said the old man. "But archaeology lets us see earlier water."

"In 1973, the Mawangdui silk manuscripts of the *Laozi* were unearthed."

## Page 15

Those words were written on silk and had rested underground for more than two thousand years.

Later, even older bamboo slips were found at Guodian, though they preserved only parts of the book.

Ahe wrote: *The same river may have different colors in different places.*
