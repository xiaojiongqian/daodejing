# 第002章：一对一对出现

## 原文

### 第1页

天下皆知美之为美，斯恶已；皆知善之为善，斯不善已。

故有无相生，难易相成，长短相形，高下相倾，音声相和，前后相随。

### 第2页

是以圣人处无为之事，行不言之教。

万物作焉而不辞，生而不有，为而不恃，功成而弗居。夫唯弗居，是以不去。

## 释义

### 第3页

当所有人都指着一件东西说"这才叫美"，别的样子就被推到了"不美"那一边。当所有人都抢着说"只有这样做才对"，别的做法就被推到了"不对"那一边。老子不是反对美和善——他只是提醒：忙着比较和评判，心里就会长出许多争抢。

### 第4页

万物常常一对一对显出来。有和无、难和易、长和短、高和低——都不是单独站着的。拿出长的，旁边那根才显得短；站到高处，低处才显得低。它们互相照见，互相帮对方出现。

### 第5页

声音和回声也是一对——敲一下，山谷回应，一个人成不了合奏。所以懂道的人不硬抢、不一直喊，让事情自己长。做成了，不把名字贴上去，事情反而留住了。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

「美」就是好看、让人喜欢的样子；「恶」在这里不是坏，是指不好看、不美。「善」就是好、做得对；「不善」是被推到"不好"那一边——不等于真的不好，只是被比较出来的。「有无相生」——有和无互相生出对方。像一只碗：碗壁是"有"，中间空的是"无"，没有那个空就装不了东西。

### 第7页

「难易相成」——难和易互相对照才成立。「长短相形」——长和短放在一起才显出彼此。「高下相倾」——高和低互相对照，谁也离不开谁。「音声相和」——单独一声叫"声"，声互相应和才变成音乐。「处无为之事」——做事不硬来，顺着事情自己的节奏帮它长。「功成而弗居」——做成了却不占着功劳不放；因为不霸占，那份好反而一直在。

## 正文

### 第8页

很久以前——比你的爷爷的爷爷还要早得多——中国出了一位很会讲故事的先生，名字叫庄子。庄子喜欢用一个简单的问题敲开人心：什么是美，什么是不美？他讲过这样一个故事。那时候有一位出名的美人，叫西施。

### 第9页

她住在一条小河边，常常去河边洗衣服。有一天她忽然心口疼，手按着胸口，微微皱起眉头。路过的人看见她这个样子，都说：呀，比平时更好看了。不是因为她故意摆姿势——是因为心疼和皱眉这些事，长在她身上，自然就动人。

### 第10页

同村还有一位女子，叫东施。她远远看见了，心想：原来捧心皱眉就是美。她也按着胸口，皱起眉头，走到人前——大家一看，吓得关上大门，拉着孩子跑得远远的。庄子说："她只知道皱眉可以美，却不知道为什么皱眉在西施身上才美。"

### 第11页

这是老子说的"天下皆知美之为美，斯恶已"——大家都去追同一个"美"，丑就跟着被造出来了。美和丑是一对——它们不是天生就分好的，是你追我赶、互相照出来的。

### 第12页

不只是美和丑。很多事都是这样一对一对的。古时候有一本书叫《乐记》，里面说：人心被外面的事情触动了，发出来就是"声"。一个声和另一个声互相应答、变化成章，这才叫"音"。短的和长的放在一起，高的和低的互相依托——单单一根竹子敲不出曲子，两个声音碰在一起，音乐才出生了。

### 第13页

所以老子说，"有无相生，难易相成，长短相形，高下相倾，音声相和，前后相随"。世界不是一块一块拼起来的——它是声音追着回声、回声道着下一声，一对接一对、一环扣一环，往前走。

### 第14页

懂了这些的圣人，做事是这样的：不硬来，不说话去压服人，做给你看。万物长起来他不拦着，做完了不把名字贴上去。"夫唯弗居，是以不去"——正因为他不占着不放，那个好才一直留在世间，谁也拿不走。

## 回扣

### 第15页

回来看这一章：老子先讲了一串成对的东西——美丑、有无、难易、长短、高下、音声、前后。他不是在列清单。他在说一个更大的道理：你别只看一边。丑不是美失败的样子，短不是长的残次品。它们是一对——互相照出彼此。

### 第16页

最后两句："万物作焉而不辞，生而不有，为而不恃，功成而弗居。夫唯弗居，是以不去。"做你该做的事，别一直喊"是我做的"。像西施——她不是故意要美，所以美。像长短两根竹子碰在一起——它们不是故意要成为曲子，所以成了。

# Chapter 002: Things Appear in Pairs

## Original Text

### Page 1

All in the world know the beauty of the beautiful, and in doing this they have (the idea of) what ugliness is; they all know the skill of the skilful, and in doing this they have (the idea of) what the want of skill is.

So it is that existence and non-existence give birth the one to (the idea of) the other; that difficulty and ease produce the one (the idea of) the other; that length and shortness fashion out the one the figure of the other; that (the ideas of) height and lowness arise from the contrast of the one with the other; that the musical notes and tones become harmonious through the relation of one with another; and that being before and behind give the idea of one following another.

### Page 2

Therefore the sage manages affairs without doing anything, and conveys his instructions without the use of speech.

All things spring up, and there is not one which declines to show itself; they grow, and there is no claim made for their ownership; they go through their processes, and there is no expectation (of a reward for the results). The work is accomplished, and there is no resting in it (as an achievement).

The work is done, but how no one can see;
'Tis this that makes the power not cease to be.

> — Translated by James Legge (1891), *The Texts of Taoism*, Sacred Books of the East, Vol. 39. Public domain.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

When everyone points at the same thing and says "Only this is beautiful," everything else gets pushed to the "not beautiful" side. When everyone says "Only this is right," others get pushed to the "not right" side. Laozi is not against beauty or goodness — he is reminding us: the moment we get busy comparing and judging, our hearts fill up with competition.

### Page 4

So many things appear in pairs: being and nonbeing, hard and easy, long and short, high and low. None stands completely alone. Hold up a long pole, and the one beside it looks shorter; stand on a high step, and the ground below looks lower. They help each other appear.

### Page 5

Sound and echo are a pair too — tap once, the hillside answers; one person alone cannot make harmony. So the sage does not push or shout. He lets things grow. When the work is done, he does not paste his name on it — and because he does not cling, the work stays.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**Beauty of the beautiful.** Legge's phrase for the idea that only one thing counts as beautiful. The moment you set that standard, you create its opposite: ugliness.

**The skilful / want of skill.** When you declare what is "skilled," you instantly create "not skilled enough." Labeling one side creates the other.

**Existence and non-existence give birth the one to (the idea of) the other.** Think of a bowl: the clay walls are "being," the hollow is "nonbeing." Without the hollow, it holds nothing.

### Page 7

**Difficulty and ease produce the one (the idea of) the other.** Tying shoelaces felt impossible the first time — hard taught you what easy means.

**Length and shortness fashion out the one the figure of the other.** Long and short reveal each other only side by side.

**(The ideas of) height and lowness arise from the contrast of the one with the other.** Stand on a peak, the valley looks low; stand in the valley, the peak looks high.

**The musical notes and tones become harmonious through the relation of one with another.** A single tap is just a noise; different sounds answering each other become music.

**Manages affairs without doing anything.** Not forcing, not meddling — working with the natural flow.

**No resting in it (as an achievement).** Step aside instead of sitting on your work. Because you do not cling, it lives on.

## Main Text

### Page 8

A long time ago — longer than your grandfather's grandfather — there lived in China a wonderful storyteller named Zhuangzi. Zhuangzi loved to crack open people's hearts with a simple question: What is beauty, and what isn't? He once told this story. In those days there was a famous beauty called Xi Shi.

### Page 9

She lived by a small river and often went there to wash clothes. One day her chest began to hurt, so she pressed her hand to her heart and her brows drew together, just a little. The people passing by saw her and said: ah, even more lovely than usual. Not because she was striking a pose — but because the gesture grew from her own life, naturally.

### Page 10

In the same village lived another woman named Dong Shi. She saw this from a distance and thought: so that is all beauty is — hand on heart, knitted brow. She pressed her chest, furrowed her forehead, and walked out among the people. At the sight of her, everyone slammed their doors shut and dragged their children far away. Zhuangzi said: "She knew the frown could be beautiful, but she did not know why it was beautiful on Xi Shi."

### Page 11

This is what Laozi means by "all in the world know the beauty of the beautiful, and in doing this they have the idea of what ugliness is." When everyone chases the same idea of beauty, ugliness is born right beside it. Beauty and ugliness are a pair — they are not separate by nature; they are made by the chase, each proving the other's existence.

### Page 12

It is not only beauty and ugliness. Many things come in pairs like this. An ancient book called the *Record of Music* says: when the heart is stirred by something outside, what comes forth is a *sound*. When one sound answers another and unfolds into a pattern, that is a *tone*. Short and long placed together, high and low leaning on each other — a single bamboo pipe tapped alone makes no music. Two sounds meeting, answering — that is where music is born.

### Page 13

So says Laozi: existence and non-existence give birth to each other, difficulty and ease complete each other, long and short shape each other, high and low rest on each other, musical notes and tones harmonize with each other, before and behind follow each other. The world is not built of separate blocks. Sound chases echo, echo calls the next sound — pair after pair, link after link, the whole thing moves forward.

### Page 14

The sage who understands this works like this: no forcing, no shouting, no lecturing — he shows you by doing. The ten thousand things rise up, and he does not block them. The work is finished, and he does not paste his name across it. "The work is done, but how no one can see; 'Tis this that makes the power not cease to be." Because he does not cling, the goodness stays in the world, and no one can take it away.

## Return to the Original

### Page 15

Look back at this chapter. Laozi first lists pairs — beauty and ugliness, being and nonbeing, hard and easy, long and short, high and low, sound and echo, before and behind. He is not making a checklist. He is saying something larger: do not look at only one side. Ugliness is not beauty failed; short is not long diminished. They are a pair — each reveals the other.

### Page 16

The final lines: "All things spring up, and there is not one which declines to show itself; they grow, and there is no claim made for their ownership… The work is accomplished, and there is no resting in it." Do the work you are meant to do, and do not keep shouting "I made this." Like Xi Shi — she did not try to be beautiful, and so she was. Like two bamboo pipes of different lengths tapping together — they did not try to be a song, and so they became one.
