# 第000-3章：这本书来到耳边

## 第1页

第三天，阿禾带来一片树叶。

他说："同一棵树，每片叶子都不一样。同一本书，也会这样吗？"

老人笑了："会。今天讲它怎样被人读懂。"

## 第2页

在很长时间里，人们主要靠传世本读《道德经》。

其中有两个注本特别重要。

一个叫河上公本，一个叫王弼本。

## 第3页

河上公是汉代传说中的隐士。

故事说，他住在河边小屋里，连皇帝来请教，也不急着迎接。

这个故事多半是传说，但河上公注本确实流传下来。

## 第4页

河上公常把《老子》讲成治国和修身的书。

他一章一章解释，像把难走的小路插上木牌。

阿禾说："这样读的人，就不容易迷路。"

## 第5页

另一个重要人物叫王弼。

他生活在三国时代，非常聪明，却只活了二十三岁。

他写下《老子注》，影响了后世许多读法。

## 第6页

王弼特别重视"有"和"无"、"自然"和"无为"。

他说"无为"不是懒着不做，而是不凭私心乱做。

这正适合给孩子讲：帮事情长，不替事情乱长。

## 第7页

老人说："我们书里的中文原文，主要依通行的王弼系统。"

"有些地方，也参考帛书、竹简和校本。"

阿禾写下：读古书，要尊重来路。

## 第8页

"每个年代的人，读到的都一样吗？"小兔问。

"不完全一样。"老人说，"乱世的人读到安静，做官的人读到不贪。"

"好书像光，照进不同的屋子。"

## 第9页

小石头问："那别的国家也读吗？"

老人说："从十七、十八世纪起，传教士和汉学家把它带到欧洲。"

后来，它被翻译成许多语言，走到世界很多地方。

## 第10页

"本书英文原文采用 James Legge 的译本。"

"他的中文名叫理雅各，1891 年出版完整英译。"

"这个译本年代早，考据认真，也已经进入公共领域。"

## 第11页

"翻译'道'很难。"老人说。

"译成 Way，像路；译成 Path，也像路。可是'道'不只是路。"

"所以有些译者干脆写 Dao，让读者慢慢体会。"

## 第12页

阿禾想起大象。

叫"粗柱子怪"不够，叫"大象"比较好。

可是"大象"也只是名字。真正的大象，会呼吸，会走路，也会用鼻子卷青草。

## 第13页

"那我们读它，是为了背答案吗？"阿禾问。

老人摇头："不是。是为了学老子看水。"

"不急着抢第一，不急着骂人。慢慢看，轻轻想。"

## 第14页

夕阳下，大家走在田埂上。

老人说："阿禾，你像尹喜。你总请老人把话说出来。"

"你的名字本，就像当年摊开的竹简。"

## 第15页

夜里，阿禾写下最后一行：

*老子不是把道理讲完的人。他像把门推开的人。*

门开着。明天，他们就要正式走进第一章。

# Chapter 000-3: This Book Reaches Our Ears

## Page 1

On the third day, Ahe brought a leaf.

"Leaves from the same tree are all a little different," he said. "Can the same book be like that too?"

The old man smiled. "Yes. Today we speak of how people came to read it."

## Page 2

For a long time, people read the *Dao De Jing* through received texts.

Two commentarial lines became especially important.

One is called the Heshang Gong version. The other is called the Wang Bi version.

## Page 3

Heshang Gong was a legendary hermit of the Han dynasty.

Stories say he lived in a hut by the river. Even when an emperor came to ask for teaching, he did not hurry out.

That tale is probably legend. But the Heshang Gong commentary truly came down to later readers.

## Page 4

Heshang Gong often reads the *Laozi* as a book for governing and self-cultivation.

He explains chapter by chapter, like placing little signs along a difficult path.

"Then readers do not get lost so easily," Ahe said.

## Page 5

Another important reader was Wang Bi.

He lived in the Three Kingdoms period. He was brilliant, but died at only twenty-three.

His commentary on Laozi shaped many later ways of reading the book.

## Page 6

Wang Bi cared deeply about "being" and "nonbeing," "naturalness" and "non-forcing."

For him, *wuwei* does not mean laziness. It means not acting from selfish meddling.

For children, we might say: help things grow; do not grow in their place.

## Page 7

"The Chinese text in our book mainly follows the received Wang Bi line," said the old man.

"In some places, we also check silk manuscripts, bamboo slips, and collated editions."

Ahe wrote: to read an old book, respect the road it traveled.

## Page 8

"Does every age find the same thing in it?" Little Rabbit asked.

"Not exactly," said the old man. "People in chaos find stillness. Officials may find a warning against greed."

"A good book is like light. It enters many different rooms."

## Page 9

Little Stone asked, "Do people in other countries read it too?"

"From the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries onward, missionaries and sinologists brought it to Europe," said the old man.

Later, it was translated into many languages and traveled widely.

## Page 10

"This book uses James Legge's English translation for the Original Text."

"His Chinese name was Li Yage. His complete English version was published in 1891."

"It is early, careful, and in the public domain."

## Page 11

"Translating Dao is hard," said the old man.

"Way sounds like a road. Path also sounds like a road. But Dao is more than a road."

"So some translators simply write Dao and let readers feel their way toward it."

## Page 12

Ahe remembered the elephant.

"Thick-Pillar Monster" was not enough. "Elephant" was better.

But "elephant" is still only a name. The real elephant breathes, walks, and curls grass with its nose.

## Page 13

"Then do we read it to memorize answers?" Ahe asked.

The old man shook his head. "No. We read it to learn how Laozi watched water."

"Do not rush to be first. Do not rush to scold. Look slowly, and think gently."

## Page 14

At sunset, they walked along the raised path between the fields.

"Ahe, you are a little like Yin Xi," said the old man. "You keep asking an old man to speak."

"Your book of names is like the bamboo slips once spread open at the pass."

## Page 15

That night, Ahe wrote one last line:

*Laozi was not the person who finished explaining Dao. He was like someone who opened a door.*

The door stood open. Tomorrow, they would finally enter Chapter One.
