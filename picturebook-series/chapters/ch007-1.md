# 第007-1章：把自己放在后面

## 原文

### 第1页

天长地久。
天地所以能长且久者，以其不自生，故能长生。
是以圣人后其身而身先；
外其身而身存。
非以其无私邪，故能成其私。

## 释义

### 第2页

“天长地久”不是说天和地像人一样想要活得很久。

老子看见的是：天地不把自己摆在万物前面，却让万物在其中生长，所以显得长久。

### 第3页

王弼解释“不自生”时说，如果只为自己生，就会和万物争。

天地不抢万物的路，万物反而都在天地里来去，这就是它们的长久。

### 第4页

“后其身而身先”，不是故意装谦虚，也不是永远躲在最后。

它是说：真正能带路的人，常先把事情和众人放在前面，自己不急着抢位置。

### 第5页

“外其身”也不是不要身体，不爱生命。

它是说：到了该承担的时候，不把“我会不会吃亏”放在中心。无私不是没有自己，而是不让私心遮住大道。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**长久**：很长很长地延续。这里指不靠争抢而能持续。

**不自生**：不只为自己活，不把自己当作所有事情的中心。

**长生**：在这里不是求仙不死，而是因不争而能长久。

### 第7页

**后其身**：把自己放在后面，先顾到事情和别人。

**身先**：反而走到前面，得到大家信任。

**外其身**：不把自己那一点得失看得最大。

**无私**：没有偏私的盘算，所以能成就更深的自己。

## 正文

### 第8页

夏天，空空村要给过路人搭一座凉棚。孩子们搬竹竿，老人们编草帘。

阿禾被推出来当小队长。他心里有点高兴，也有点想站在最前面。

### 第9页

太阳很大，大家都想先喝酸梅汤。阿禾刚伸手，老人把水勺递给他。

老人说：“你来分。”阿禾愣住了：原来当队长，先拿到的不是杯子，而是水勺。

### 第10页

他先给最小的孩子倒，又给搬竹竿的叔叔倒，再给额头冒汗的老人倒。

等轮到自己，桶底只剩一点点。可一个小女孩把半杯推回来，说：“阿禾哥哥也热。”

### 第11页

午后忽然起风，草帘被吹得翻起来。阿禾本想躲到屋檐下，却看见竹绳快松了。

他跑到棚边，踮脚拉住绳子，雨点打在脸上，手心也被磨红。

### 第12页

风停以后，凉棚没有倒。过路人坐在棚下，鞋边滴着雨水，脸上却有了笑。

大家商量怎么加固时，第一个转头看阿禾。不是因为他喊得最大，而是因为他刚才站在需要他的地方。

### 第13页

傍晚，阿禾抬头看天。天空盖住每一间屋子，却没有在云上写自己的名字。

他又低头看地。土地托住每一双脚，也没有问谁先夸它。

## 回扣

### 第14页

第七章说，圣人把自己放后，反而在前；把自己放外，反而能存。

这不是交换称赞的小聪明。若心里想着“我装在后面，别人就会夸我”，那还是私心站在前面。

### 第15页

真正的“后”，是先看见什么需要被照顾。真正的“外”，是让小小的我不挡住大大的事。

阿禾写下：天空不抢位置，所以能盖住许多路；人不抢自己，心里也会变大。

# Chapter 007-1: Putting Yourself Behind

## Original Text

### Page 1

Heaven is long-enduring and earth continues long. The reason why heaven and earth are able to endure and continue thus long is because they do not live of, or for, themselves. This is how they are able to continue and endure.

Therefore the sage puts his own person last, and yet it is found in the foremost place; he treats his person as if it were foreign to him, and yet that person is preserved. Is it not because he has no personal and private ends, that therefore such ends are realised?

## Interpretation

### Page 2

"Heaven is long-enduring" does not mean heaven and earth try to live a long life like people.

Laozi sees this: heaven and earth do not put themselves in front of all things. They let all things grow within them, so they seem to last.

### Page 3

Wang Bi says that if heaven and earth lived only for themselves, they would struggle against things.

Because they do not seize the road from things, all things come and go within them. That is their endurance.

### Page 4

"The sage puts his own person last" is not pretending to be humble, and it is not hiding forever at the back.

It means that a true guide often puts the matter and the people first, without hurrying to grab the front place.

### Page 5

"Treats his person as if it were foreign" does not mean hating the body or throwing life away.

It means that when something must be carried, "Will I lose?" is not placed at the center. Selflessness is not having no self; it is not letting private wants cover the Way.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**long-enduring** — Lasting for a very long time. Here it means lasting without fighting to possess.

**do not live for themselves** — They do not make themselves the center of everything.

**continue and endure** — Keep going and remain.

### Page 7

**puts his own person last** — Puts himself behind the task and the people.

**foremost place** — The front place, where trust gathers.

**foreign to him** — Not treated as the biggest concern.

**private ends** — Selfish aims or hidden personal gain.

## Main Text

### Page 8

In summer, Empty Village built a shade shelter for travelers. The children carried bamboo poles, and the elders wove grass mats.

Ahe was chosen as the small team leader. He felt pleased, and part of him wanted to stand in front.

### Page 9

The sun was hot, and everyone wanted sour plum drink first. Just as Ahe reached for a cup, the old man handed him the ladle.

"You serve," said the old man. Ahe paused. To lead, he had not received the cup first; he had received the ladle.

### Page 10

He poured first for the smallest children, then for the uncles carrying bamboo, then for the elders with sweat on their foreheads.

When his turn came, only a little remained. But a small girl pushed back half her cup and said, "Brother Ahe is hot too."

### Page 11

In the afternoon, wind rose and lifted the grass mats. Ahe wanted to hide under the eaves, but he saw a bamboo rope loosening.

He ran to the edge of the shelter and pulled the rope tight. Rain struck his face, and the rope reddened his palms.

### Page 12

When the wind stopped, the shelter still stood. Travelers sat beneath it with wet shoes, but their faces had softened.

When everyone discussed how to strengthen the shelter, they first looked at Ahe. Not because he had shouted the loudest, but because he had stood where he was needed.

### Page 13

At dusk, Ahe looked up at the sky. It covered every roof, yet wrote no name on the clouds.

Then he looked at the ground. It carried every pair of feet, yet did not ask who praised it first.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Chapter Seven says the sage puts himself behind and is found in front; he sets himself outside and is preserved.

This is not a clever trade for praise. If the heart thinks, "I will act last so people praise me," private desire is still standing in front.

### Page 15

True "behind" means seeing what needs care first. True "outside" means not letting the small self block the larger matter.

Ahe wrote: The sky does not seize a place, so it can cover many roads. When people do not seize themselves, the heart becomes wider.
