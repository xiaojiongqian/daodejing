# 第004-2章：不刺眼的光和看不见的深处

## 典故引入

### 第1页

庄子讲过“空船”的故事。一个人乘船过河，如果一只空船撞来，他多半只会把船推开。

可若船上站着一个人，他就容易生气，觉得对方故意撞他。

### 第2页

同样一下碰撞，为什么心会不一样？因为“我”一站出来，锋芒就跟着站出来。

把心空一点，锐气就少一点。许多纷争，还没长大就散了。

### 第3页

唐代大将郭子仪，平定安史之乱，功劳很大。后来皇帝听了谗言，担心他功高难制，便收走兵权。

郭子仪没有拍桌喊冤。他去修皇陵，又把从前赐给自己的诏书和机密文书交还朝廷。

### 第4页

这等于告诉皇帝：我手里没有要挟朝廷的东西，也没有藏着锋芒不放。

皇帝看见后，心里惭愧。疑结不是被刀砍开，而是被坦荡慢慢解开。

### 第5页

还有一次，仇人暗中挖了郭子仪父亲的坟。满朝都怕他大怒报复，风波会越闹越大。

郭子仪却先反省自己带兵时也管束不周。怒火没有烧成大火，许多人的命也没有被卷进去。

### 第6页

李泌也懂“挫锐”。安史乱中，他看出叛军锐气很盛，却多为抢掠而来，不能久守。

他建议不急着硬攻，而是切断要路，让叛军来回奔走，自己以逸待劳。

### 第7页

他的谋划很细：一路守太原，一路入河东，再故意留出一角，让敌军在范阳和长安之间疲于奔命。

锋芒不是硬砍掉的，而是让它在奔跑中慢慢钝下来。

### 第8页

可皇帝急着收复两京，没有完全听从。眼前胜利很快来了，叛乱的根却没有彻底拔掉。

这也是“纷”的一面：只解眼前的结，深处的线还会再缠回来。

### 第9页

懒残禅师住在山寺里，做粗活，吃剩饭，衣服也不讲究。李泌夜里听见他念诵，知道他不是普通人。

寒夜里，懒残递出半个烤芋头。它不像宝物，却像一盏藏在灰里的灯。

### 第10页

西方画家维米尔，也懂柔光。他不常画战争凯旋或王宫大典，只画窗边倒牛奶的人、读信的人、安静的房间。

光从窗外进来，轻轻落在普通生活上。

### 第11页

《戴珍珠耳环的少女》只是一个女孩回头的一瞬。背景很暗，珍珠很小，神情也不吵闹。

几百年过去，人们仍安静站在画前。柔光，比强光更能留下来。

### 第12页

英国巨石阵站在索尔兹伯里平原上。谁建的？为什么建？许多答案都不能完全说定。

石头不解释，只在风雨里站了很久很久。它不喊“我伟大”，却让人一代代停下脚步。

### 第13页

日出时，光从石缝间穿过。雾气升起来，草地湿着，巨石仍沉默。

这种沉默不是空白。它像一个深井口，让人知道下面还有很深很深的水。

### 第14页

贝多芬后来几乎听不见外面的声音。对音乐家来说，这像一扇重要的门慢慢关上。

可是门关上以后，他没有只盯着门缝哭。更深的旋律，在心里一点点亮起来。

### 第15页

晚年，他写出《第九交响曲》。首演时，掌声像浪，可他背对观众，几乎听不见。

有人轻轻提醒他转身。他才看见人们站起来，知道无声深处的音乐已经抵达许多人。

### 第16页

维米尔的柔光，巨石阵的沉默，贝多芬心里的声音，都不是刺眼的东西。

它们没有大声解释自己，却让人一次又一次回头。像“湛兮似或存”——像有，又像藏着。

## 回扣

### 第17页

空船、郭子仪和李泌，都在讲“挫锐解纷”。不是软弱，而是不把事情推向更锋利的地方。

有时，最深的谋不是赢一场仗，而是不让乱线越缠越紧。

### 第18页

懒残、维米尔、巨石阵和贝多芬，都在讲“和其光，同其尘”。光柔下来，反而更久。

真正珍贵的东西，不一定站在高台上；它可能在灰里的芋头、窗边的光、石缝的日出、心里的乐声中。

### 第19页

第四章不是叫人躲开世界。它教人带着柔光走进世界，带着清水走进尘土。

挫锐，不是折断自己；解纷，不是逃走。是把太尖的地方磨圆，把太紧的地方松开。

### 第20页

如果心像空船，别人撞来时，波会过去。如果人像深井，来打水的人多了，井也不喊累。

如果光不刺眼，尘土也能被照亮。道就在这些安静的小地方，像有，又像没有。

# Chapter 004-2: Gentle Light and the Invisible Depth

## Allusion

### Page 1

Zhuangzi tells the story of an empty boat. If someone crossing a river is bumped by an empty boat, he will probably just push it away.

But if a person stands inside that boat, anger rises. He thinks, "You hit me on purpose."

### Page 2

The bump is the same, so why does the heart change? Because once "me" stands up, sharpness stands up with it.

Make the heart a little emptier, and the edge grows softer. Many quarrels fade before they become large.

### Page 3

In Tang China, the great general Guo Ziyi helped end the An Lushan Rebellion. Later, slander made the emperor fear that Guo's merit had grown too large.

Guo did not slam the table. He repaired the imperial tomb, then returned old imperial letters and secret documents to the court.

### Page 4

It was as if he said: I hold nothing to threaten the court, and I am not hiding a blade in my sleeve.

When the emperor saw this, shame softened his suspicion. The knot was not cut by a knife; it was slowly loosened by openness.

### Page 5

Another time, an enemy secretly dug up Guo Ziyi's father's grave. The court feared that Guo would rage and take revenge.

Guo first reflected on how soldiers under him had once harmed others' graves. His anger did not become a spreading fire, and many lives were spared.

### Page 6

Li Bi also understood "blunting sharpness." During the rebellion, he saw that the rebels were fierce but driven by plunder, not patient rule.

He advised the court not to rush into a hard clash, but to cut key roads and make the rebels run back and forth while loyal forces waited steadily.

### Page 7

His plan was detailed: one force would hold Taiyuan, another enter Hedong, and one opening would be left so the enemy would exhaust itself between Fanyang and Chang'an.

Sharpness would not be chopped off. It would be worn down by its own running.

### Page 8

The emperor wanted the two capitals back quickly and did not fully follow this plan. A quick victory came, but the root of the rebellion remained.

That is another face of complication: if only the surface knot is untied, the deeper thread may tangle again.

### Page 9

Lazy Can, a Chan monk, lived in a mountain temple. He did rough work, ate leftovers, and cared little for fine clothing. At night, Li Bi heard his clear chanting and knew he was no ordinary man.

On a cold night, Lazy Can handed him half a roasted taro. It looked nothing like treasure, but it was like a lamp hidden in ash.

### Page 10

The Dutch painter Vermeer also understood gentle light. He did not often paint battles or royal ceremonies; he painted people pouring milk, reading letters, and sitting in quiet rooms.

Light came through windows and softly touched ordinary life.

### Page 11

*Girl with a Pearl Earring* is only a young girl turning her head. The background is dark, the pearl is small, and nothing shouts.

After centuries, people still stand quietly before her. Gentle light can remain longer than glaring light.

### Page 12

Stonehenge stands on Salisbury Plain in England. Who built it? Why was it built? No answer settles everything.

The stones do not explain. They stand through wind and rain for a very long time. They do not announce greatness, yet people keep stopping before them.

### Page 13

At sunrise, light passes between the stones. Mist rises, grass is wet, and the stones are silent.

This silence is not blankness. It is like the mouth of a deep well, telling us there is water far below.

### Page 14

Beethoven later could barely hear the outer world. For a musician, this was like an important door slowly closing.

But after that door closed, he did not only stare at the crack and weep. A deeper music kept glowing inside.

### Page 15

In his later years, he wrote the Ninth Symphony. At its first performance, applause rose like waves, but he stood with his back to the audience and could barely hear.

Someone gently turned him around. Then he saw people standing, and knew music from the depth of silence had reached many hearts.

### Page 16

Vermeer's soft light, Stonehenge's silence, and Beethoven's inner music are not dazzling things.

They do not loudly explain themselves, yet people return to them again and again. Like "pure and still, as if present": there, and hidden.

## Return to the Original

### Page 17

The empty boat, Guo Ziyi, and Li Bi all teach "blunting sharpness and unraveling complications." This is not weakness; it is refusing to push matters toward a sharper edge.

Sometimes the deepest strategy is not winning a battle. It is keeping the tangled thread from tightening.

### Page 18

Lazy Can, Vermeer, Stonehenge, and Beethoven all teach "tempering brightness and joining the dust." Softer light often lasts longer.

What is precious may not stand on a high platform. It may be in a taro in ashes, window light, sunrise through stones, or music held in the heart.

### Page 19

Chapter Four does not tell us to flee the world. It teaches us to enter the world with softer light and clear water.

To blunt sharpness is not to break oneself. To unravel complications is not to run away. It is to round what is too sharp and loosen what is too tight.

### Page 20

If the heart is like an empty boat, waves pass after the bump. If a person is like a deep well, many may draw water without exhausting it.

If light does not sting, even dust can glow. Dao is in these quiet places, as if present and as if hidden.
