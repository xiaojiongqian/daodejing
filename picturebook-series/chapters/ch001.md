# 第001章：名字不是全部

## 原文

### 第1页

道可道，非常道；名可名，非常名。

无名，天地之始；有名，万物之母。

故常无欲，以观其妙；常有欲，以观其徼。

此两者，同出而异名，同谓之玄。

玄之又玄，众妙之门。

## 释义

### 第2页

老子一开口就提醒我们：能被说尽的“道”，不是永远的道。能被一个名字装住的东西，也不是它真正的全部。

字很有用，像门牌。可是门牌不是家，名字也不是生命本身。

### 第3页

在万物还没有名字以前，那个看不见的源头已经在。后来山、水、鸟、树都有了名字，我们才好分辨和照料它们。

所以“无名”像开始，“有名”像养育。二者不是敌人，而是从同一个深处出来。

### 第4页

“无欲”不是不要生活，而是先放下“我全知道了”的心。心空一点，就能看见深处的奇妙。

“有欲”也不是全错。它让我们看见事物的边界和样子，只是不要把边界当成全部。

### 第5页

老子把这个深处叫作“玄”。玄不是黑暗，而是深得说不完。

越往里看，越发现还有门。每一扇门后面，都有新的奇妙。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**道（dào）**：本来是路。在这里，它像万物背后看不见的方向，让花会开，水会流，孩子会长大。

**常（cháng）**：一直如此、不被一两句话改掉。能被说完的，就不是“常道”。

### 第7页

**名（míng）**：名字和标签。它帮我们叫出事物，却装不下事物的全部。

**无（wú）**：不是空荡荡，而是还没有形状和名字的深处。

**有（yǒu）**：已经显出样子、可以被看见和分辨。

### 第8页

**欲（yù）**：带着自己的想法去看。想法太满，就容易只看见一小边。

**妙（miào）**：藏在深处的奇妙。

**徼（jiào）**：边界、外面能看见的一圈。

**玄（xuán）**：深远难尽，越看越深。

## 正文

### 第9页

有个古老故事说，几个人第一次摸到一头大象。每个人只摸到一点点，却都急着给它取名字。

阿禾听完这个故事，心想：如果是我，一定能看清楚。

### 第10页

第二天清晨，空空村真的来了一头大象。它从雾里慢慢走出，灰色的身子像一座小山。

阿禾只看见四条腿，立刻写下：“粗柱子怪。”

### 第11页

小兔绕到后面，看见尾巴甩来甩去。他喊：“不对，是鞭子怪！”

小鸟飞到前面，看见长鼻子一卷一卷。她叫：“明明是灰蛇怪！”

### 第12页

小石头站在侧面，看见两片大耳朵扇风。他认真地说：“你们都错了，是大扇子怪。”

四个名字撞在一起，谁也不肯让。大象只是安静地吃草。

### 第13页

老人来了，没有急着评判。他说：“别守着自己那一小块，再绕一圈看看。”

他们从头看到尾，又从左看到右。每个人都看见了别人说的那一部分。

### 第14页

老人说：“它叫大象。”阿禾轻轻念了一遍，觉得这个名字很好。

可是大象抬起鼻子，喷出一口温热的气。阿禾忽然知道：这个名字只是指向它，并没有装下它。

### 第15页

“粗柱子”没有全错，那是腿。“灰蛇”也没有全错，那是鼻子。

可是如果只抓住一个名字，就会错过整头大象。名字像小灯，照见一点，也留下许多黑夜。

## 回扣

### 第16页

回看第一章，老子不是叫我们不要说话、不要起名。他是在说：说出来的，只是通向道的一条小路。

路能带我们走近。可真正的道，比路牌更深、更远。

### 第17页

“无名”时，我们想起万物还没有分开的源头。“有名”时，我们照顾眼前的山水草木。

这两面同出一处，所以老子说它们都很“玄”。深处还连着更深处。

### 第18页

夜里，阿禾在本子上写：名字不是没用，名字也不是全部。

我会认真叫出一朵花、一条河、一个朋友。然后，我还要安静地看一会儿，看见名字外面的光。

# Chapter 001: Names Are Not Everything

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Dao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Dao.
The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name.

(Conceived of as) having no name, it is the Originator of heaven and earth;
(conceived of as) having a name, it is the Mother of all things.

Always without desire we must be found,
If its deep mystery we would sound;
But if desire always within us be,
Its outer fringe is all that we shall see.

Under these two aspects, it is really the same; but as development takes place, it receives the different names.
Together we call them the Mystery.
Where the Mystery is the deepest is the gate of all that is subtle and wonderful.

> — Translated by James Legge (1891), *The Texts of Taoism*, Sacred Books of the East, Vol. 39. Public domain.

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Laozi begins with a warning: the Dao that can be fully spoken is not the enduring Dao. A thing that can be trapped inside one name is not the whole thing.

Words are useful, like signs on doors. But the sign is not the home, and the name is not the living being.

### Page 3

Before anything had a name, the hidden source was already there. Later, mountains, rivers, birds, and trees received names, so we could recognize and care for them.

So "having no name" is like the beginning, and "having a name" is like nurturing. They are not enemies; they rise from the same depth.

### Page 4

"Without desire" does not mean refusing life. It means setting down the thought, "I already know everything."

With a quieter heart, we can glimpse the deep mystery. With fixed wants, we may see only the outer edge.

### Page 5

Laozi calls this depth "the Mystery." It is not darkness; it is depth beyond easy speech.

The farther we look, the more doors we find. Behind each door, there is another quiet wonder.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**Dao** — Originally, a road or way. Here it is like the unseen direction behind things: flowers opening, water flowing, children growing.

**enduring and unchanging** — Legge's phrase for 常. It means always so. If a sentence can finish it, it is not the enduring Dao.

### Page 7

**name** — A label that helps us call something. Helpful, but never the whole living thing.

**having no name** — The deep source before clear shapes and labels.

**having a name** — The world after things take shape, so we can notice and care for them.

### Page 8

**desire** — Looking with your own fixed idea already in front of your eyes.

**deep mystery** — The hidden wonder inside things.

**outer fringe** — The edge or surface we can see from outside.

**the Mystery** — Legge's word for 玄: deep, far, and never used up.

## Main Text

### Page 9

An old story says that several people once touched an elephant for the first time. Each touched only one part, then hurried to name the whole creature.

When Ahe heard the story, he thought, If it were me, I would see clearly.

### Page 10

The next morning, an elephant really came to Kongkong Village. It walked out of the mist, gray and huge as a small hill.

Ahe saw only its four legs. He quickly wrote, "Thick-Pillar Monster."

### Page 11

Little Rabbit circled behind it and saw the tail swishing. "Wrong!" he cried. "It is a Whip Monster!"

Little Bird flew to the front and saw the long trunk curling. "No, it is a Gray-Snake Monster!"

### Page 12

Little Stone stood at the side and watched the wide ears flap. "You are all wrong," he said. "It is a Big-Fan Monster."

Four names bumped into one another, and nobody gave way. The elephant only stood there, quietly eating grass.

### Page 13

The old man came, but he did not hurry to judge. "Do not guard only your small piece," he said. "Walk around and look again."

They looked from head to tail, then from left to right. Each child saw the part another child had named.

### Page 14

"It is called an elephant," said the old man. Ahe whispered the word and liked it.

Then the elephant lifted its trunk and breathed warm air into the morning. Ahe suddenly knew: the name pointed to it, but did not hold it.

### Page 15

"Thick pillar" was not entirely wrong; it was a leg. "Gray snake" was not entirely wrong; it was a trunk.

But if you grip one name too tightly, you miss the whole elephant. A name is a small lamp: it lights one place and leaves much in the night.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

Looking back at Chapter One, Laozi is not telling us to stop speaking or naming. He is saying that what we say is only a small path toward Dao.

A path can help us come closer. But the true Dao is deeper and farther than the sign beside the road.

### Page 17

With "having no name," we remember the source before things were divided. With "having a name," we care for the mountains, rivers, grasses, and trees before us.

These two sides rise from one place. That is why Laozi calls them Mystery: depth opening into deeper depth.

### Page 18

That night, Ahe wrote in his notebook: Names are not useless, and names are not everything.

I will carefully call a flower, a river, and a friend by name. Then I will stay quiet a little longer, and look for the light beyond the name.
