# 第002-1章：一对一对出现

## 原文

### 第1页

天下皆知美之为美，斯恶已；皆知善之为善，斯不善已。

故有无相生，难易相成，长短相形，高下相倾，音声相和，前后相随。

### 第2页

是以圣人处无为之事，行不言之教。

万物作焉而不辞，生而不有，为而不恃，功成而弗居。夫唯弗居，是以不去。

## 释义

### 第3页

当大家都说“只有这样才美”，别的样子就被推到“不美”那边。当大家都说“只有这样才善”，别的做法也会被推到“不善”那边。

老子不是反对美和善。他提醒我们：太爱比较，心里就容易起争抢。

### 第4页

许多事一对一对显出来。有和无、难和易、长和短、高和低，都要互相照见。

像碗一样，碗壁是“有”，中间的空处是“无”。没有那个空，碗就装不了汤。

### 第5页

所以懂道的人做事，不是懒着不动。他不硬推、不乱抢，让事情按自己的节奏长。

事成之后，他也不把功劳紧紧抱住。正因为不占着，那份好反而留得久。

## 难词注解

### 第6页

**美（měi）**：让人喜欢、觉得好看的样子。

**恶（è）**：这里多指“不美”，不是骂谁坏。

**善（shàn）**：好、合宜、做得对。

**不善（bù shàn）**：被比较出来的“不够好”，不一定真的没有价值。

### 第7页

**有无相生**：有和无互相显出对方。房间有墙，也要有空处，人才能住进去。

**长短相形**：长和短放在一起才看得清。

**高下相倾**：高处和低处互相依着，才有高低。

### 第8页

**音声相和**：一个声音应着另一个声音，才成音乐。

**无为**：不是不做，而是不凭私心乱做。

**不言之教**：少用嘴压人，多用行动给人看。

**弗居**：不把功劳占住不放。

## 正文

### 第9页

阿禾给小屋做书架，拿起两根竹条。他说：“这一根长，那一根短。”

老人问：“如果只有一根，你还知道它长还是短吗？”阿禾愣住了。

### 第10页

他们又看一只碗。阿禾摸摸硬硬的碗壁，说：“这里是有。”

老人指着中间：“这里是空。饭和汤，正是住在这个空里。”

### 第11页

小石头敲了一下木鼓，咚。小鸟吹了一声竹笛，呜。

单独听，只是一声。两声一前一后接上，孩子们忽然听见了小小的曲子。

### 第12页

阿禾急着告诉大家：“我懂了，我来教你们！”老人没有说他错，只把鼓轻轻放回桌上。

大家看见老人先听，再敲；先等，再应。没有一句大道理，孩子们却慢慢安静下来。

### 第13页

院角有几株豆苗，风一吹就摇。阿禾想扶直它们，手伸到一半又停住。

他只把旁边的石头挪开，让阳光照进来。豆苗自己慢慢站稳了。

## 回扣

### 第14页

回看这一章，老子把世界讲成一串相依的对子。美和不美、有和无、难和易、长和短，都不是孤零零站着。

看见一边时，也要记得另一边。这样心就不容易被比较牵着走。

### 第15页

“无为”也在这里：不硬做，不乱做，不把自己摆在事情前面。该帮时帮，该等时等。

阿禾写下：短让长显出来，空让碗能盛汤。做好了，就让好自己留下。

# Chapter 002-1: Things Appear in Pairs

## Original Text

### Page 1

All in the world know the beauty of the beautiful, and in doing this they have (the idea of) what ugliness is; they all know the skill of the skilful, and in doing this they have (the idea of) what the want of skill is.

So it is that existence and non-existence give birth the one to (the idea of) the other; that difficulty and ease produce the one (the idea of) the other; that length and shortness fashion out the one the figure of the other; that (the ideas of) height and lowness arise from the contrast of the one with the other; that the musical notes and tones become harmonious through the relation of one with another; and that being before and behind give the idea of one following another.

### Page 2

Therefore the sage manages affairs without doing anything, and conveys his instructions without the use of speech.

All things spring up, and there is not one which declines to show itself; they grow, and there is no claim made for their ownership; they go through their processes, and there is no expectation (of a reward for the results). The work is accomplished, and there is no resting in it (as an achievement).

The work is done, but how no one can see;
'Tis this that makes the power not cease to be.

> — Translated by James Legge (1891), *The Texts of Taoism*, Sacred Books of the East, Vol. 39. Public domain.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

When everyone says, "Only this is beautiful," other forms get pushed to the "not beautiful" side. When everyone says, "Only this is good," other ways get pushed aside too.

Laozi is not against beauty or goodness. He warns us that too much comparing easily turns into competition.

### Page 4

Many things appear in pairs. Being and nonbeing, hard and easy, long and short, high and low all reveal one another.

A bowl shows this well: the wall is "being," and the hollow is "nonbeing." Without the hollow, the bowl cannot hold soup.

### Page 5

So a person who understands Dao is not lazy or blank. He does not push, grab, or meddle; he lets things grow in their own rhythm.

When the work is done, he does not clutch the credit. Because he does not cling, the good can last.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 6

**beauty** — A form people enjoy or find lovely.

**ugliness** — Here, the "not beautiful" side created by comparison. It is not a word for shaming someone.

**skilful** — Able, good at doing something.

**want of skill** — The "not skilled enough" side that appears once a standard is set.

### Page 7

**existence and non-existence** — Being and nonbeing showing one another. A room has walls, but people live in the open space.

**length and shortness** — Long and short become clear only side by side.

**height and lowness** — High and low lean on each other.

### Page 8

**musical notes and tones become harmonious** — One sound answers another, and music appears.

**without doing anything** — Not doing nothing; not forcing from selfish will.

**without the use of speech** — Teaching more by example than by pressure.

**no resting in it** — Not sitting on your achievement as if it belonged only to you.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Ahe was building a shelf for his little house. He held up two bamboo strips and said, "This one is long, and that one is short."

The old man asked, "If there were only one strip, would you still know whether it was long or short?" Ahe went quiet.

### Page 10

Then they looked at a bowl. Ahe touched the hard clay wall and said, "This part is being."

The old man pointed to the middle. "This part is empty. Rice and soup live exactly in that emptiness."

### Page 11

Little Stone tapped a wooden drum: dong. Little Bird blew a bamboo pipe: woo.

Alone, each was only one sound. One after the other, the children suddenly heard a tiny song.

### Page 12

Ahe rushed to say, "I understand! Let me teach everyone!" The old man did not scold him; he simply placed the drum back on the table.

Everyone watched him listen first, then tap; wait first, then answer. Without a lecture, the children slowly grew quiet.

### Page 13

In the corner of the yard, bean sprouts shook in the wind. Ahe wanted to hold them straight, but his hand stopped halfway.

He only moved a stone aside so sunlight could reach them. The sprouts slowly stood steady by themselves.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

Looking back, Chapter Two shows the world as pairs that depend on one another. Beauty and not-beauty, being and nonbeing, hard and easy, long and short do not stand alone.

When we see one side, we should remember the other. Then comparison will not pull our heart around so easily.

### Page 15

"Without doing anything" is here too: do not force, do not meddle, do not put yourself in front of the matter. Help when help is needed; wait when waiting is needed.

Ahe wrote: Short reveals long. Emptiness lets the bowl hold soup. When good work is finished, let the goodness remain by itself.
