# 第002-1章：养身与无为

## 原文

### 第1页

天下皆知美之为美，斯恶已；皆知善之为善，斯不善已。

### 第2页

故有无相生，难易相成，长短相形，高下相倾，音声相和，前后相随。

### 第3页

是以圣人处无为之事，行不言之教，万物作焉而不辞，生而不有，为而不恃，功成而弗居。

夫惟弗居，是以不去。

## 释义

### 第4页

老子指出：当“美”和“善”被当作固定标准时，它们的反面便随之而生。美与丑、善与不善不是独立的实体，而是因为命名和对照而出现。

### 第5页

因此，有与无、难与易、长与短、高与下、音与声、前与后这些对立，实际上是互相生成的关系。它们通过相互比较而显现，而不是独立存在。

### 第6页

圣人以无为应对事务，以不言教化众生。他不将自己的私欲强加于事物，而是顺势而为；成事后不据为己，正因为不据为己，功德才得以长久。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第7页

**无为**：不是无所作为，而是在行动时避免以私欲干涉，让事势自然流动。

**不言之教**：通过行为、安排和制度来引导，而不是依靠口头命令。

**弗居**：不把成果当成自己的标签，成事后不居功，从而让成就继续留在世界。

## 正文

### 第8页

知美为不美

春秋时期的越国，苎萝山下若耶溪畔有一位女子，因心口常有隐疾，病发时总以双手捧心，眉头微蹙。她的这一瞬间，虽由痛苦而起，却在自然之中生出一种楚楚动人的凄美。邻里见了，不禁怜惜，称她为“颦”。

### 第9页

溪的东边住着另一位女子，她容貌平平，见西施因此受人瞩目，心中起了仿效之念。于是她特意学着捧心蹙眉，沿街走动，期待借此赢得别人的同情。

### 第10页

她的动作却显得做作。富人见她闭门不出，穷人见她避避缩缩。西施之美在于无心，而东施之丑在于有意。当“美”被抽离为一种可复制的姿势时，它便失去了原本的生命力。

### 第11页

这正是老子所说的“天下皆知美之为美，斯恶已”。当他人把一次自然的流露定格为“美”，并将这种形式奉为标准时，原本的真实被夺走。美不在于皱眉的动作，而在于当下无心的存在。

### 第12页

为善成恶

《庄子·应帝王》中记载：远古有帝王名为混沌，他没有七窍，不通视听食息，却以浑然一体的方式待人亲切。南海之帝倏与北海之帝忽受其厚待，内心不安。

### 第13页

他们商议要报答恩情，便每日手持利凿，为混沌开凿眼、耳、口、鼻。到了七窍齐备时，混沌那种完整的、未被切割的生命却永远消失了。

### 第14页

倏忽并非恶人，而是好意过重。他们以“有七窍才是完整”为前提，强行将混沌那种独特的圆融切割成具体功能。这样的“修补”，反而成了毁灭。

### 第15页

混沌之死，死于被定义和被分割。它提醒我们：当我们执着于“美”“善”等名相时，就会在名相之外制造出对立。养身与处世，皆应保留差异的流动空间，而不是用固定标准去钉住生命。

## 回扣

### 第16页

回到第二章，老子并非否认美或善，而是在提醒我们：一旦用固定的名相去判断，原本流动的存在就会被分裂，对立便随之发生。

### 第17页

真正的无为，是在对立中保持不执着，让万事自然发生；成事之后不据为己，这样功德才会如水般留在世界，而不是停留在自我的名字里。

# Chapter 002-1: Nurturing Life and Non-Action

## Original Text

### Page 1

All under heaven know beauty as beauty; thereby ugliness exists. All know good as good; thereby not-good exists.

### Page 2

Thus being and non-being produce each other; difficult and easy complete each other; long and short shape each other; high and low depend on each other; sound and tone harmonize; before and after follow one another.

### Page 3

Therefore the Sage manages affairs without action; he teaches without words. Things arise and he does not reject them; he produces but does not possess; he acts but does not rely on the result; when his work is done he does not take credit.

Because he does not take credit, it does not leave him.

## Interpretation

### Page 4

Laozi says that when people fix beauty and goodness as permanent standards, ugliness and not-goodness are born as their counterparts.

### Page 5

Being and non-being, difficult and easy, long and short, high and low are not isolated entities. They take shape through their relations to one another.

### Page 6

The Sage handles affairs with wu wei and teaches without words. He avoids imposing private desire, allows conditions to unfold, and because he does not take credit, his achievements endure.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 7

**Wu wei**: Not doing nothing, but acting without intrusive self-interest, allowing situations to unfold naturally.

**Teaching without words**: Guiding through example and the arrangement of conditions rather than through verbal command.

**Not taking credit**: Refusing to make one’s accomplishment an identity, so the result stays with the world.

## Main Text

### Page 8

Knowing beauty to be beauty makes it not beauty.

In the state of Yue, beneath Zhuluo Mountain on the banks of the Ruoye Stream, a woman suffered a hidden illness in her chest. When the pain flared, she would hold her hand to her heart and furrow her brow. That fragile moment did not diminish her grace; it gave her a mournful beauty. Neighbors called her “Pín” and felt pity.

### Page 9

Across the stream lived another woman of ordinary looks. Seeing Xi Shi admired for this gesture, she decided to copy it. She held her chest and furrowed her brow in hope of winning the same compassion.

### Page 10

Her gesture, however, came across as contrived. The wealthy shut their doors; the poor hurried away. Xi Shi’s beauty came from unselfconsciousness, while Dong Shi’s ugliness came from intention. When beauty is reduced to a repeatable form, the living reality has already been drained from it.

### Page 11

This is what Laozi means by “All under heaven know beauty as beauty; thereby ugliness exists.” When observers fix one natural moment as “beauty” and turn that form into a model, the original reality is lost. Beauty lies not in the furrowed brow itself, but in the unselfconscious moment.

### Page 12

Good intent can also become harm.

In Zhuangzi’s “Responding to the Emperor,” there is a ruler named Hundu. He had no seven orifices, no sight, hearing, mouth, or nose, yet he treated others kindly as a single, undivided being. The emperors Shu of the South and Hu of the North were grateful but uneasy.

### Page 13

They decided to repay him and each day took chisel and hammer to carve his seven orifices. When the holes were complete, Hundu’s great undivided life was gone forever.

### Page 14

Shu and Hu were not evil. They acted from care, but they carried a cognitive tyranny. They assumed “having seven orifices is the normal way of being,” and so they sliced apart Hundu’s unique wholeness into functional parts. Their “goodness” became destruction.

### Page 15

Hundu dies because he is defined and divided. This story shows that when we cling to labels like beauty and goodness, we produce their opposites. Nurturing life and governance require leaving room for difference to move, not pinning life down with fixed standards.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

In Chapter Two, Laozi does not deny beauty or goodness. He warns that once we judge them with fixed names, living things are split apart and opposites are created.

### Page 17

True wu wei holds a non-attached attitude amid oppositions. Let things arise naturally; do not seize the result. Then the result remains with the world, not with your name.
