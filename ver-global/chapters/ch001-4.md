# 第001-4章：名字把什么带进秩序

## 原文

### 第1页
道可道，非常道；名可名，非常名。

无名天地之始，有名万物之母。

——《道德经》第一章

### 第2页
耶和华神用土所造成的野地各样走兽和空中各样飞鸟都带到那人面前，看他叫什么。那人怎样叫各样的活物，那就是它的名字。那人便给一切牲畜和空中飞鸟、野地走兽都起了名。

《创世记》第2章第19-20节


## 释义

### 第3页
“名”不只是贴在事物表面的标签。叫出一个名字，我们便把某物从周围分出来，使它可以被记住、转述和安排。《创世记》写亚当为动物命名，所呈现的正是人以语言整理世界的一刻。

《道德经》并不否认这种作用。它提醒我们：任何名称都只截取了事物的一部分；可以说出的“道”，也已经进入一种特定说法，不能冒充那不断生成的全部。

### 第4页
埃及神话讲述伊西斯设法得知太阳神拉的隐秘名字，并由此取得力量。这个故事不必被当作历史制度；它用神话的方式表达一种直觉：知道如何称呼某物，往往也意味着获得接近它、召唤它甚至支配它的办法。

日本“言灵”观念则把话语本身视为能够影响现实的力量。两种传统并不相同，却都不把名字看成无害的声音。

### 第5页
现代命名少了神话色彩，却更深入日常。病症有了名称，医生才便于讨论治疗；一种社会现象有了称呼，公众才容易辨认它。与此同时，名字也会预先安排态度：“病人”“异议者”“难民”或“入侵者”，都不只是中性的声音。

“名可名，非常名”要求我们在使用名称时，继续追问是谁命名、依据什么、遗漏了什么。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第6页
- 真名（Ren）：古埃及神话中的重要观念；在伊西斯与拉的故事里，获知隐秘之名与获得力量相连。
- 言灵：日本文化中关于言语具有作用力的观念，强调说出的话并非没有后果。
- 命名：把经验划分为可辨认、可交流的对象；每次划分都会突出一些特征，也省略另一些特征。
- 无名：《道德经》用来指称天地万物尚未被区分、规定的根源状态。

### 第7页
- 《创世记》2:19-20：亚当给动物命名，表现人以语言辨认和安排受造世界。
- 《道德经》第一章：“无名”与“有名”同出，既说明命名带来万物的分别，也限制任何名称成为终极答案。
- 伊西斯与拉：以“隐秘之名”表现知识、接近与权力之间的关系。
- 跨文化比较：相似的母题可以彼此照亮，但不能据此断定不同传统对“名字”有完全相同的理解。

## 正文

### 第8页
动物被带到亚当面前，他逐一叫出名字。经文没有细写命名的方法，却清楚展示了结果：原本混在“各种活物”中的生命，从此可以一个个被指认。

命名在这里是一种整理，也带着人的位置。谁有权说“这是什么”，谁便较容易决定它应当放在哪里、如何被谈论。

### 第9页
伊西斯与拉的故事把这种关系推得更远：普通称号还不够，她需要拉隐藏的名字。神话让“知道”与“能够”紧密相连；秘密一旦被说出，力量关系也随之改变。

这不是老子思想的注脚。它提供的是另一种观察：有些文化叙事特别警觉名字落到谁手中。

### 第10页
日本“言灵”观念关注的重点略有不同：语言不只是描述已经发生的事，说话本身也可能带来后果。祝愿、诅咒、郑重的誓言之所以不能随口说过，正因为话被看作一种行动。

这使我们回头检查日常语言：一个称呼反复落在某人身上，久而久之，旁人会照着它期待，他本人也可能开始照着它生活。

### 第11页
现代科学和公共制度每天都在命名。元素、病症、思想体系、社会运动有了共同称呼，资料才能归档，争论才有对象，资源也能被调动。

但名称从来由具体的人和机构提出。谁可以命名，谁有机会修改名称，谁只能接受别人给的称呼，这些问题直接触及权力。

### 第12页
无论分类表多么精密，总有人或事落在格子之间。一个诊断说明症状的一部分，却说不完病人的恐惧、工作和家庭；一个国籍便于法律识别，却说不完一个人的来路。

“无名”不是分类表之外另藏着一件神秘物体。它首先提醒我们，现实总比现成栏目多。

### 第13页
名字的价值也正在这里显出：因为它有限，所以可以操作。医生不必先解释一个人的全部生命，才为眼前的感染用药；法律也需要明确称谓，才能规定责任。

危险发生在工具越过用途的时候。诊断若变成对整个人的判决，分类便不再帮助理解，反而替代了理解。

### 第14页
《创世记》中的上帝与《道德经》中的道，不应因为都涉及语言的限度便被说成同一种存在。前者在经文中创造并命令，后者不是一位发号施令的人格神。

它们在这里形成的只是一个有限的对照：语言赋予人秩序能力，而人的语言又不能穷尽秩序的根源。

### 第15页
所以，面对一个名字，先不必急着赞美或拆毁它。可以问几个具体问题：它帮助谁看清了什么？它遮住了什么？被命名的人能否开口修正？

这样的追问不会使语言失效，只会让语言少一点武断。

## 回扣

### 第16页
《创世记》把命名写成人进入受造世界秩序的行动；《道德经》则在开篇为这种行动划出限度。两者并非同一答案，却在同一个难题旁相遇：人必须借语言生活，也必须提防语言自称为全部。

“名可名，非常名”不是取消名字，而是取消名字的最后裁决权。

### 第17页
我们仍要给疾病命名，给道路立标，给不公正的处境找到可以共同说出的词。

只是每次说完，都给眼前的人或事留下申辩和变化的可能。名字负责让交流开始，不负责宣布交流已经结束。

# Chapter 001-4: What a Name Brings into Order

## Original Text

### Page 1
The Dao that can be spoken of is not the eternal Dao; the name that can be named is not the eternal name.

The nameless is the beginning of heaven and earth; the named is the mother of ten thousand things.

— Dao De Jing, Chapter 1

### Page 2
The LORD God formed out of the ground every beast of the field and every bird of the air, and brought them to the man to see what he would call them. Whatever the man called each living creature, that was its name. The man gave names to all cattle, to the birds of the air, and to every beast of the field.

Genesis 2:19-20

## Interpretation

### Page 3
A name is more than a label placed on the surface of a thing. To name is to separate something from its surroundings so that it can be remembered, discussed, and arranged. Genesis shows Adam naming the animals: a scene of human beings ordering the world through language.

The Dao De Jing does not deny that usefulness. It warns that any name selects only part of what it names. A Dao put into words has entered one particular account and cannot stand in for the whole process still unfolding.

### Page 4
Egyptian myth tells how Isis learned the hidden name of the sun god Ra and thereby gained power. This is not a record of social practice. It is a mythical expression of an intuition: knowing how to call something can grant access to it, summon it, and sometimes make it easier to control.

The Japanese idea of kotodama treats speech itself as capable of affecting reality. The traditions differ, but neither regards a name as an innocent sound.

### Page 5
Modern naming is less mythic but more deeply woven into ordinary life. A condition receives a name, and physicians can discuss treatment; a social experience receives a term, and the public can recognize it. Yet names also arrange attitudes in advance: patient, dissenter, refugee, and invader are not neutral sounds.

“The name that can be named is not the enduring name” asks who named, on what evidence, and what the name leaves out.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 6
- True Name (Ren): An important idea in Egyptian myth; in the story of Isis and Ra, learning the hidden name is linked to acquiring power.
- Kotodama: A Japanese idea that words have efficacy and that speech is not without consequences.
- Naming: Dividing experience into objects that can be recognized and discussed. Every division emphasizes some features and omits others.
- Nameless: In the Dao De Jing, the source condition before heaven and earth are divided and specified.

### Page 7
- Genesis 2:19-20: Adam names the animals, presenting human language as a way of recognizing and arranging the created world.
- Dao De Jing Chapter 1: Places the named beside the nameless, showing both the distinctions naming creates and the limit of any name as a final answer.
- Isis and Ra: A mythic account linking a hidden name with knowledge, access, and power.
- Cross-cultural comparison: Similar motifs can illuminate one another, but do not prove that different traditions understand names in exactly the same way.

## Main Text

### Page 8
The animals are brought before Adam, and he calls them one by one. The passage does not explain his method, but its result is clear: lives first gathered under the phrase “living creatures” can now be identified separately.

Naming is an act of arrangement, and it establishes the speaker’s position. Whoever is authorized to say what something is will often have greater influence over where it belongs and how it is discussed.

### Page 9
The story of Isis and Ra pushes the relation further. An ordinary title is not enough; Isis needs the name Ra conceals. The myth ties knowing closely to being able. Once the secret is spoken, the balance of power changes.

This is not a gloss on Laozi. It offers another observation: some cultural narratives are especially alert to whose hands receive a name.

### Page 10
The Japanese idea of kotodama places the emphasis elsewhere. Language does not merely report what has happened; speaking can itself have consequences. A blessing, a curse, or a solemn vow cannot be treated as empty noise because speech is understood as an act.

That invites us to inspect ordinary language. When a label is applied to a person again and again, others begin to expect the label’s behavior, and the person may begin to live inside it.

### Page 11
Science and public institutions name things every day. Once elements, illnesses, schools of thought, and social movements share a term, records can be organized, disputes can find an object, and resources can be directed.

But names are proposed by particular people and institutions. Who may name, who may revise a name, and who must simply accept a term imposed by others are questions of power.

### Page 12
However detailed the classification, someone or something will fall between its boxes. A diagnosis describes part of a condition, but not a patient’s fear, work, and family. A nationality enables legal recognition, but does not finish the story of a life.

The nameless is not another mysterious object hidden beyond the table. It first reminds us that reality exceeds the available columns.

### Page 13
The limit of a name is also what makes it useful. A physician need not explain an entire life before treating an infection; a law needs defined terms before it can assign responsibility.

Danger begins when the tool exceeds its use. If a diagnosis becomes a verdict on the whole person, classification no longer assists understanding. It replaces it.

### Page 14
The God of Genesis and the Dao of the Dao De Jing should not be treated as the same reality merely because both raise questions about the limits of language. The former creates and commands in the biblical account; the latter is not a personal deity issuing orders.

Their meeting here is limited but useful: language grants human beings the ability to order, while human language cannot exhaust the source of order.

### Page 15
So we need not rush either to celebrate a name or to tear it down. We can ask concrete questions: whom does it help to see, what does it conceal, and can the person named answer or revise it?

These questions do not disable language. They make it less arbitrary.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16
Genesis presents naming as an act by which humanity enters the order of creation. The Dao De Jing begins by limiting that act. They do not offer the same answer, but they meet beside the same difficulty: we must live through language, yet must not let language claim to be the whole.

“The name that can be named is not the enduring name” does not abolish names. It abolishes their right to a final verdict.

### Page 17
We will still name diseases, mark roads, and find shared terms for injustice.

But after speaking, we can leave the person or thing before us room to answer and change. A name begins a conversation. It does not announce that the conversation is over.
