# 第005-2章：不仁与刍狗

## 原文

### 第1页

当我们把“不仁”与“刍狗”并置时，日常的道德直觉难免感到寒意：若连一个具体的人都不救，又何谈天下苍生？这个疑问也提醒我们，老子的词语一旦离开其语境，很容易被权力重新解释。法家从《老子》中吸收了若干概念，却把原本用来限制主宰冲动的“道”，改造成了君主驾驭臣下的技术。

### 第2页

这层转变可以从“无为”看出。老子用水、山谷和风箱说明不强作、不主宰的生成方式；法家则把这种关于世界如何运行的理解，收窄为君主如何用权的方法。“道”不再约束统治者的欲望，反而被用来提高统治的可控性。

## 释义

### 第3页

老子说“天地不仁”，并不是说自然冷酷无情，而是说天地不以人的偏爱来安排万物。每个生命都经历生长、成形与衰败，没有谁因受到特别眷顾而免于变化。法家式的政治解释却容易把这种“不偏爱”推向“无须顾及人的感受”。

### 第4页

同样地，老子所说的“圣人不仁”，也不是要求统治者变得冷漠，而是要求治理者不要把个人情感和私人偏爱带进公共秩序。真正的治理，不是靠情感施舍来维持秩序，而是靠规则、边界与自发的秩序来完成。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第5页

“刍狗”是古代祭礼中用来供祭的草扎狗，祭祀完毕便被弃置，象征着物件在仪式中的作用是暂时的，而不是值得执著的对象。老子借此表达一种平等而非偏私的视角。

“橐籥”指古代风箱，空而不屈，鼓动之后气息愈出。它用来说明虚而不空、动而不滞的道理。老子借此说明真正的力量并不在于充满，而在于保持空明和通达。

## 正文

### 第6页

法家的误读，最早体现在对“无为”的偷换。老子说“辅万物之自然”，强调君主是辅助者，而不是主宰者。法家却只取“无为”的形式，把它改写为“深藏不露、不测之威”。君主的不作为，不是为了给万物留出空间，而是为了让自己成为一个无法被揣测的黑箱，以便通过术来驾驭群臣。

### 第7页

法家又把“不仁”解释为“绝对中立”或“不要有温情”。老子的“不仁”要求治理者越过私人好恶；法家则更关心如何避免施惠削弱刑罚的威慑。商鞅的思路与此相近，认为使百姓贫弱、难以形成自主力量，更便于国家驱使。

### 第8页

至于“刍狗”，老子用它来象征万物在天地面前的平等，祭祀之后即弃，说明一切存在都不应被执著成绝对对象。法家却把百姓视为“耕战之资”，把国家的机器看成可以不断消耗和调配的资源。韩非子说“民之性，恶劳而乐佚”，便据此主张以重刑驱民于农战，把每个人都变成可计量的生产力单位。

### 第9页

韩非的《解老》《喻老》把老子的若干概念纳入君主权术的语境。与其说他逐句照搬“上善若水”，不如说他把无为、势与隐藏意图结合成一套统治技术。老子所说的“无为”，重在不以主宰者的私意干扰万物；到了韩非那里，它更多成为君主隐藏意图、以术驾驭群臣的方法。

### 第10页

秦朝是法家政治最集中的历史实践之一。它迅速灭六国，统一度量衡，也大规模修筑长城；强大的动员能力伴随着沉重的徭役与刑罚。传统叙事中，陈胜、吴广因雨误期，又担忧秦法惩处，遂起兵反抗。秦亡固然不能归结为单一原因，但一个缺少弹性、惯于把人当作耕战资源的制度，确实更难吸收灾害、误期与反抗带来的冲击。

## 回扣

### 第11页

把人物化、把人当工具、以冷酷方式治理天下的后果，历史上确实出现过，但那并不是老子的本意，而是对老子最严重的机械性误读。真正的“不仁”，不是天地对生命无情，而是天地不偏私、不妄加偏爱；真正的“无为”，不是让君主为所欲为，而是让治理回到边界清楚、秩序自发的状态。

### 第12页

所以，老子的“不仁”与法家的“冷酷”，并不是一回事。老子所说的，是一种超越私心、保持公正的广阔视角；法家所做的，是把这种视角拿来为权力服务。两者之间，差别不只是术语上的不同，而是整个政治伦理的不同。

# Chapter 005-2: Non-Benevolence and Straw Dogs

## Original Text

### Page 1

When “non-benevolence” is placed beside “straw dogs,” ordinary moral intuition understandably recoils: if one concrete person is not worth saving, what could it mean to care for all under Heaven? The question also shows how readily Laozi’s terms could be repurposed once removed from their setting. Legalist thinkers adopted several of his concepts, but turned a Dao that restrained the impulse to dominate into techniques by which a ruler might control ministers and subjects.

### Page 2

The change is clearest in the treatment of “non-action.” Laozi’s images of water, valley, and bellows describe generation without forcing or mastery. Legalist thought narrows this account of how the world works into a method for exercising power. Dao no longer restrains the ruler’s appetite; it helps make rule more controllable.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

When Laozi says “heaven and earth are not benevolent,” he does not mean that nature is cruel. He means that heaven and earth do not arrange life according to human preference. Every creature grows, takes form, and declines; none is exempted from change by special favor. A Legalist political reading can push this absence of favoritism toward the claim that human feeling need not count.

### Page 4

Likewise, when Laozi says that the sage is not benevolent, he does not mean that rulers should become indifferent. He means that political order should not be built on private affection or personal favoritism. True governance is not sustained by sentimental benevolence, but by rules, boundaries, and spontaneous order.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 5

“Straw dogs” were ritual objects made of straw and used in sacrifice; after the rite they were discarded. This image suggests that things should not be clung to as absolute possessions.

“A bellows” is an image of emptiness and movement. It suggests that power comes not from fullness, but from emptiness and openness.

## Main Text

### Page 6

The Legalists’ misreading begins with their alteration of “non-action.” Laozi says that rulers should assist the natural course of things, not dominate it. The Legalists, however, retained only the form of non-action and turned it into a strategy of concealment and intimidation. The ruler’s inaction was not meant to leave space for all beings, but to make himself an unfathomable black box through which power could be exercised.

### Page 7

They likewise reinterpreted “non-benevolence” as absolute neutrality or absence of warmth. Laozi asks rulers to move beyond private preference; Legalist reasoning is more concerned that favors might weaken the deterrent force of punishment. Shang Yang’s political logic is correspondingly severe: a population kept weak and unable to organize independently is easier for the state to direct.

### Page 8

As for the straw dog, Laozi used it to symbolize the equality of all beings before the cosmic order. Once the ritual is over, the object is discarded. The Legalists, however, treated people as resources for agriculture and warfare. Han Feizi’s claim that “human nature dislikes labor and loves ease” became the basis for coercive policies that turned each person into a measurable unit of production.

### Page 9

Han Feizi’s *Explaining Laozi* and *Illustrating Laozi* place several Daoist concepts within the language of rulership. Rather than claiming that he simply rewrites the line “the best is like water,” it is more accurate to say that he joins non-action, positional power, and concealed intention into a technique of rule. Laozi’s non-action restrains the ruler’s urge to impose; in Han Feizi it more often becomes a way for rulers to hide their intentions and manage ministers.

### Page 10

The Qin dynasty was one of the most concentrated historical applications of Legalist statecraft. It conquered the six states, standardized weights and measures, and undertook enormous building projects; the same capacity for mobilization imposed heavy labor and punishment. In the traditional account, Chen Sheng and Wu Guang were delayed by rain and, fearing the legal consequences, began their revolt. Qin’s fall had more than one cause, but a rigid system that treats people chiefly as resources for farming and war has little capacity to absorb disaster, delay, or resistance.

## Return to the Original

### Page 11

Politics that turns people into tools and governs through cold calculation did exist in history. But that was not Laozi’s teaching. His “non-benevolence” is not indifference; it is a way of stepping beyond private attachment and preserving public justice. His “non-action” is not a license for domination, but a way of restoring balance and allowing life to unfold.

### Page 12

That is why the difference between Laozi and the Legalists is not merely verbal. It concerns the deepest question of political ethics: whether power exists to dominate life, or to create conditions in which life can remain free, diverse, and humane.
