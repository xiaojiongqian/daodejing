# 第010-1章：能为——抱一而不宰

## 原文

### 第1页

载营魄抱一，能无离乎？专气致柔，能婴儿乎？

白话：使精神与形体相安相守，抱持内在的一体，能够不彼此分离吗？让生命之气专一而柔和，能够像婴儿那样柔软、完整而少有造作吗？

### 第2页

涤除玄览，能无疵乎？爱国治民，能无知乎？

白话：洗去心中观看世界时附着的偏见与装饰，能够使观照清明、少有瑕疵吗？爱护国家、治理人民，能够不依靠卖弄智巧和操控人心吗？

这里的“无知”不是愚昧无能，而是不把自己的聪明当作治理一切的充分理由。

### 第3页

天门开阖，能为雌乎？明白四达，能无为乎？

白话：面对变化的开合进退，能够保持接纳、回应而不抢先主宰吗？明白通达以后，能够不以自己的见识强行干预万物吗？

生之，畜之。生而不有，为而不恃，长而不宰，是谓玄德。

白话：使生命发生，养育它们；生养却不据为己有，做成事情却不倚功自重，帮助成长却不充当永久的主宰，这就叫作深微的德。

## 释义

### 第4页

本章由六个问题和一个回答组成。老子没有命令人必须成为完人，而是一再问：“能……乎？”

能不能身心不相离？能不能有力量而仍柔软？能不能看得清而不被偏见遮住？能不能治理而不卖弄智术？能不能回应变化而不抢占主动？能不能通达而不强作妄为？

六问最后汇入“玄德”：真正深厚的能力，不以占有、邀功和控制来证明自己。

### 第5页

“营魄”是古代关于生命与精神的用语，历代解释并不完全相同。河上公从魂魄与养生讲起，王弼则把它理解为人安处其身心、守住自身之真。今天不必把这些古说硬译成现代医学概念。

我们可以先把“抱一”理解为不让生命长期分裂：身体坐在餐桌旁，注意力却仍困在工作邮件里；口中答应休息，内心却不断审判自己不够勤奋。抱一不是永远专注于一件事，而是所做、所感与所在之处不至于彼此遗弃。

### 第6页

“专气致柔”也不必被简化成某一种呼吸术。王弼强调顺任自然之气，达到柔和；河上公强调使精气不乱。两家都没有把“柔”说成软弱和退缩。

婴儿尚未学会用僵硬的姿态维护身份。他饿了便哭，得到安抚便松开手掌，不必把一次不适写成关于自己失败的长篇故事。成年人无法回到无知的婴儿状态，却可以找回一种能力：经历压力以后，仍能恢复，不让防御成为永久性格。

### 第7页

“玄览”常被比作幽深而能照见事物的心镜。“涤除”不是把记忆与经验清空，而是擦去附着其上的成见。镜面若蒙着尘，映出的并非完全虚假，只是每一样东西都染上同一种灰色。

人在职位升高、知识增多以后，最难洗去的尘埃往往是“我已经知道”。真正的清明不是没有立场，而是知道自己的立场会遮住什么，并愿意让新事实修正旧判断。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第8页

**能为：** 河上公为第十章所加的章题。本章既谈“能”，也不断限制“为”：能力越大，越要避免能力变成占有与主宰。

**爱国治民与爱民治国：** 王弼本作“爱国治民”，河上公本作“爱民治国”。两者都把个人修养推向公共治理。

**无知与无为：** 王弼本作“爱国治民，能无知乎”“明白四达，能无为乎”；河上公本在这两处的文字次序不同。正文依王弼本，不把“无知”解释为无知无能，也不把“无为”解释为什么都不做。

**为雌：** “雌”在这里指接纳、柔静、应而不倡的姿态，不是对现实中的女性作性格规定。

**玄德：** 幽深而不显露主人的德。它使生命得以生长，却不把生命变成自己的财产。

## 画面描述

### 第9页

深夜里，一位刚成为父亲的年轻人抱着哭泣的婴儿，在房间里来回踱步。他担心手臂太松，便越抱越紧。坐在一旁的祖母轻声说：“托住他的背，不要攥住他。”

年轻人问：“不攥紧，怎么保护？”

“保护不是让他不能动。”祖母回答，“你给他一个不会跌落的地方，也给他转身和呼吸的余地。”

婴儿渐渐安静，手指从拳头里松开。年轻人低头看了很久，又问：“我怎样才能一直做对？”

祖母笑了：“你不能替他活成正确的人。你只能在他还小的时候养护他，在他能够站立以后，慢慢把你的手收回来。”

## 正文

### 第10页

“抱一”在今天最直接的敌人，是持续的自我分割。

一场会议尚未结束，我们已经在回复另一场会议的消息；陪伴家人时，我们计算明天的业绩；终于独处，又用屏幕填满所有空隙。效率似乎提高了，生命却像被拆成许多同时响起的房间。

抱一不是关闭世界。它只是要求我们在一个时刻真正抵达一个地方：谈话时听完一句话，疲惫时承认身体已经疲惫，作决定时让价值与行动暂时站在一起。

### 第11页

柔软并不意味着没有边界。真正柔软的材料能够承受变化，再回到可用的形状；僵硬之物看起来强，却可能把全部压力集中在一个断点。

一个专业人士承认“我需要再想想”，不等于缺乏能力；伴侣在争执中降低声音，也不等于放弃立场。致柔，是让力量不必始终保持攻击姿势。

婴儿之喻提醒我们：生命最初的完整，不靠形象管理。我们可以成熟，却不必把成熟误解成永远不能惊讶、不能求助、不能重新开始。

### 第12页

“涤除玄览”可以从一次不愉快的谈话开始。我们常在对方开口以前，便已准备好他将说什么；后来听见的每一句，只用来证明原先的判断。

洗去心镜的尘，不是要求自己毫无偏见——这几乎不可能。更现实的做法是，在判断旁边留一个问题：还有什么事实，会使我改变主意？

若答案是“没有任何事实”，那便不是清明，而是结论已经占据了观看的位置。

### 第13页

“爱国治民，能无知乎”最容易被误读成赞美昏庸。《史记》记载，刘邦谈及汉初三杰时坦承：谋略不如张良，治国与粮饷不如萧何，统兵不如韩信；自己的长处在于能够任用他们。

这段自评当然不能证明刘邦的一切治理都符合老子，却很好地说明一种有限的智慧：领导者不必假装自己在每个领域都最懂。

“无知”不是取消知识，而是取消全知的表演。能够说“此事他比我明白”，往往是组织真正开始拥有众人智慧的时刻。

### 第14页

《管子》和《国语》保存了管仲举荐人才的叙述。他向齐桓公逐项说明，在外交、农政、军事、司法和谏诤方面，分别有人比自己更合适。

管仲没有谦虚到否认自己的才能，也没有骄傲到包办所有事务。他知道自己负责什么，也知道哪一部分应交给别人。

这正连接了“明白四达”与“能无为”：知道得更多，不一定意味着亲手干预得更多。成熟的能力会建立条件、安排合适的人，然后不让自己的存在阻塞他们工作。

### 第15页

“生而不有”最容易在养育关系中被听懂。父母给予孩子身体、语言和最初的安全，却不能因此拥有孩子的人生。老师帮助学生形成能力，也不能要求学生永远重复老师的答案。

“为而不恃”不是假装自己没有付出，而是不把付出变成永久债权；“长而不宰”也不是放任不管，而是在需要保护时承担责任，在对方获得能力以后逐步归还选择。

玄德之“玄”，正在于作用真实，主人却不必处处显形。我们看见树在生长，却不需要每片叶子都刻着园丁的名字。

## 回扣

### 第16页

本章的六问，从内在完整一路走向与世界相处。

抱一，使精神与生活不长期分裂；致柔，使力量保有恢复和转向的能力；涤除玄览，使知识仍能接受现实；治民无知，使领导不以智术操控；开阖为雌，使行动能够回应而非总要抢先；明达无为，使看见全局的人不必霸占每个细节。

最后的“玄德”，不是第七项技巧，而是前六问共同形成的品格。

### 第17页

我们通常用控制来证明关心：因为珍惜，所以紧握；因为负责，所以包办；因为看得清，所以替别人决定。

老子提出另一种更难的关心。它使生命发生，给它养分，也肯让它拥有自己的方向。它做了该做的事，却不把“我做过”变成统治未来的凭据。

一个人真正强大，也许不在于多少事情必须经过他，而在于经过他的帮助以后，多少生命能够不再依赖他。

# Chapter 010-1: The Capacity to Act—Holding to One Without Ruling

## Original Text

### Page 1

Can you carry body and spirit, embrace the One, and keep them from separating? Can you gather the vital breath into softness and become like an infant?

In plain language: can the life of the mind and the life of the body remain at home with one another? Can strength become gentle enough to recover the openness, wholeness, and unforced vitality of an infant?

### Page 2

Can you cleanse the dark mirror and leave it without blemish? In caring for the state and governing the people, can you do without clever manipulation?

In plain language: can you wash away the assumptions and embellishments that cling to perception? Can you govern without treating cleverness and control as sufficient answers to every human problem?

“Without knowing” does not praise ignorance. It asks us not to make a performance of intelligence into the basis of rule.

### Page 3

As the gate of Heaven opens and closes, can you remain receptive? When understanding reaches everywhere, can you refrain from contrived action?

In plain language: amid change, can you respond without always rushing to dominate? After gaining wide understanding, can you avoid forcing that understanding upon everything?

Give life and nourish it. Give life without possessing, act without relying on the credit, help things grow without ruling them. This is called profound virtue.

## Interpretation

### Page 4

The chapter consists of six questions followed by one answer. Laozi does not command us to become flawless. He repeatedly asks, “Can you?”

Can body and mind remain together? Can strength stay soft? Can clear sight avoid distortion? Can government do without manipulative cleverness? Can change be met without seizing the initiative? Can far-reaching knowledge refrain from unnecessary interference?

All six questions arrive at profound virtue: capacity so deep that it no longer needs possession, credit, or control to prove itself.

### Page 5

“Body and spirit” translates an ancient expression whose meaning has never been entirely settled. Heshang Gong approaches it through the cultivation of the soul and the body; Wang Bi reads it as dwelling within oneself and preserving what is true. We need not force either account into the language of modern medicine.

We may begin by hearing “embrace the One” as a refusal to live permanently divided. The body sits at dinner while attention remains inside work email. The mouth agrees to rest while the mind prosecutes itself for insufficient effort. Holding to one does not mean thinking of one thing forever. It means that action, feeling, and presence do not continually abandon one another.

### Page 6

“Gather the breath into softness” should not be reduced to one prescribed breathing technique. Wang Bi emphasizes allowing natural vitality to reach harmony; Heshang Gong emphasizes keeping vital energy from disorder. Neither makes softness a synonym for weakness.

An infant has not learned to defend an identity through permanent tension. Hunger brings tears; comfort allows the hand to open again. One discomfort does not become a lifelong theory of personal failure. Adults cannot return to infant ignorance, but we can recover a related capacity: after stress, become available to life again rather than turning defence into character.

### Page 7

The “dark mirror” has often been understood as the deep awareness through which things are seen. Cleansing does not erase memory or experience. It removes what has settled upon them. A dusty mirror does not show a wholly invented world; it gives everything the same grey stain.

After status rises and knowledge accumulates, the hardest dust to remove is the sentence, “I already know.” Clarity is not the absence of a standpoint. It is knowing what our standpoint obscures and allowing new facts to revise an old conclusion.

## Key Terms and Textual Notes

### Page 8

**The Capacity to Act:** Heshang Gong's title for Chapter Ten. The chapter concerns ability, but repeatedly places limits on action. The greater the capacity, the more carefully it must avoid becoming possession and rule.

**Caring for the state and governing the people:** Wang Bi's text reverses the order found in Heshang Gong. Both move from personal cultivation into public government.

**Without knowing and without contrived action:** In Wang Bi's text, these phrases follow government and far-reaching understanding respectively. Heshang Gong's version places them differently. This chapter follows Wang Bi and reads neither ignorance nor inactivity into the words.

**Remaining receptive:** The image traditionally translated as “female” indicates receptivity, quiet strength, and responding without always initiating. It is not a prescription for the personalities of actual women.

**Profound virtue:** Influence whose owner remains difficult to see. It allows life to grow without turning life into property.

## Visual Description

### Page 9

Late at night, a young father walks back and forth with a crying infant. Afraid that his hold is too loose, he tightens his arms. The grandmother beside him speaks softly: “Support his back. Do not close your fist around him.”

“If I do not hold tightly, how can I protect him?” he asks.

“Protection does not mean making movement impossible,” she replies. “Give him a place from which he cannot fall, and room enough to turn and breathe.”

The infant gradually quiets, his fingers opening. The young father looks down for a long time. “How can I make sure I always do the right thing?”

The grandmother smiles. “You cannot live the correct life on his behalf. While he is small, nourish him. Once he can stand, slowly draw back your hand.”

## Main Text

### Page 10

The most immediate enemy of holding to one is the modern habit of dividing ourselves without pause.

Before one meeting ends, we answer messages from another. While sitting with family, we calculate tomorrow's performance. When solitude finally arrives, we fill every opening with a screen. Efficiency appears to rise, while life becomes a house of many rooms sounding at once.

Holding to one does not close the world. It asks us to arrive somewhere for one moment: hear a sentence to its end, admit when the body is tired, and let value and action briefly occupy the same place.

### Page 11

Softness does not mean the absence of boundaries. A genuinely flexible material can receive change and return to a useful shape. A rigid one appears strong but may concentrate all pressure at a single breaking point.

A professional who says, “I need to think again,” has not abandoned competence. A partner who lowers her voice during conflict has not abandoned her position. Softness allows strength to leave its permanent posture of attack.

The infant image reminds us that life's earliest wholeness needs no management of appearances. We can mature without defining maturity as the inability to wonder, ask for help, or begin again.

### Page 12

Cleansing the dark mirror may begin with one unpleasant conversation. We often decide what another person will say before he speaks. Every sentence afterward is used only to confirm the judgment already made.

To remove the dust is not to become free of all bias; that may be impossible. A more practical discipline is to place one question beside every conclusion: what fact would cause me to change my mind?

If the answer is “No fact could,” this is not clarity. The conclusion has occupied the place from which we were meant to see.

### Page 13

“Can you govern without clever manipulation?” is easily mistaken for praise of incompetence. The Records of the Grand Historian tells how Liu Bang spoke of the three outstanding men of the early Han. He admitted that he could not equal Zhang Liang in strategy, Xiao He in administration and supply, or Han Xin in command. His strength, he said, was his ability to employ them.

This self-assessment does not prove that all of Liu Bang's government followed Laozi. It does illustrate limited intelligence at its best: a leader need not pretend to be the greatest expert in every domain.

Doing without cleverness does not abolish knowledge. It abolishes omniscience as theatre. The words “She understands this better than I do” may be the moment when an organization first acquires the intelligence of many people.

### Page 14

The Guanzi and Discourses of the States preserve accounts of Guan Zhong recommending people to Duke Huan of Qi. He explains, field by field, that others are more suitable than he is for diplomacy, agriculture, military affairs, justice, and remonstrance.

Guan Zhong is not so modest that he denies his own ability, nor so proud that he attempts to perform every task. He knows his responsibility and knows what should belong to someone else.

This connects far-reaching understanding with non-interference. Knowing more does not always require doing more by hand. Mature capacity creates conditions, places suitable people, and then refuses to obstruct their work with its own importance.

### Page 15

“Give life without possessing” is easiest to hear in the work of care. Parents give a child body, language, and early safety, but do not thereby own the child's future. Teachers help students acquire ability, but cannot require them to repeat the teacher's answers forever.

To act without relying on credit is not to pretend that no effort was made. It is to refuse to turn effort into permanent debt. To help things grow without ruling is not neglect. It means accepting responsibility while protection is needed, then returning choice as capacity develops.

Profound virtue is profound because its influence is real while its owner need not be visible everywhere. We see the tree grow without finding the gardener's name carved into every leaf.

## Return

### Page 16

The six questions move from inner wholeness toward our conduct in the world.

Holding to one keeps mind and life from permanent division. Softness lets strength recover and turn. Cleansing perception keeps knowledge answerable to reality. Governing without cleverness refuses manipulation. Remaining receptive allows action to respond rather than always initiate. Understanding without interference prevents the person who sees the whole from occupying every detail.

Profound virtue is not a seventh technique. It is the character formed by all six questions together.

### Page 17

We often use control to prove that we care. Because we treasure something, we grip it. Because we are responsible, we do everything ourselves. Because we see clearly, we decide for others.

Laozi proposes a more difficult kind of care. It gives life and nourishment, yet permits a direction of its own. It performs the necessary work without turning “I did this” into a licence to govern the future.

Perhaps a person's real strength is measured not by how many things must pass through his hands, but by how many lives, after receiving his help, no longer need to.
