# 第004-3章：回归

## 原文

### 第1页

回归

和光同尘，不是厚黑学

在庸俗的官场解读中，“光”被视作“显赫的才能或功绩”，“尘”被视作“庸俗的环境或同僚”。“和光同尘”因此沦为一种策略：收敛光芒，以免刺眼；混入尘土，以免被排斥。

### 第2页

但在老子的宇宙观里，“光”与“尘”并非外在的处境，而是“道”在世间的两种存在质地：

“光”，是照见事物的能力：辨明处境，也看见彼此的关联。它不是“我要比别人耀眼”的光，更接近日光落在山河上——使事物显形，却不要求事物回头赞美。

“尘”，是衣食、劳作、衰老这些寻常生活，也是万物终会归返的物质。它不高贵，却具体承受着生长、磨损与死亡。

### 第3页

“和光同尘”不是把一个自认高贵的人放进低微环境中伪装，而是在觉性（光）与物质性（尘）的相遇中，松动“高贵与低贱”的分别。当人不再认定光必然高于尘，也不再一味追求发光或害怕蒙尘，才可能接近老子所说的道。

### 第4页

**体与用：“道冲而用之或不盈。”** 因为留有空处，所以能够持续容纳。这是道的自足性：它不依赖外在名目来证明自身，而以不盈的状态不断发挥作用。对生命而言，丰盛未必来自填满，也可能来自内在的空明与虚怀。

**功：“挫锐解纷。”** 这是道的消融性。注意，道不是“战胜”锐气与纷争，而是“挫”之、“解”之。这如同大雪覆盖山峦，并非用暴力铲平棱角，而是以轻柔的累积，将一切尖锐溶解于浑厚之中。这指向一种非对抗的转化力。

### 第5页

**相：“和光同尘。”** 这是道的隐匿性与遍在性。光与尘，本是一体两面。道不只存在于神庙或云端，也在呼吸所及的尘埃里，在眼前寻常的光线中。神圣以平凡的形态出现，不是故作低调，而是说明道从不孤悬于日常世界之外。

**源：“象帝之先。”** 老子把道放在“帝”的观念之前。他不再追问哪一位神发出第一道命令，而把目光移到命令和意志尚未成形时，万物何以发生。这一层的“回归”，是回到命名与支配尚未开始的状态。

### 第6页

“和光同尘”也可以成为一种生命理想：让光保持谦卑，让尘获得尊严。

这是许多传统文人向往的生命状态。苏轼在赤壁之下写道：“惟江上之清风，与山间之明月，耳得之而为声，目遇之而成色”，这可以理解为与光同游；他躬耕东坡、与农人往来，则近于与尘同息。他未必真正做到“玄同”，却一生都在向此道回归。

这个理想给了“光”以谦卑：智慧不必成为标榜的勋章，它可以像一盏不刺眼的灯，照见他人脚下的路，而不要求他人注目灯本身。

### 第7页

它给了“尘”以尊严：日常琐碎、柴米油盐、生老病死，这些看似“低微”的存在，不再是需要被超脱的“红尘牢笼”，而是道最亲切的显形。慧能说“佛法在世间，不离世间觉”，正是“同尘”的禅宗回响。

### 第8页

禅宗的一则公案，让光与尘在眼前相遇。

禅宗有一则公案，可以把“光与尘”拉回手边：

有人问洞山守初禅师：“如何是佛？”

洞山答：“麻三斤。”

麻是三斤重的一团麻线，是尘中之尘，物中之物。当学人满怀对“佛光”的崇敬去追问时，禅师却以最粗粝、最平凡之物截断其妄想。

禅师没有把学人领到另一处，只把问题压回眼前这三斤麻。麻要称量，也要用手捻成线；答案不在更高的地方，而在他刚才略过的事物里。

“和光同尘”可以落实为这种“归家稳坐”的踏实感：不再到别处寻找光，也不再急于逃离尘。低头面对手中的事，光与尘便同在其中。

### 第9页

光落在尘埃上，尘埃才显出光路；没有光，尘土昏暗，没有尘，光也无从被眼睛看见。

“和光同尘”并不要求人熄灭才智，也不要求把日常生活抬成神圣。它只让二者回到同一间屋子：看得明白，也肯把手放到眼前具体的事情上。

# Chapter 004-3: Return

## Original Text

### Page 1

Return

Blending light with dust is not an art of corruption.

In vulgar interpretations of official life, “light” is seen as prominent talent or achievement, and “dust” is seen as vulgar surroundings or colleagues. Thus “conceal light and blend with dust” becomes a strategy: dampen your brilliance so it does not glare; mix into the dust so you are not rejected.

### Page 2

But in Laozi’s cosmology, “light” and “dust” are not external circumstances; they are two modes of being for Dao in the world:

“Light” is the capacity to see: to distinguish a situation and perceive its relations. It is not the light of “I want to outshine others,” but something closer to daylight falling across rivers and mountains. It makes things visible without asking them for praise.

“Dust” is food, work, aging, and the ordinary matter to which living things return. It is not exalted, but it bears growth, wear, and death in concrete form.

### Page 3

To “conceal light and blend with dust” is not to disguise a supposedly noble self in a lowly environment. It loosens the distinction between noble and lowly by bringing awareness (light) and material existence (dust) together. When light is no longer assumed to be superior to dust, and one no longer seeks only to shine or fears being soiled, one draws closer to what Laozi calls Dao.

### Page 4

**Substance and function: “Dao is empty, yet in use it may never be filled.”** Because it leaves room, Dao can continue to receive and respond. Its self-sufficiency does not require an external title to validate it; its usefulness continues precisely because it is never full. For human life, abundance may likewise depend on openness and humility rather than accumulation alone.

**Action: “Blunt sharpness and dissolve conflict.”** This is Dao’s dissolving nature. Note, Dao does not “overcome” sharpness and conflict; it blunts them and dissolves them. This is like snow covering mountains, not smashing away edges with violence but gently accumulating until all sharpness dissolves into fullness. It points to a non-confrontational transformative power.

### Page 5

**Appearance: “Conceal light and blend with dust.”** This is Dao’s hiddenness and ubiquity. Light and dust are two sides of one whole. Dao is not confined to temples or clouds; it is also in the dust we breathe and the ordinary light before our eyes. The sacred appears in common forms because Dao is not suspended outside daily life.

**Origin: “Seeming to precede the Lord.”** Laozi places Dao before the idea of a supreme ruler. Rather than ask which god issued the first command, he turns toward the conditions from which things arise before command and intention have taken shape. Here, return means going back before naming and domination begin.

### Page 6

“Conceal light and blend with dust” can also become an ideal of life: light retains humility, while dust is granted dignity.

Many traditional literati longed for such a way of life. At Red Cliff, Su Shi wrote that the river breeze became sound to the ear and the mountain moon color to the eye; this can be read as traveling with light. Farming at Dongpo and living among villagers brought him closer to breathing with dust. He may never have fully attained profound unity, but he spent his life returning toward it.

This ideal gives humility to light. Wisdom need not become a badge; it can be a lamp that illuminates the ground before others without demanding attention for itself.

### Page 7

It gives dignity to dust. Daily trifles, firewood, rice, oil, salt, birth, aging, sickness, and death—these apparently lowly existences are no longer a red dust prison to escape. They are the most intimate manifestation of Dao. Huineng said, “Buddha Dharma is in the world, not separate from worldly awareness.” This is the Chan echo of blending with dust.

### Page 8

A Zen case brings light and dust into an encounter before our eyes.

A Zen case brings light and dust back within reach.

Someone asked Dongshan Shouchu, “What is Buddha?”

Dongshan answered: “Three catties of hemp.”

Hemp is a bundle of hemp weighing three catties. It is dust within dust, thing within thing. When a student asks in full reverence for Buddha light, the master stops the delusion with the roughest, most ordinary object.

The master sends the questioner nowhere else; he presses the question back into the three catties of hemp at hand. Hemp must be weighed and twisted into cord. The answer is not higher up, but in the thing the student had just overlooked.

“Conceal light and blend with dust” can be felt as the grounded ease of “returning home and sitting steadily.” There is no need to search elsewhere for light or to flee from dust. When attention returns to the task at hand, light and dust are already together there.

### Page 9

Light falls on dust, and dust makes the path of light visible. Without light, the dust is dim; without dust, the beam cannot be seen by the eye.

Blending light with dust asks neither that intelligence be extinguished nor that ordinary life be declared sacred. It returns both to the same room: see clearly, and place your hands on the particular work before you.
