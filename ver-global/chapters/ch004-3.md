# 第004-3章：回归

## 原文

### 第1页

回归

真正的和光同尘，而非厚黑学

在庸俗的官场解读中，“光”被视作“显赫的才能或功绩”，“尘”被视作“庸俗的环境或同僚”。“和光同尘”因此沦为一种策略：收敛光芒，以免刺眼；混入尘土，以免被排斥。

### 第2页

但在老子的宇宙观里，“光”与“尘”并非外在的处境，而是“道”在世间的两种存在质地：

“光”，是道的明觉性。它代表智慧、觉知、穿透力，是道那种“昭昭然”却能照亮万物的能力。它不是“我要成为英雄”的光，而是“太阳照亮山河”那种无目的、无主体的纯粹明朗。

“尘”，是道的遍在性与平凡性。它代表物质、现象、日常琐碎，是道“无所不在”的卑微载体。万物归根于尘土，尘土承载着生死荣枯，是最真实的“在”。

### 第3页

真正的“和光同尘”，不是将高贵的自己下放到低贱的环境中去伪装，而是在觉性（光）与物质性（尘）的合一中，消解了“高贵与低贱”这个分别本身。当您不再认为“光”比“尘”更高级，您便解脱了；当您不再追求“只发光”或“怕蒙尘”，您便得道了。

### 第4页

1. 第一、二层次（体与用）：“虚而不竭”——空，所以无限。这是道的自足性。它不需要任何外部供养，它自身就是永不枯竭的源泉。这向我们揭示：生命真正的丰盛，恰在于内在的空灵与虚怀。

2. 第三层次（功）：“挫锐解纷”——这是道的消融性。注意，道不是“战胜”锐气与纷争，而是“挫”之、“解”之。这如同大雪覆盖山峦，并非用暴力铲平棱角，而是以轻柔的累积，将一切尖锐溶解于浑厚之中。这指向一种非对抗的转化力。

### 第5页

3. 第四层次（相）：“和光同尘”——这是道的隐匿性与遍在性。光与尘，本是一体两面。道不显灵于神庙，不垂迹于云端，它就在你呼吸的尘埃里，在你目光所及的光线中。最高的神圣，以最平凡的形态栖居。这不是故作低调，而是神圣的本性就厌恶孤悬于外的“超越”，它渴望通过“同尘”来完成自身的圆满。

4. 第五层次（源）：“象帝之先”——这是道的原生性。它彻底拒绝了“被造”的身份。在人类所有宗教的创世神话中，神都是“第一推动者”；但老子说，道比这个“第一推动者”更早。这意味著：道不服从任何命令，它本身就是命令的源头；它不遵循任何意志，它本身就是意志得以产生的“前意志”状态。 这一层的“回归”，是回到一切命名、一切神祇、一切哲学体系诞生之前的那个“寂静”。

### 第6页

三、“和光同尘”作为生命的最高理想：光的谦卑与尘的尊严

它是“历代顶级文人的生存梦想”，苏轼在赤壁之下悟得“惟江上之清风，与山间之明月，耳得之而为声，目遇之而成色”，这便是与光同游；他又在躬耕东坡、与农人同坐时，实践了与尘同息。他一生未真正做到“玄同”，但一生都在回归此道。

这个理想的魅力在于它给了“光”以谦卑：您的智慧不应成为标榜的勋章，而应成为不刺眼的烛火，它照亮他人脚下的路，却不允许他人膜拜这根蜡烛。

它给了“尘”以尊严：日常琐碎、柴米油盐、生老病死，这些看似“低微”的存在，不再是需要被超脱的“红尘牢笼”，而是道最亲切的显形。慧能说“佛法在世间，不离世间觉”，正是“同尘”的禅宗回响。

### 第7页

四、禅宗的一则公案：光影里的相遇

我想起一则禅宗公案，或许能补足您对“光与尘”的体悟：

有人问洞山良价禅师：“如何是佛？”

洞山答：“麻三斤。”

麻是三斤重的一团麻线，是尘中之尘，物中之物。当学人满怀对“佛光”的崇敬去追问时，禅师却以最粗粝、最平凡之物截断其妄想。

那“象帝之先”的道，那无远弗届的光，正藏在这一团麻线的纤维里，等待你从仰望星空的姿态，回归到低头搓绳的当下。

真正的“和光同尘”，便是这种“归家稳坐”的踏实感。您不再需要到别处去寻找光，因为您本身就是一束不发光的光；您也不再需要逃离尘，因为您终于理解，尘埃落定之处，正是净土。

### 第8页

刻观看光尘的这份宁静里，悄然敷用，不竭不盈。

光和尘，既高贵，又卑微，既给尘世带来光亮，又给尘世带来真实。它们处处弥漫，无远弗届，却不让自己的形体显现，这实在是一种生命理想。

# Chapter 004-3: Return

## Original Text

### Page 1

Return

True concealment of light and blending with dust, not the dark arts of corruption.

In vulgar interpretations of official life, “light” is seen as prominent talent or achievement, and “dust” is seen as vulgar surroundings or colleagues. Thus “conceal light and blend with dust” becomes a strategy: dampen your brilliance so it does not glare; mix into the dust so you are not rejected.

### Page 2

But in Laozi’s cosmology, “light” and “dust” are not external circumstances; they are two modes of being for Dao in the world:

“Light” is the luminous awareness of Dao. It represents wisdom, perception, and penetrating power. It is the Dao that “shines clearly” and illuminates all things. It is not the light of “I want to become a hero,” but the pure, purposeless, subjectless brightness of “the sun lighting rivers and mountains.”

“Dust” is the ubiquity and ordinariness of Dao. It represents matter, phenomena, and daily trivialities. It is the humble carrier of Dao’s omnipresence. All things return to dust, and dust bears the rise and fall of life. It is the truest form of being.

### Page 3

True “conceal light and blend with dust” is not lowering your noble self into a lowly environment to disguise yourself. It is dissolving the distinction between noble and lowly through the unity of awareness (light) and materiality (dust). When you stop thinking light is higher than dust, you are liberated. When you stop pursuing only to shine or fearing being covered in dust, you have attained Dao.

### Page 4

1. First and second levels (substance and function): “Empty yet inexhaustible”—emptiness, therefore infinity. This is Dao’s self-sufficiency. It needs no external nourishment. It is itself a source that never runs dry. This reveals that the true abundance of life lies in inner emptiness and humility.

2. Third level (action): “Blunt sharpness and dissolve conflict”—this is Dao’s dissolving nature. Note, Dao does not “overcome” sharpness and conflict; it blunts them and dissolves them. This is like snow covering mountains, not smashing away edges with violence but gently accumulating until all sharpness dissolves into fullness. It points to a non-confrontational transformative power.

### Page 5

3. Fourth level (appearance): “Conceal light and blend with dust”—this is Dao’s hiddenness and ubiquity. Light and dust are two sides of one whole. Dao does not reveal its spirit in temples or leave traces in clouds. It is in the dust you breathe and the light your eyes perceive. The highest sacredness dwells in the most ordinary form. This is not false modesty. The sacred nature simply detests being isolated in transcendence. It longs to complete itself through blending with dust.

4. Fifth level (origin): “Before the image of the emperor”—this is Dao’s original nature. It utterly rejects the identity of being created. In human creation myths, gods are first movers; Laozi says Dao precedes that first mover. This means Dao obeys no command. It is itself the source of command. It follows no will. It is the pre-volitional state from which will arises. This layer of return is a return to the silence before all names, deities, and philosophical systems were born.

### Page 6

Three: “Conceal light and blend with dust” as life’s highest ideal: the humility of light and the dignity of dust.

It is the survival dream of top literati across generations. Su Shi realized at Red Cliff, “Only the cool breeze on the river and the bright moon between mountains become sound to the ear and color to the eye.” That is traveling with light. When he farmed at Dongpo and sat with villagers, he practiced breathing with dust. He never fully achieved profound unity, but he spent his life returning to this Dao.

The charm of this ideal is that it gives humility to light. Your wisdom should not become a badge to show off. It should be a candle that does not glare, lighting the path beneath others’ feet without allowing anyone to worship it.

### Page 7

It gives dignity to dust. Daily trifles, firewood, rice, oil, salt, birth, aging, sickness, and death—these apparently lowly existences are no longer a red dust prison to escape. They are the most intimate manifestation of Dao. Huineng said, “Buddha Dharma is in the world, not separate from worldly awareness.” This is the Chan echo of blending with dust.

### Page 8

Four: a Zen story—an encounter in light and shadow.

I recall a Zen case that may complete your understanding of light and dust.

Someone asked Dongshan Liangjie, “What is Buddha?”

Dongshan answered: “Three catties of hemp.”

Hemp is a bundle of hemp weighing three catties. It is dust within dust, thing within thing. When a student asks in full reverence for Buddha light, the master stops the delusion with the roughest, most ordinary object.

That Dao “before the image of the emperor,” that light with no distance, is hidden in the fibers of that bundle of hemp, waiting for you to return from gazing at the stars to bow your head and twist rope in the present.

True “conceal light and blend with dust” is this grounded sense of “returning home and sitting steadily.” You no longer need to seek light elsewhere because you yourself are a light that does not shine. You no longer need to escape dust because you finally understand that where dust settles is pure land.

### Page 9

In the quiet of watching light and dust, it is applied subtly, inexhaustible yet never overflowing.

Light and dust are both noble and humble. They bring light to the mundane world and also bring reality. They permeate everywhere, reaching without leaving form. This is truly a life ideal.
