# 第006-2章：留白的门

## 原文

### 第1页

河上公本将这一章题为“成象”：

谷神不死，是谓玄牝。
玄牝之门，是谓天地根。
绵绵若存，用之不勤。

### 第2页

白话说来：那像山谷一样空灵而有感应的生命作用，并不会消亡；它可称作幽深而能孕育万物的根源。万物由此出入，天地由此生发。它细微、连续，仿佛若有若无；顺着它来用，便不必把它逼到疲惫。

## 释义

### 第3页

古代注家从不同方向接近这几句话。王弼着眼于谷的空处：它无形无影，居于低处而不争，却使万物由此得以生成。河上公则把“谷”读作养，把“用之不勤”落实到呼吸与养身：气息宜宽舒，不宜急疾劳顿。

这两种读法不必彼此取消。一种提醒我们，真正的根源往往不占据舞台；另一种提醒我们，身体也会立刻知道什么叫“使用得太勤”。

### 第4页

因此，“用之不勤”并不是劝人逃离工作，或把安静变成一项新的绩效任务。它是在问：我们是否把一切可贵的东西——睡眠、注意力、亲密、好奇心——都误当成一口只要加大压力便会出水的井？

谷神不是一块永不磨损的电池。它更像一片能涵养水的土地：需要来去的节律，需要没有被立即占满的地方，才会持续地给出。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第5页

**成象**：河上公本为第六章所加的章题。它提示本章以谷、门、根等可感的形象，引向难以指认的生成之理。

**谷神**：并非山谷中的人格神。王弼以无形、处卑而能成物来解释它，强调“空”所具有的感应与成全。

**玄牝**：以幽深与生养的意象指向根源；不宜把它缩成某种固定的性别角色或神秘实体。

**绵绵若存，用之不勤**：细密地延续，仿佛存在而不可把握；使用时不急迫、不劳伤。河上公的注释特别把它联系到宽舒而不急促的呼吸。

## 画面描述

### 第6页

夜色刚刚越过城市公寓的玻璃窗。餐桌上放着一台仍亮着的笔记本电脑，屏幕里的日程表被蓝色会议块塞得没有缝隙；一杯水在旁边，已经很久没人喝。一个刚结束视频会议的人坐在桌前，终于合上电脑。她的女儿从走廊尽头问：“你现在能听我说一件事吗？”镜头不急着给出回答，只慢慢移向半开的窗：风吹动窗帘，远处车流像低低的潮声，屋里第一次出现可供一句话落下的空间。

## 正文

### 第7页

许多疲惫并不来自困难本身，而来自一种更隐蔽的习惯：我们把每一段空白都解释成浪费。升职之后，日历更满；假期里，手机仍在证明自己不可替代；与家人共处时，也急着用建议、消息和安排填满停顿。

这是一种富足时代特有的贫乏。我们拥有许多选择，却渐渐失去一处让选择沉淀为判断的内在空地。

### 第8页

在职场里，“用之太勤”未必表现为加班。它也可能表现为：会议还没有出现真正的问题，结论已经被抢先写好；团队还没来得及提出异议，下一张幻灯片已被推进；每个人都很忙，却没有谁有机会听见自己究竟在替什么而忙。

一间组织需要的，不只是更快的反馈，也是一点不被立即征用的时间。像山谷留出回声的距离，好的讨论要允许一句话走出去，再带着不同的意义回来。

### 第9页

家庭里也是如此。沉默不一定是疏离；有时它是让一个不善表达的人慢慢开口的门。真正的倾听不是急着给出聪明的回应，而是暂时不把对方的经验加工成自己的判断。

个人成长也不靠把心灵拧得更紧。散步时不带耳机，午后不把每一分钟填成任务，给一段关系留下不必立刻解释的余地——这些不是优雅的装饰，而是对“玄牝之门”最朴素的尊重：让尚未成形的东西有机会出现。

### 第10页

1952年，约翰·凯奇请钢琴家大卫·都铎坐到一架钢琴前，打开琴盖，却在四分三十三秒里不弹出一个音符。起初，观众只听见自己的不耐烦；随后，座椅的轻响、厅外的风、衣料的摩擦和呼吸，逐渐成了整场演出的内容。

这并不是说沉默比音乐更高贵。它只是让人发现：音乐从来不只在音符中发生。正如山谷不是因为塞满了东西才有回声，留白并非缺席；它是让原本被忽略的事物能够抵达我们的条件。

### 第11页

凯奇不是在重述老子，他的作品也不等于道家哲学。但它提供了一个跨文化的近邻：当演奏暂时停止，世界并没有变空，反而变得可听。老子所说的“谷神”，同样不是一片僵死的空白，而是一种不抢先占满、因而能够回应的开放。

对习惯以产出衡量价值的人来说，这一点格外难。我们很容易敬重能立即交付的答案，却不容易敬重孕育答案的停顿。第六章要保护的，正是这种尚未被商品化的深处。

## 回扣

### 第12页

“玄牝之门”不在远方，也不要求我们遁入山中。它可能是一口没有被催促的呼吸，一场允许迟疑的对话，一份不把人耗尽才算尽责的工作，或一间让沉默不必尴尬的屋子。

“用之不勤”不是少做一点的口号，而是一种较为温柔、也更有耐心的使用之道：知道何时推动，何时停手；知道源头并非为了被耗尽，而是为了使生命能够一再开始。

# Chapter 006-2: The Gate That Leaves Room

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Heshang Gong recension gives this chapter the title “Forming Images”:

The valley spirit never dies; this is called the mysterious female.\
The gate of the mysterious female is called the root of heaven and earth.\
Fine and unbroken, it seems to be there; draw upon it, and it is never wearied.

### Page 2

In plain language: the living efficacy of a valley-like emptiness does not perish. It may be called the deep, generative source from which things arise. Through it, the ten thousand things come and go, and heaven and earth take root. It continues delicately, almost as if it were not there; when we use it in keeping with its nature, we need not drive it into fatigue.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

Ancient commentators approached these lines from different directions. Wang Bi attended to the emptiness of the valley: it has no fixed form, remains low rather than contending, and yet allows the ten thousand things to arise. Heshang Gong reads *valley* as nurture, and brings “do not use it too diligently” down to breath and care of the body: breath should be spacious, not rushed or strained.

The two readings need not cancel one another. One reminds us that a true source rarely occupies the stage; the other reminds us that the body is often the first place to tell us when we have been using something too hard.

### Page 4

“Do not use it too diligently” is therefore not an instruction to flee work, nor a demand that calm become another performance metric. It asks whether we have mistaken everything precious—sleep, attention, intimacy, curiosity—for a well that will yield more water whenever we apply more pressure.

The valley spirit is not an everlasting battery. It is closer to land that holds water: it needs a rhythm of coming and going, and places not immediately occupied, if it is to keep giving.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 5

**Forming Images**: The title assigned to Chapter Six in the Heshang Gong recension. It points to the chapter’s use of graspable images—valley, gate, root—to approach a process of generation that cannot itself be pointed to.

**Valley spirit**: Not a personal god in a valley. Wang Bi reads it as the responsive and completing efficacy of what has no visible form and remains low.

**Mysterious female**: An image of depth and generation. It should not be reduced either to a fixed gender role or to a mystical object.

**Fine and unbroken; never wearied**: A continuation too subtle to grasp. Heshang Gong particularly connects the line with breathing that is spacious rather than hurried.

## Visual Description

### Page 6

Night has just crossed the glass of a city apartment. On the dining table, a laptop still glows with a calendar packed solid by blue meeting blocks; beside it stands a glass of water that no one has touched for a long time. A person who has just left a video call sits down and finally closes the computer. From the far end of the hallway, her daughter asks, “Can you listen to something now?” The camera does not hurry to supply an answer. It moves toward the half-open window: a curtain stirs, the traffic below sounds like a low tide, and for the first time the room has space in which a sentence might land.

## Main Text

### Page 7

Much exhaustion comes not from difficulty itself, but from a quieter habit: we interpret every blank space as waste. Promotion makes the calendar fuller; holidays still require the phone to demonstrate our indispensability; time with family is filled, too, with advice, messages, and arrangements.

This is a poverty peculiar to an age of abundance. We possess many options, yet gradually lose the inward clearing in which an option can settle into judgment.

### Page 8

At work, using ourselves too diligently does not always look like overtime. It can look like a meeting in which the conclusion is written before the real question has appeared; a team pushed on to the next slide before anyone has time to disagree; a room full of busy people, none of whom can say what their busyness is for.

An organisation needs more than rapid feedback. It also needs time that is not immediately claimed. Just as a valley leaves enough distance for an echo, a good discussion lets a sentence travel outward and return with a meaning no one had planned.

### Page 9

The same is true at home. Silence is not always distance; sometimes it is the door through which someone who struggles to speak can slowly enter. To listen well is not to produce a clever response at once, but to refrain for a moment from processing another person’s experience into our own conclusion.

Growth, too, does not come from tightening the mind another turn. A walk without headphones, an afternoon not filled minute by minute, a relationship allowed not to explain itself at once: these are not elegant accessories. They are modest forms of respect for the gate of the mysterious female, giving what has not yet taken shape a chance to appear.

### Page 10

In 1952, John Cage asked the pianist David Tudor to sit at a piano, raise its lid, and play no notes for four minutes and thirty-three seconds. At first, the audience heard only its own impatience. Then the creak of seats, the wind outside, the friction of fabric, and the sound of breathing became the evening’s material.

This is not to say that silence is nobler than music. It simply reveals that music has never happened only in notes. A valley does not echo because it has been crammed full; blankness is not absence, but the condition in which what we had overlooked can reach us.

### Page 11

Cage was not restating Laozi, and his work is not a substitute for Daoist thought. But it offers a nearby experience across cultures: when playing pauses, the world does not become empty; it becomes audible. Laozi’s “valley spirit” is likewise not a dead blank, but an openness that can respond precisely because it does not rush to fill itself.

For those accustomed to measuring worth by output, this can be difficult to honour. We readily admire an answer that arrives at once, and less readily the pause that makes an answer possible. Chapter Six protects this depth before it is turned into a commodity.

## Return to the Original

### Page 12

The gate of the mysterious female is not far away, nor does it demand that we retreat to the mountains. It may be a breath not hurried along, a conversation permitted to hesitate, a form of work that does not count exhaustion as proof of devotion, or a room in which silence need not embarrass anyone.

“Use it without overworking it” is not a slogan for doing less. It is a gentler, more patient art of use: knowing when to press forward and when to stop; knowing that a source is not there to be consumed, but to let life begin again and again.
