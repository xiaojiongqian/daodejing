# 第009-3章：把位置还给时间——功成之后的退身之道

## 原文

### 第1页

功遂身退，天之道。

白话：事情已经完成，便不再把自己长久放在功劳和位置的中央；知道何时让位、交还与离开，这是顺应天地运行的方式。

河上公本作“功成、名遂、身退，天之道”。比王弼本多出的“名遂”二字，使劝诫更加清楚：工作完成是一回事，名声已经归来又是另一回事。最难放下的，往往不是工作本身，而是工作完成后别人看待我们的方式。

### 第2页

人们通常以为，失败之后最需要智慧。老子却把劝诫放在成功之后。

失败会迫使人停下，成功却给人继续占据的理由。昨天的决定带来胜利，今天便更难容许别人决定；一次掌声证明了能力，下一次沉默便像是失去。功劳本应结束一项工作，却可能悄悄开始另一项工程：不断证明“这里仍然需要我”。

“功遂身退”所处理的，正是成功者与自己成果之间的新关系。

## 深层释义

### 第3页

王弼解释“功遂身退”时说：“四时更运，功成则移。”春天并不因为催生了花木，便阻止夏天到来；白昼也不要求黄昏继续使用自己的光。

河上公则用“日中则移，月满则亏”说明盛满难以久居。这些古注不是要人害怕一切成就，而是提醒：完成之后，时间已经改变了问题。先前的问题是怎样做成；此后的问题是怎样不让已成之功被占有、骄矜与失时损坏。

### 第4页

“功”“名”“身”“退”，可以看成四个连续的关口。

功，是事情确已完成，不是半途而废；名，是成果已经被看见，不必继续追索承认；身，是那个渐渐把位置当成自我的人；退，则是重新调整关系——把权力交还，把功劳分开，把自己从唯一中心移到合适的位置。

因此，“退”不一定意味着消失。它首先意味着：我不再要求一件公共成果永远作为我的私人证明。

### 第5页

成功为什么尤其需要退？因为功劳会改变周围人的说话方式。

一个人尚未成功时，伙伴还会与他争论；一旦他被视为英雄，意见便容易变成请示，质疑也容易变成冒犯。他拥有的经验没有减少，能够听见的现实却可能越来越少。

所以，身退不只是避祸之术，也是恢复现实的办法。领导者让出发言的第一分钟，前辈允许后来者修改旧方案，创办人接受组织不再处处像自己，都是在让成果脱离个人威望，重新接受世界的检验。

### 第6页

退可以发生在四个层面。

退位，是在阶段完成后交出职权；退名，是不独占署名与赞誉；退利，是不把共同成果尽数换成个人所得；退我，则最难——不再以“没有我就不行”确认自己的价值。

一个人可能离开职位，却仍隔着门遥控一切；也可能继续承担责任，却已经让别人取得能力、荣誉和决定权。前者有退的外形而没有退的实质，后者未必归隐，却已经从中心退开。

### 第7页

老子的次序不能倒置：先“功遂”，后“身退”。

在困难刚出现时抽身，把未完成的责任留给同伴，不叫知止；事情仍需要自己的专业判断时，为了表现洒脱而沉默，也不是无为。真正的退身要求人先完成承诺，再判断哪些权力已经没有继续占有的理由。

同样，退也不必成为一种表演。若一个人不断提醒众人自己多么淡泊，淡泊便成了新的名声。身退的尺度不在姿态有多优雅，而在离开之后，事情是否更能独立运行，别人是否真正获得了空间。

## 画面描述

### 第8页

楚国复国后的清晨，宫门外摆着一方官印、一袋黄金和一把旧屠刀。卖羊肉的屠羊说站在台阶下，衣服仍带着长途奔走后的尘土。楚昭王请他走近，再一次问：“流亡路上，你照料我的饮食。如今国家恢复，我该拿什么报答你？”

屠羊说答道：“国家失去时，我失去了羊摊；国家回来，我也取回了羊摊。我只是随着大家逃难，又随着大家回来，不能把共同经历说成我一个人的功劳。”

“那么给你高位呢？”

“我若因一次相随便取得高位，后来的人会以为，忠诚只是一种更长远的买卖。”

他说完，拿起旧屠刀，向市集走去。国王望着那方无人领取的官印，忽然明白：有些人拒绝赏赐，并非看轻国家，而是不肯让自己的帮助变成索取国家的凭据。

## 典故展开

### 第9页

《庄子·让王》中的屠羊悦——“说”在这里读作“悦”——不是一位拥有封地的贵族，只是市井里的屠者。楚昭王流亡时，他随行照料；昭王复国后要赏他，他却说，国家失去时自己失去职业，国家恢复时自己也恢复职业，此外没有什么功劳可领。

这段话听起来近乎固执。按照通常的成功叙事，受苦应当兑换补偿，忠诚应当兑换晋升。屠羊悦却切断了这条交换。他承认自己做过事，却拒绝把所做之事变成无限索取的账户。

### 第10页

屠羊说的“退”，不是厌弃社会。他没有走入深山，也没有宣布世事皆空；他只是回到自己的羊摊。

这使“身退”出现了更朴素的一面：不是从生活退出，而是从功劳的特殊待遇中退出。普通生活并非成功以前不得不忍受的候车室，也可以是一个人主动返回的完整世界。

曾国藩后来在诗中劝弟弟低头敬服屠羊说，因为褒奖与毁谤总会相伴而来。名声升高以后，控制名声的能力并不会一同增加；不把自己完全交给赞誉，也就不必被每一份毁谤重新定义。

### 第11页

范蠡提供了另一种退法。《史记》记载，他辅佐越王勾践成就霸业后离开越国，改换姓名，在齐与陶经营产业。十九年间，他三次积累千金，其中两次把财富分给贫困旧交和族人。

后世常把这段历史简化成“三聚三散”，仿佛他每次都把财富散尽。更谨慎的说法是：范蠡能聚，也曾能散；晚年仍留下可观家业。他没有证明财富毫无价值，而是证明身份、职位和财富都不必成为不可移动的牢笼。

他的身退不是从能力退回无能。离开权力中心以后，判断力仍在，只是换了一个不必依附旧功的生活方向。

### 第12页

张良的故事让我们看见：想退，与能够退，并不是同一件事。

《史记》说，刘邦让他自择齐地三万户，他只请求封在早年与刘邦相遇的“留”。后来他想远离人间事务，从赤松子游，却没有完全从汉初政治与宫廷关系中消失。

张良不是一个失败的反例。他提醒我们，权力关系一旦缠得太深，退路便不再只由个人决定。若等到所有人都无法想象没有你的秩序，身退就会变得格外困难。培养接替者、分散知识和减少个人依赖，必须在掌声最高以前开始。

### 第13页

南朝名将韦睿没有在每次胜利后辞官，却懂得“退名”。《南史》记载，他有功时常常迟些报捷，不急着争先；庆功宴上，他还曾翻转骰子，把彩头让给曹景宗，口中装作惊讶。

一枚被轻轻翻过的骰子，当然不能概括复杂的军政生涯，却揭示了一种细小而困难的节制：有能力的人不必把每一次比较都赢到底。

这不是讨好，也不是否认实力。若一项事业需要多人同行，适时让出可见的功劳，会使伙伴仍愿意贡献，而不是逐渐变成一个英雄身后的布景。

### 第14页

跨越文化来看，乔治·华盛顿在独立战争结束后，于一七八三年向大陆会议辞去大陆军总司令职务，交还军权；两届总统任期之后，他又选择不再参选。

他的选择与老子并非出自同一哲学传统，却共同提出一个政治学问题：个人取得权力之后，制度是否具备让他离开的道路？一次自愿交还权力，价值不只在个人谦逊，也在于它向后来者证明，职位可以有终点，国家不必与一个人的生命捆在一起。

这里的“退”因此不只是自我保全。它也可能成为公共秩序的一部分：权力因为能够交接，才不必每次都以冲突结束。

### 第15页

并非所有不退都违背老子，也并非所有离开都合于道。

诸葛亮“鞠躬尽瘁，死而后已”，与范蠡选择不同。但在他自己理解的使命中，蜀汉事业并未“功遂”。我们可以讨论他的判断是否合宜，却不能只凭没有归隐，便断言他不懂退。

反过来，一个人在危机中辞去责任，或退居幕后却继续控制继任者，也不能仅凭“离职”二字获得老子的赞许。经文真正要求的不是同一种人生结局，而是诚实判断：事情是否完成？继续居中是在服务成果，还是在服务一个不肯结束的自我？

## 回扣

### 第16页

把“功遂身退”带回今天，可以从几个具体动作开始。

完成项目时，把关键知识留在团队，而不是只留在自己的记忆里；接受荣誉时，说清他人的贡献；培养继任者时，允许对方形成不同风格；离开岗位以后，不再用旧身份否决每个新决定；帮助家人时，也不把付出变成日后支配他们的凭据。

这些动作不会保证一个人永远安全，也不是获取更大名声的技巧。它们只是使成功不再成为新的占有。

### 第17页

功成以前，人要战胜困难；功成以后，人常要面对更隐蔽的对手——那个已经习惯被需要、被赞美、被等待的自己。

老子劝人退，不是因为成果不值得珍惜。恰恰相反，正因为成果值得长久，才不能让它永远依赖一个人的威望。季节完成自己的工作，便把天地交给下一季；成熟的人完成自己的工作，也把位置还给后来者和时间。

真正的身退，不是从世界消失。

它是让世界在没有我们站在中央的时候，仍然能够继续。

# Chapter 009-3: Returning the Place to Time—The Art of Stepping Back

## Original Text

### Page 1

When the work is fulfilled, step back. This is the way of Heaven.

In plain language: once the work is complete, do not remain forever at the centre of its credit and authority. Know when to yield, return, and leave. This is how one moves with the order of Heaven and earth.

Heshang Gong's version reads, “When the work is accomplished and the name achieved, step back. This is the way of Heaven.” The added reference to reputation sharpens the warning. Finishing the task is one thing; receiving recognition is another. What is hardest to release is often not the work, but the way the completed work has taught others to look at us.

### Page 2

We usually imagine that wisdom is most necessary after failure. Laozi places his warning after success.

Failure forces a pause. Success supplies reasons to remain. Yesterday's decision brought victory, so today it becomes harder to let another person decide. One round of applause confirmed our ability; the next silence feels like disappearance. Achievement, which should conclude a task, quietly begins another project: proving that “I am still indispensable here.”

“When the work is fulfilled, step back” addresses this new relationship between successful people and their results.

## Deeper Interpretation

### Page 3

Wang Bi explains the line with eight words: “The four seasons revolve; when the work is complete, each moves on.” Spring does not prevent summer from arriving because it brought forth the flowers. Daylight does not ask evening to continue using its light.

Heshang Gong invokes the sun moving after noon and the moon waning after fullness. These commentaries do not teach fear of achievement. They remind us that completion changes the problem. Before success, the question was how to accomplish the work. Afterward, the question is how to keep possession, pride, and poor timing from damaging what has been accomplished.

### Page 4

Work, name, self, and retreat can be read as four successive thresholds.

The work must truly be complete; this is not abandonment. Recognition has already arrived; it need not be pursued without end. The self is the person beginning to confuse a position with an identity. Retreat then changes the relationship: return authority, share credit, and move from the only centre to a fitting place.

Stepping back does not necessarily mean disappearing. It means first of all that a public achievement no longer has to serve forever as private proof of our worth.

### Page 5

Why does success especially require retreat? Because achievement changes the way people speak around us.

Before someone succeeds, colleagues still argue with her. Once she is treated as a hero, opinions turn into requests for permission, and disagreement begins to sound like disrespect. Her experience has not diminished, but the amount of reality she can hear may steadily decline.

Stepping back is therefore more than a technique of self-protection. It restores contact with reality. A leader gives up the first minute of every discussion. A senior colleague lets a newcomer revise the old plan. A founder accepts that the organization will no longer resemble him in every detail. The result is released from personal prestige and tested by the world again.

### Page 6

Retreat can take place on four levels.

One can yield office by returning authority when a phase is complete; yield fame by sharing credit and authorship; yield advantage by refusing to convert a common achievement wholly into private gain; and, most difficult of all, yield the self that needs to hear, “Without you, none of this can continue.”

A person may leave a title and still control everything from outside the door. Another may remain responsible while giving others real ability, recognition, and choice. The first has the appearance of retreat without its substance. The second has not become a hermit, yet has moved away from the centre.

### Page 7

Laozi's sequence cannot be reversed: first the work is fulfilled, then one steps back.

Leaving when difficulty first appears and handing unfinished duties to colleagues is not knowing when to stop. Nor is silence a form of non-action when the task still requires one's professional judgment. Genuine retreat first honours the commitment, then asks which powers no longer have a reason to remain in our hands.

Retreat need not become a performance. If we repeatedly remind everyone how detached we are, detachment becomes a new reputation. Its measure is not how graceful our departure appears. It is whether the work can stand more independently afterward and whether other people have received real room.

## Visual Description

### Page 8

On the morning after the restoration of Chu, an official seal, a bag of gold, and an old butcher's knife rest outside the palace gate. The butcher Yue stands below the steps, his clothes still carrying the dust of exile. King Zhao asks him once more, “You looked after my food during our flight. Now that the state has been restored, how shall I reward you?”

“When the state was lost,” the butcher replies, “I lost my stall. When the state returned, I recovered it. I fled with everyone and returned with everyone. I cannot call a shared ordeal my private achievement.”

“Then accept a high office.”

“If one journey beside a king gives me high office, those who come later may think loyalty is only a longer form of trade.”

He takes up the old knife and walks toward the market. Looking at the unclaimed seal, the king understands: some refuse reward not because they care nothing for the state, but because they will not turn service into a permanent claim upon it.

## Stories and Reflections

### Page 9

The butcher Yue in the Zhuangzi is no landholding noble. He is an ordinary market butcher. He tends King Zhao during exile. After the king returns to Chu and offers a reward, Yue answers that when the state was lost he lost his occupation, and when it was restored he recovered it. He has no further merit to collect.

The reasoning sounds almost obstinate. In an ordinary story of success, suffering should become compensation and loyalty should become promotion. Yue breaks that exchange. He acknowledges what he did but refuses to turn the deed into an account from which he may draw without limit.

### Page 10

The butcher's retreat is not a rejection of society. He does not disappear into the mountains or declare ordinary affairs meaningless. He simply returns to his stall.

This gives stepping back a humbler meaning. It is not withdrawal from life, but withdrawal from the exceptional treatment that achievement can demand. Ordinary life is not merely a waiting room endured before success. It can be a complete world to which one deliberately returns.

Centuries later, Zeng Guofan wrote a poem advising his brother to bow before the butcher Yue. Praise and denunciation, he suggested, rise together. As reputation grows, our power to control it does not grow in equal measure. If praise does not wholly define us, every criticism need not remake us either.

### Page 11

Fan Li offers another kind of retreat. The Records of the Grand Historian says that after helping King Goujian of Yue achieve supremacy, he left Yue, changed his name, and built businesses in Qi and Tao. Over nineteen years he amassed a thousand in gold three times and twice distributed wealth among poor old friends and relatives.

Later retellings often compress this into “three accumulations and three dispersals,” as though he gave away everything each time. More cautiously, we can say that Fan Li knew how to gather and had also learned how to release. He still left a substantial family fortune. His story does not prove that wealth is worthless. It shows that identity, office, and property need not become immovable prisons.

He did not retreat from competence into helplessness. Away from the centre of power, his judgment remained; it simply entered a life that no longer depended on his earlier achievement.

### Page 12

Zhang Liang's story reveals that wanting to withdraw and being able to withdraw are different things.

According to the Records of the Grand Historian, Liu Bang invited him to choose thirty thousand households in Qi. Zhang Liang asked only for Liu, the place where he had first met Liu Bang. Later he wished to leave human affairs and follow the immortal Master Red Pine, yet he never wholly vanished from the political and court relationships of the early Han.

He is not a failed counterexample. He shows that once relations of power become deeply entangled, the way out is no longer ours alone to choose. If everyone has been taught to imagine no order without us, departure becomes exceptionally difficult. Successors, distributed knowledge, and reduced personal dependence must begin before the applause reaches its height.

### Page 13

The Southern Dynasties general Wei Rui did not resign after every victory, but he knew how to yield fame. The History of the Southern Dynasties records that he was slow to report his own victories rather than racing to claim them. At a celebration, he is also said to have turned the dice so that Cao Jingzong received the prize, while pretending to be surprised.

One quietly turned die cannot summarize a complicated military life. It does reveal a small and difficult restraint: a capable person need not win every comparison to its furthest point.

This is neither flattery nor the denial of ability. When a common undertaking requires many people to continue together, yielding visible credit can keep colleagues from becoming scenery behind a single hero.

### Page 14

Across cultures, George Washington resigned his commission as commander in chief of the Continental Army to Congress in 1783 after the War of Independence. After two presidential terms, he chose not to stand again.

His decisions did not arise from Laozi's philosophical tradition, yet they pose the same political question: after an individual acquires power, does the system provide a way for him to leave it? A voluntary return of authority matters not only as personal modesty. It shows successors that an office can have an ending and that a state need not be tied to the span of one life.

Retreat is therefore not merely self-preservation. It can become part of public order. When power can be handed over, every succession need not end in conflict.

### Page 15

Not every refusal to retire violates Laozi, and not every departure accords with Dao.

Zhuge Liang's phrase “to bend oneself to the task until one's strength is exhausted” describes a path very different from Fan Li's. Yet within Zhuge Liang's own understanding of his duty, the work of Shu Han had not been fulfilled. We may question his judgment, but we cannot decide that he failed to understand retreat merely because he did not become a recluse.

Conversely, resigning during a crisis or moving behind the curtain while continuing to control a successor does not become wise because it is called retirement. The real questions are more exacting: Is the work complete? Does remaining at the centre serve the result, or a self that cannot bear to end?

## Return

### Page 16

To bring “step back when the work is done” into modern life, we can begin with concrete acts.

At the end of a project, leave essential knowledge with the team rather than only in personal memory. When receiving honour, name the contributions of others. When preparing a successor, permit a different style. After leaving a role, do not use an old identity to veto every new decision. When helping relatives, do not turn generosity into a future claim to govern their lives.

None of these acts guarantees safety, and none should be used as a trick for obtaining greater prestige. They simply prevent success from becoming a new possession.

### Page 17

Before achievement, we struggle with difficulty. After achievement, we often meet a quieter opponent: the self that has grown accustomed to being needed, praised, and awaited.

Laozi advises retreat not because the result is unworthy of care. It is precisely because the result deserves a longer life that it cannot remain dependent on one person's authority. A season completes its work and returns the world to the next season. A mature person completes the work and returns the place to successors and time.

True retreat is not disappearance from the world.

It is allowing the world to continue when we are no longer standing at its centre.
