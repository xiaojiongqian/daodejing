# 第001-1章：地图与未命名海岸

## 原文

### 第1页

道可道，非常道；名可名，非常名。

无名，天地之始；有名，万物之母。

### 第2页

故常无欲，以观其妙；常有欲，以观其徼。

此两者，同出而异名，同谓之玄。玄之又玄，众妙之门。

## 释义

### 第3页

老子开篇不是劝人闭口不言。

他说的是一句很日常的事实：话一出口，便只能照亮事物的一面。医生写下诊断，海员标出航道，父母说“这孩子很内向”——这些说法都可能有用，却没有一句足以代替眼前那个仍在变化的人或世界。

### 第4页

名字很有用。路标让旅人找到渡口，病名让医生交换判断，姓名使一封信能够抵达。

问题出在名字用久以后，我们常忘记它只是一次划分。一个人被称作“失败者”，一片土地被标成“荒地”，称呼便开始替我们决定该怎样对待它。此时我们看见的，往往先是标签，再是事物。

### 第5页

“无名”不是空白，而是划分尚未发生时，事物仍可向许多方向发展的状态。

“有名”也不是错误。孩子要被呼唤，药材要被辨认，季节要有农时。老子没有要求我们在“无”与“有”之间选边；他把命名之前和命名之后同时保留下来。

### 第6页

“无欲”是暂时不急着取用，不让已有目的替眼睛作答，因而有机会察觉“妙”。

“有欲”则带着事情去看：哪里可走，什么可用，边界在何处，这便是“徼”。同一片海岸，画师看光，渔民看潮，工程师看岩层。老子要保留的，是目的之外仍能再看一眼的余地。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第7页

**道**：本义是路，也可以指方法、原则、运行方式。第一章里的“道”，不能只缩小成普通规则。

在第一章中，它指向万物生成和运行的根本方式，却不等于任何一条普通规则。王弼说，可说可名的东西已经落入“指事造形”：话语一旦指出某件事、描成某种形状，也就舍弃了其余角度。

### 第8页

**名**：名字、称谓、分类和概念。名让世界可交流，也让世界被切分。

名字的作用是把某一处说清楚，它的限度也正在这里。把沼泽叫作“待开发土地”，与把它叫作“候鸟栖地”，会引出不同的行动。第一章并不反对命名，只要求命名者记得：每个称呼都带着取舍。

## 正文

### 第9页

一个旅行者来到一片尚未画完的海岸。

向导把一张尚有大片空白的地图铺在木桌上，请他先别急着补齐。旅行者受过测绘训练，习惯给地形定名、画线、编号。纸上没有标记的地方令他不安，也令他兴奋。

### 第10页

旅行者写下岬角、礁石、港湾、市集小路。

地图很快有了用处。陌生人循标记找到水井，船夫避开暗礁，孩子也能指出回家的路。命名使一个人的经验留给了后来者。

### 第11页

到了黄昏，潮水上涨。

白天露出的礁石沉入海里，一条未曾标出的水路在月光下显了出来。图上的线没有错，却少了潮汐、季节和风向。旅行者第一次认真看见，一张准确的地图也有日期。

### 第12页

旅行者拿起笔，急着补上新的名字。

向导按住笔杆：“明早再写。先看看它退到哪里，什么时候又合上。”匆忙命名会把一次偶然当成规律，也会让人停止追问。

### 第13页

旅行者停住了。

他看着潮线一寸寸移动，船影被波纹折断又接起。地图的空白不再像一个等待消灭的缺口，而像一项尚未完成的观察。

### 第14页

他终于明白：地图不是错的。

错的是以为画过一次，海岸便应当照着纸上的线保持不动。地图保存了人走过的路，也删去了无数细节；它的可靠，来自不断校订，而不是假装永远正确。

### 第15页

他把地图的一角留白。

他在空白旁写了一个日期和两个字：“待察。”这不是故作神秘，而是给下一次潮水留下位置。

## 回扣

### 第16页

回到第一章，老子不是叫我们丢掉名字。

他要我们分清名字能做什么，不能做什么。地图可以带路，却不能成为大地；“有名”使生活可以安排，“无名”提醒我们，安排之外仍有变化。两者同出，不必互相取消。

### 第17页

旅行者带着地图继续走。

从此，他写下一个地名时，也会记下潮位、日期，以及尚未弄清的地方。

# Chapter 001-1: The Map and the Unnamed Coast

## Original Text

### Page 1

The Tao that can be trodden is not the enduring and unchanging Tao.

The name that can be named is not the enduring and unchanging name.

### Page 2

(Conceived of as) having no name, it is the Originator of heaven and earth; (conceived of as) having a name, it is the Mother of all things.

Always without desire we must be found, if its deep mystery we would sound; but if desire always within us be, its outer fringe is all that we shall see.

Under these two aspects, it is really the same. Together we call them the Mystery. Where the Mystery is the deepest is the gate of all that is subtle and wonderful.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

Laozi does not begin by asking us to fall silent.

He starts from an ordinary fact: once spoken, a sentence can show only one side of a thing. A doctor records a diagnosis; a sailor marks a channel; a parent says, “She is a shy child.” Each statement may be useful. None has finished describing the person or world still changing before us.

### Page 4

Names are useful. A sign directs a traveler to the ferry; a diagnosis lets physicians compare judgments; a person’s name allows a letter to arrive.

Trouble begins when we forget that a name is only one division. Call a person a failure, or mark a wetland as vacant land, and the label begins deciding how we treat what is there. We meet the category before we meet the thing.

### Page 5

The nameless is not a blank. It is the condition before our divisions settle, while a thing may still develop in several directions.

The named is not an error. Children must be called, medicines distinguished, and seasons tied to the work of planting. Laozi does not ask us to choose one side; he preserves the world both before and after naming.

### Page 6

“Without desire” means looking without immediately trying to use or settle what we see. “With desire” means looking for a path, a purpose, or a boundary. A painter, a fisher, and an engineer notice different features of the same coast. Laozi preserves the chance to look once more beyond the purpose that brought us there.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 7

**Dao**: A way, road, method, or mode of unfolding. In Chapter One, it cannot be reduced to an ordinary rule.

Here it points toward the basic way things arise and move, without becoming one ordinary rule among others. Wang Bi notes that speech must point to a particular affair and give it shape. In doing so, it necessarily leaves other angles outside the frame.

### Page 8

**name**: A name, label, category, or concept.

A name clarifies one aspect, and that is also its limit. Calling a marsh “land awaiting development” leads to different actions than calling it “a nesting ground.” Chapter One does not reject names; it asks the namer to remember that every term includes a choice.

## Main Text

### Page 9

A traveler arrived at a coast no map had finished.

The guide spread an unfinished map on a wooden table and asked him not to fill it too quickly. Trained in surveying, the traveler was accustomed to naming, drawing, and numbering terrain. The unmarked spaces unsettled and excited him.

### Page 10

The traveler named the headland, the reef, the harbor, and the road to the market.

The map became useful. Strangers found the well, sailors avoided the reef, and children could point out the road home. A name allowed one person’s experience to remain for the next.

### Page 11

At dusk, the tide rose.

The reef sank under water, and a moonlit passage appeared where no line had been drawn. The map was not wrong; it was missing tide, season, and wind. For the first time, the traveler noticed that even an accurate map has a date.

### Page 12

The traveler lifted his pen, eager to add another name.

The guide held the pen still. “Write tomorrow. First see how far it recedes, and when it closes again.” A hurried name can turn an accident into a rule and bring inquiry to an early end.

### Page 13

The traveler stopped.

He watched the tide line move by inches and the shadows of boats break and join in the ripples. The blank area no longer looked like a defect to eliminate. It looked like an observation not yet completed.

### Page 14

He understood at last: the map was not wrong.

What was wrong was expecting the coast to keep faith with a line drawn once. A map preserves where people have gone and omits countless details. Its reliability depends on revision, not on pretending to be final.

### Page 15

He left one corner of the map blank.

Beside the blank he added a date and two words: “Observe again.” He was not cultivating mystery. He was leaving room for the next tide.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

Chapter One does not ask us to throw names away.

It asks us to distinguish what names can and cannot do. A map can guide us, but it cannot become the ground. The named makes life workable; the nameless reminds us that life continues beyond our arrangements. Neither needs to cancel the other.

### Page 17

The traveler went on with the map.

After that, whenever he wrote a place-name, he also recorded the tide, the date, and what he had not yet understood.
