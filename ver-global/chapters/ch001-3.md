# 第001-3章：轮匠放下凿子

## 典故引入

### 第1页

堂上有书，堂下有木。

齐桓公坐在堂上读古人的话，轮扁在堂下斫车轮。一个人的依据写在书上，另一个人的判断留在手里：木料吃进多少，轮毂松紧几分，做错了当场便看得见。

### 第2页

这个故事出自《庄子》。

轮扁并非笼统反对读书。他追问的是：文字能传下多少经验？古人已经不在，书页保存了他们说过的话，却能否连同说话时的判断、处境与手感一并保存？

### 第3页

齐桓公读的是古人的书，轮扁做的是眼前的轮。

一个面对已经留下的文字，一个面对还没有成形的木头。书中的判断可以反复读，手下的判断必须立刻承担结果。二者都是真知识，却不能互相代做。

### 第4页

书页沙沙，凿声笃笃。

堂上翻过一页，堂下的凿声停一下，又继续。书页保存前人的话；凿子碰到的是这一块木头的结疤、韧性和湿度。文本可以相同，材料不会。

### 第5页

轮扁忽然停手，抬头问：“君上读的是什么？”

齐桓公说：“圣人的话。”轮扁接着追问那些人是否还活着。他要分开的，正是说话的人与留下来的文字。

### 第6页

轮扁又问：“那些圣人还在吗？”

齐桓公说：“早已不在了。”

### 第7页

轮扁说：“那么君上所读的，恐怕只是古人留下的迹。”

齐桓公听出了冒犯，命他说明白。轮扁没有搬出另一部书，只能拿自己每天做轮子的经验作证；若说不清，这经验也救不了他。

## 正文

### 第8页

轮扁举起手里的车轮。

他说，动作放缓了，榫接容易松；逼得太急，工具又进不去。差一点，轮便不合用。这样的分寸不是先在脑中得到一个答案，再命令手照做，而是在许多次偏松、偏紧之后逐渐长到手上。

### 第9页

“不松不紧的那一点，在手里，也在心里。”

他说，其中的分寸，嘴不能完全说清。他并非什么也说不出；松紧、快慢都可以讲。难传的是遇到这一块木头时，手该在哪一刻停下，又该多进半分的判断。

### 第10页

他说自己不能完全传给儿子。

儿子也不能从一句口诀里把它全部接走，所以他到了老年仍在亲手斫轮。父亲可以示范、纠正，把失败的原因讲明；但儿子的手仍须在木头上犯过自己的错，才会知道那句话落在哪里。

### 第11页

这个故事不是叫人轻视书。

没有书，齐桓公连古人的话也读不到。轮扁反对的是另一件事：把留下来的话当作完整无缺的本人，把能背诵的知识误当成已经会做。

### 第12页

一册讲游泳的书可以说明换气、姿势和水流，却不能替读者下水；师傅画出的墨线也不能替徒弟落下第一刀。

文字把容易遗漏的经验保存下来，但身体必须重新检验它。读书与练习之间，少了哪一边都不完整。

### 第13页

轮匠量木、看弧、试松紧，这是“有欲”。

他必须知道轮的尺寸、木料的走向、工具进入的角度。目的使注意力集中在可测量、可修正之处；没有这些明确的边界，手艺便无从谈起。

### 第14页

轮匠放下成见，等手、心和木纹在同一刻相遇，这是“无欲”。

这里的“妙”不是技术以外的神秘力量。轮扁不先强迫木头符合想象，而是根据手上传来的阻力调整动作。所谓“无欲”，在这一刻就是不让预设压过材料。

### 第15页

齐桓公没有再急着翻页。

书还在案上，轮扁也回到木头前。一次问答没有判定书与手谁更高，只让齐桓公以后读到一句成说时，多问一句：这句话在怎样的事情里才算数？

## 回扣

### 第16页

第一章让每一句话记得自己的边界。

能说出的道保留了前人的指点，却保留不了每一次现场。一个人还须亲自看材料如何回应，亲自承受判断的误差。“不可尽言”不是拒绝说明，而是承认说明之后还有练习。

### 第17页

读书的人合上书，轮匠放下凿子。

新做的车轮滚过院地，辙痕很快被尘土盖住。书页可以留下轮扁的话，下一只轮子仍要由另一双手重新做成。

# Chapter 001-3: The Wheelwright Sets Down the Chisel

## Allusion

### Page 1

There were books above, and wood below.

Duke Huan of Qi sat in the hall reading the words of the ancients, while Wheelwright Pian shaped a wheel below. One man’s authority stood on the page; the other man’s judgment remained in his hands. How much wood would yield, how tightly the parts would fit—an error showed itself at once.

### Page 2

The story comes from the Zhuangzi.

Pian is not simply attacking books. He asks how much experience writing can carry. The ancients are gone: can a page preserve not only their words, but also the circumstances, judgment, and practiced feel with which those words were used?

### Page 3

Duke Huan read an ancient book. The wheelwright worked on a wheel still taking shape.

One faced words already left behind; the other faced wood not yet finished. A written judgment can be read again. A judgment at the workbench must bear its result immediately. Both are forms of knowledge, but neither can do the other’s work.

### Page 4

Pages whispered. The chisel answered.

A page turned above. Below, the chisel paused and resumed. The book preserved another person’s words; the tool met this piece of wood, with its knot, moisture, and resistance. A text can remain the same. Material does not.

### Page 5

The wheelwright stopped and looked up.

“What is my lord reading?” he asked. “The words of sages,” said the duke. Pian then asked whether those sages were still alive. He was separating the speakers from the words that remained.

### Page 6

“Are those sages still alive?” asked the wheelwright.

“Long gone,” said the duke.

### Page 7

“Then what my lord reads,” said the wheelwright, “may be only the traces they left behind.”

Duke Huan heard the insult and demanded an explanation. Pian had no rival scripture to cite. He could appeal only to what happened each day at his bench, and if he failed to make his case, that experience would not protect him.

## Main Text

### Page 8

The wheelwright lifted the rim in his hands.

He said that if he worked too gently, the fit remained loose; if he pressed too urgently, the tool would not enter. A small error made the wheel useless. The measure did not begin as an answer in his head. It gathered in his hands through many attempts that were too loose or too tight.

### Page 9

“The point between loose and tight lives in the hand and in the heart.”

He said the measure between the two could not be fully put into words. He could explain speed, pressure, looseness, and fit. What he could not hand over was the judgment that tells a particular hand when to stop and when to press a fraction farther into this particular wood.

### Page 10

He said he could not fully pass it to his son.

His son could not receive it entire from a formula, so Pian still shaped wheels in old age. A father can demonstrate, correct, and explain why a piece failed. The son’s own hand must still make errors in wood before the words acquire a place.

### Page 11

The story does not ask us to despise books.

Without books, Duke Huan would not have had even the words of the ancients. Pian resists something else: mistaking the words left behind for the whole person, or mistaking knowledge that can be recited for a skill one can perform.

### Page 12

A book on swimming can explain breathing, posture, and current, but it cannot enter the water for its reader. A master’s ink line cannot place an apprentice’s first cut.

Words preserve experience that might otherwise be lost. The body must test it again. Reading and practice are incomplete without each other.

### Page 13

The wheelwright measures wood, reads the curve, and tests loose and tight. This is desire.

He must know the wheel’s dimensions, the run of the grain, and the angle of the tool. Purpose directs attention toward what can be measured and corrected. Without those boundaries, there is no craft to discuss.

### Page 14

The wheelwright releases his fixed idea and waits until hand, heart, and grain meet in one moment. This is desirelessness.

Nothing supernatural has entered the workshop. Pian does not force the wood to match an idea formed in advance; he adjusts to the resistance arriving through his hands. Here, desirelessness means refusing to let expectation overrule the material.

### Page 15

Duke Huan did not hurry to turn the page again.

The book remained on the table, and Pian returned to the wood. Their exchange did not decide whether books or hands were superior. It left Duke Huan with a further question whenever he met a settled phrase: in what kind of practice does this sentence prove true?

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

Chapter One asks every sentence to remember its edge.

The Dao that can be spoken preserves guidance, but not every encounter in which it must be used. A person still has to watch how material responds and bear the cost of error. What cannot be fully said is not an excuse to avoid explanation; it is a reminder that explanation is followed by practice.

### Page 17

The reader closes the book. The wheelwright sets down the chisel.

The new wheel crosses the courtyard, and its track is soon covered by dust. The page may preserve Pian’s words. The next wheel must still be made again by another pair of hands.
