# 第006-1章：谷神不死

## 原文

### 第1页

谷神不死，是谓玄牝。玄牝之门，是谓天地根。绵绵若存，用之不勤。

### 第2页

此句以“谷神”比喻道之本源，说明万物之所以能够生生不息，并非因为外在的力量始终强盛，而是因其内在有一种不竭的根基。

### 第3页

“玄牝”既是深奥之母，也是万物生成之门。它看似隐而不显，却能持续滋养天地万物，使一切事物得以由微而盛、由弱而强。

## 释义

### 第4页

“谷神不死”并非在讲某种神秘的灵体，而是在说明世界的根本力量是隐而不见、却永不枯竭的。它像山谷中的水源，虽然不显山川之势，却能长久地滋润四方。

### 第5页

“玄牝”是道的阴柔而深邃的一面。它并不以强硬姿态显现，而是以柔和和包容为万物提供生成的条件。所谓“玄”，是深奥；所谓“牝”，是生育与承载。

### 第6页

“玄牝之门”不是某个具体的门户，而是万物生成的起点。天地之所以能够长久存在，是因为它们拥有这样一个根基。所谓“绵绵若存”，是说这根基虽不张扬，却持续存在；“用之不勤”，则说明它并不需要耗尽自身便能不断发挥作用。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第7页

**谷神**：指道之深层本源，常被解释为不死的、无形的生命之根。它不是外在的神明，而是万物生成的内在源头。

**玄牝**：玄，深奥；牝，阴柔、孕养。此处用以形容道之母性的、生成性的根基。

**玄牝之门**：万物生发的入口，亦可理解为天地生成的根本关窍。

**天地根**：天地存在之根本，不是外在造物，而是内在不绝的源头。

**用之不勤**：使用它时并不费力，也不需要刻意耗损；它自然而然地持续运作。

## 正文

### 第8页

这一章的核心，在于提醒人们：真正能支撑世界的，并不是外在的浮华与强力，而是深藏于万物之中的那个不竭根源。

### 第9页

在日常经验里，人们往往只看见事物表面的张力与变化，却忽略了其背后的源头。一个山谷看起来空空荡荡，却恰恰因为它储藏着水脉，才能在干旱时依然滋养生命。

### 第10页

老子借“谷神”来说明，万物之所以能长久，不是因为它们一味强求，而是因为它们根植于一种隐而不显的力量。与其盲目追逐外在的繁盛，不如回到根本，守住那份不绝的源流。

### 第11页

“玄牝”之所以重要，是因为它代表了一种生成的方式。它不靠锋芒毕露，不靠强行推动，而是以柔和的承载和细微的滋养，使生命得以自然成长。

### 第12页

这也解释了为什么道家常强调“柔弱胜刚强”。外在的强硬终究容易耗损自身，而深层的柔和却能长期维持秩序。真正有力量的，不一定是最显眼的，而是最能持久的。

### 第13页

“绵绵若存”说明这种根基虽然看似无声，却始终存在。它不是偶然的爆发，而是持续的回流；不是一时的风采，而是长久的生命力。

### 第14页

而“用之不勤”，则告诉人们：当一个人顺应道的运行时，并不需要以过度的努力去维持一切。事情自然发生时，反而更能长久。所谓“无为”，并不等于什么都不做，而是在不妨碍根本秩序的前提下，让万物自成其理。

### 第15页

因此，这一章的意义，不只是关于宇宙生成的隐喻，更是关于人生修养的提醒。一个人若想真正立身于世界，不必过分张扬，而要在内心保有深厚、平静、持久的根基。

## 回扣

### 第16页

本章所强调的，是世界和人生的根本并不在于外在的炫耀，而在于内在源头的持续存在。任何事物若失去其根基，便难以持久。

### 第17页

所以，真正的力量不是外在的争夺，而是内在的充盈；真正的长久，不是不断消耗，而是始终守住那份深沉、柔和、无言而不绝的根。

# Chapter 006-1: The Valley Spirit Never Dies

## Original Text

### Page 1

The valley spirit never dies; this is called the mysterious female. The gateway of the mysterious female is called the root of heaven and earth. It is continuous and seems to remain; use it without effort.

### Page 2

This passage uses “the valley spirit” as a metaphor for the source of the Dao, showing that all things continue to arise not because external force remains strong, but because there is an inexhaustible foundation within them.

### Page 3

The “mysterious female” is both the profound mother and the gate of generation. It appears hidden and unnoticeable, yet it continually nourishes heaven, earth, and all living things, allowing everything to grow from small beginnings into fullness.

## Interpretation

### Page 4

“The valley spirit never dies” does not refer to a literal spirit, but to the hidden and never-ending power that sustains the world. It is like a spring hidden in a valley: though it does not display great force, it can nourish the land for a long time.

### Page 5

The “mysterious female” represents the yin, subtle, and nurturing side of the Dao. It does not manifest through force, but through receptivity and care, providing the conditions for all beings to arise.

### Page 6

The “gateway of the mysterious female” is not a physical doorway, but the point from which all things are generated. Heaven and earth endure because they rest upon such a foundation. “Continuous and seems to remain” means this source does not vanish even though it is not loudly displayed; “use it without effort” means it can sustain everything without being exhausted.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 7

**The valley spirit**: The deep source of the Dao, often understood as an immortal and invisible root of life. It is not an external deity, but the inner source of all generation.

**The mysterious female**: “Mysterious” means profound; “female” means receptive, nurturing, and generative. It refers to the motherly aspect of the Dao.

**The gateway of the mysterious female**: The opening through which all things are born, or the fundamental passage of generation.

**The root of heaven and earth**: The fundamental basis of the existence of heaven and earth, not a separate created object but an unbroken source.

**Use it without effort**: One can draw upon it without strain or exhaustion; it operates naturally and continuously.

## Main Text

### Page 8

The core of this chapter is to remind us that what truly sustains the world is not outward display or force, but a hidden source that remains inexhaustible at the deepest level.

### Page 9

In everyday experience, people notice only the surface motion and change of things, but overlook the source behind them. A valley may look empty, yet because it stores water it can still nourish life in times of drought.

### Page 10

Laozi uses the image of the valley spirit to show that things endure not because they constantly strive, but because they are rooted in a hidden power. Rather than obsessively chasing outward prosperity, one should return to the source and preserve the unbroken flow.

### Page 11

The importance of the “mysterious female” lies in the way it generates life. It does not rely on force or display; instead, it sustains growth through gentle receptivity and quiet nourishment.

### Page 12

This also explains why Daoist thought often praises softness over hardness. What is outwardly forceful is often fragile, while what is deeply gentle can endure for a long time. The strongest thing is not always the most conspicuous, but the most lasting.

### Page 13

“Continuous and seems to remain” means this foundation persists even when it is not visible. It is not a sudden burst, but a lasting return; not a temporary flourish, but a long-lived vitality.

### Page 14

“Use it without effort” teaches that when one follows the course of the Dao, there is no need to force everything through excessive striving. When things are allowed to unfold naturally, they last longer. Non-action does not mean doing nothing; it means not obstructing the natural order.

### Page 15

Thus this chapter is not only a cosmological image, but also a lesson for personal cultivation. If one wishes to stand firmly in the world, one should not be overly showy, but should preserve a deep, calm, and enduring inner root.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

This chapter emphasizes that the foundation of both the world and human life is not outward display, but the continued presence of an inner source. Anything that loses its root cannot endure for long.

### Page 17

True strength lies not in contention, but in fullness; true endurance lies not in constant consumption, but in keeping a quiet, gentle, and unbroken source alive.
