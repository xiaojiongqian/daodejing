# 第001-2章：帛书里的恒道

## 考据引入

### 第1页

1973 年，湖南长沙东郊，考古人员打开马王堆三号汉墓。

墓中没有宏大的宣告，只有封闭在湿暗里的漆器、帛画、兵书、医方，以及写在丝上的文字。两千多年的沉睡之后，一部早已被读熟的经典，忽然有了更早、更陌生的面孔。

### 第2页

出土帛书中有《老子》甲、乙两种抄本。

它们写在丝帛上，年代远早于我们熟悉的许多传世注本；字形、用字和篇序也不尽相同。它们没有宣布后来版本作废，只是把文本形成的过程摆到了读者眼前。

### 第3页

这些帛书不是“唯一原版”，也不能简单说成“最正确的老子”。

它们保存的是《老子》早期流传中的两个样本，不是作者亲笔，也不是所有异文的裁判。考据首先要求节制：我们可以比较现存证据，却不能用一次出土填满两千年间所有空白。

### 第4页

我们熟悉的开篇，在帛书里带着另一种字形与节奏出现。

同一句话放回抄本中，便不再只是悬空的格言。它经过抄写者的手，受当时用字和避讳影响，也承受丝帛破损与后人整理。我们今天读到的文本，本来就有一段物质的历史。

### 第5页

传世本第一章说：“道可道，非常道。”

帛书相应处常见更舒缓的句式：“道，可道也，非恒道也。”多出来的停顿和语气，像是把一句被后世读熟的格言，还原成一个人正在思考、正在开口的声音。

### 第6页

传世本说“常”，帛书多作“恒”。

这个异文首先是版本事实，不必急着判成哲学胜负。“恒”与“常”意义相近，却携带不同的时代痕迹；它们使读者意识到，自己背得最熟的那句话，也经过选择与传承。

### 第7页

帛书还常多“也”这样的语气。

“也”字使句读舒展，读起来更接近说话，而不是后世排定的一行警句。这种差别未必改变主旨，却会改变耳朵所听见的语气。

### 第8页

更奇妙的是，马王堆帛书多见“德经”在前，“道经”在后。

这说明《老子》早期并非只有我们今天熟悉的一种编排。篇序变了，许多句子仍可辨认；稳定与变化同时存在，比“原本从来如此”的想象更接近文本实际。

## 正文

### 第9页

在整理室里，残片按位置摊开，灯光不能太强，移动也必须谨慎。

研究者先辨一笔一画，再比对缺口、字距和同墓材料。决定一个字如何释读的，常不是灵光乍现，而是反复核对后仍保留疑问。

### 第10页

那些字没有像石刻一样硬。

丝帛会裂，墨迹会漫漶，残缺处也不会因为读者着急便自动补全。“道，可道也”看似熟悉，多出的语气词却让背诵停顿了一下。正是这一下停顿，使人重新看字，而不是只把记忆念过去。

### 第11页

读者原本以为自己已经知道第一章。

可是“常”旁边出现了“恒”，“道经”之前出现了“德经”。这些差异未必导向全新的哲学，却迫使读者区分三件事：纸上确有的字、后人的校订，以及自己习以为常的读法。

### 第12页

这时，第一章像是在解释自己。

第一章告诫人不要把可说之言当成恒常之道；帛书则让这份告诫落在文本自身。我们读到的每个定本都值得认真使用，却仍是传承链条中的一个版本，而不是不曾经历时间的声音。

### 第13页

版本差异也不必立刻被当成损坏。

有些异文来自避讳，有些可能是抄写、地域用字或整理方式造成；具体情况须逐字判断。它们让我们看见经典如何被保存，也看见保存从来不是机械复印。

### 第14页

如果只想抓住“唯一正确的字”，我们会把经典变成标本。

承认文字有来路，不等于所有读法都一样。恰恰相反，越知道材料残缺，就越要说明证据在哪里、推断到哪一步为止。不确定不是漂亮姿态，而是研究结论的一部分。

### 第15页

考据训练的是一种克制。

面对残字，研究者有时必须写下“待考”；面对通行已久的解释，也必须问它依据哪一种版本。这样的迟疑不会削弱经典，反而防止我们把自己的确信冒充成古人的原话。

## 回扣

### 第16页

“名可名，非常名”在这里变得更深。

“常”与“恒”的差别提醒我们：连第一章的关键字，也有具体的版本来历。考据所做的，是把能查明的部分查明，把不能确定的部分如实留下。如此，“不可尽言”才不至于成为逃避证据的借口。

### 第17页

帛书被妥善收起，释文仍在继续修订。

读者回到第一章时，熟悉的句子不再那么光滑：每个字都可以追问出处，每种解释也应交代限度。阅读由此变慢了一点，却更可靠。

# Chapter 001-2: The Enduring Dao in Silk

## Textual-Historical Opening

### Page 1

In 1973, archaeologists opened Tomb Three at Mawangdui, in the eastern suburbs of Changsha, Hunan.

Inside were no grand proclamations, but lacquerware, silk paintings, military texts, medical writings, and words written on silk. After more than two thousand years underground, a classic that many readers thought they knew returned with an earlier, stranger face.

### Page 2

Among the finds were two manuscript copies of the Laozi.

Written on silk, they long predate many of the commentarial editions familiar today, and their characters, wording, and order do not always agree. They do not invalidate later texts. They make the process of textual formation visible.

### Page 3

These manuscripts are not “the only original,” nor simply “the correct Laozi.”

They are two surviving witnesses to an early stage of transmission, not the author’s manuscript and not judges of every later variant. Textual study begins with restraint: surviving evidence can be compared, but a single discovery cannot fill every gap across two millennia.

### Page 4

The opening many readers know appears in the silk manuscripts with another written shape and rhythm.

Set back inside a manuscript, the sentence is no longer an abstract maxim. It passed through a scribe’s hand, local conventions of writing, political taboo, damage to silk, and modern reconstruction. The text we read has a material history.

### Page 5

The received text begins: “Dao ke dao, fei chang dao.”

The silk manuscripts often preserve a slower rhythm: “Dao, if it can be spoken as dao, is not the enduring Dao.” The added pauses make a line long memorized as doctrine feel again like a thought being formed in a human mouth.

### Page 6

The received text says chang, “constant.” The silk often has heng, “enduring.”

This is first of all a fact about versions, not a philosophical contest with a winner. Chang and heng are close in meaning but carry different historical traces. Together they remind us that even the sentence we know by heart arrived through selection and transmission.

### Page 7

The silk text also often carries particles like ye.

The particle loosens the cadence. The line sounds more like speech and less like an aphorism cut into stone. It may not alter the main claim, but it changes the tone heard by the reader.

### Page 8

Even more striking, the Mawangdui manuscripts often place the De chapters before the Dao chapters.

The early Laozi did not circulate in only the arrangement familiar today. The order changes, while many sentences remain recognizable. Stability and variation coexist, which is more useful than imagining that the book always looked exactly as it does now.

## Main Text

### Page 9

In a conservation room, fragments lie in their recorded positions. The light is controlled, and every movement must be cautious.

Researchers identify strokes, compare gaps and spacing, and consult other material from the tomb. A reading is often established not by revelation, but by repeated checking—and sometimes by leaving the question open.

### Page 10

The characters are not hard like stone.

Silk splits, ink fades, and a missing graph does not restore itself because a reader wants certainty. “Dao, if it can be daoed” looks familiar, yet the extra particle interrupts memorized cadence. That small interruption makes us look at the words again instead of merely reciting them.

### Page 11

The reader thought Chapter One was already known.

Then chang stands beside heng, and De before Dao. These differences may not produce an entirely new philosophy, but they force the reader to distinguish among what appears on the silk, what editors supply, and what habit expects.

### Page 12

At that moment, Chapter One seems to explain itself.

Chapter One warns against mistaking a statement for the enduring Dao. The manuscripts let that warning fall upon the text itself. Every received edition deserves careful use, but it remains one point in a chain of transmission, not a voice untouched by time.

### Page 13

Variant readings are not automatically damage.

Some may reflect taboo, some copying, regional usage, or editorial arrangement; each case must be judged on its own evidence. They show both how a classic was preserved and why preservation is never mechanical duplication.

### Page 14

If we hunt only for the one final word, we turn the classic into a specimen.

Admitting that words have histories does not mean that every reading is equal. The more fragmentary the evidence, the more precisely we must say where it begins and where inference takes over. Uncertainty is not a decorative pose; it belongs to the conclusion.

### Page 15

Philology trains restraint.

Before a damaged graph, a scholar may have to write “uncertain.” Before a long-established interpretation, the scholar must still ask which version supports it. Such hesitation does not weaken the classic; it prevents our confidence from masquerading as the ancient text.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

“The name that can be named is not the enduring name” becomes deeper here.

The difference between chang and heng reminds us that even the chapter’s key terms have particular textual histories. Philology establishes what can be established and leaves the rest honestly unsettled. “Beyond words” should never become an excuse for ignoring evidence.

### Page 17

The silk is stored again, while editions and readings continue to be revised.

When we return to Chapter One, its familiar surface is less smooth. Each word may be asked for its source, and each interpretation for its limits. Reading becomes slower, and more dependable.
