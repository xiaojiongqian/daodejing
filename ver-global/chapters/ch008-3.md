# 第008-3章：竞争之下，仍有一条共同的河

## 原文

### 第1页

上善若水。水善利万物而不争，处众人之所恶，故几于道。

居善地，心善渊，与善仁，言善信，正善治，事善能，动善时。

夫唯不争，故无尤。

——《道德经》第八章，河上公章题《易性》

### 第2页

天之道，利而不害；圣人之道，为而不争。

——《道德经》第八十一章

在一个以增长、排名和市场份额衡量成败的时代，“利万物而不争”很容易被听成一句不合时宜的安慰。企业若不争顾客，员工若不争机会，投资者若不争收益，经济又如何运转？

可老子并没有要求人停止作为。他说的是“为而不争”：事情要做，能力要用，价值要创造；只是别把一切价值都截留在自己这里，也别把每一种关系都变成胜负。

## 释义

### 第3页

世界贸易组织2026年3月发布的报告显示，到当年2月底，全球约百分之七十二的货物贸易仍依照最惠国关税进行。共同规则仍在承托大部分跨境贸易；与此同时，新增关税、产业政策、地缘政治与供应链安全，又使越来越多的闸门出现在河道上。

这不是一个纯粹自由贸易的时代，也不是全球交换已经停止的时代。货物、资本、知识与风险仍在跨境流动，只是流动所经过的边界更受审视。第八章因此显得格外贴近现实：经济既需要水的流动，也需要回答谁能开闸、谁被拦在下游，以及代价最终流向哪里。

### 第4页

竞争有真实的作用。两家店争取顾客，可能带来更好的产品、更低的价格和更周到的服务；两种方案彼此检验，也可能让错误更早暴露。若没有选择，懒惰与傲慢很容易躲在唯一的位置上。

但市场从来不是一群孤立的人在荒地上追逐。每一次交易都借用了共同条件：可以使用的货币，大体能够执行的契约，运送货物的道路，彼此承认的标准，以及陌生人不会在每个环节欺骗自己的最低信任。可持续、规模化的竞争，发生在这套合作河床之上。

### 第5页

“不争而胜”常被讲成一种更高明的商战：表面退让，暗中等待对手松懈，最后取得更大的胜利。若是如此，不争只是一层伪装，终点仍是占尽河道。

第八章的次序更严格：“利万物”在前，“不争”在后。先问产品是否解决了真实问题，交易是否让双方愿意再次相见，组织是否因你的加入而更有能力。竞争若不再服务于事情，只为了让别人失败，便会从发现价值的工具，变成吞噬价值的目的。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第6页

**利万物：** 不是要求企业无偿供给一切，而是把创造可用的价值放在索取回报之前。利润说明企业的收入超过了自身计入的成本，却不能单独证明社会净价值增加；市场势力与未被计价的代价，仍可能藏在利润之后。

**不争：** 不取消价格、能力与方案之间的竞争，而是不把压倒对方当作唯一目标。它反对无谓争夺、规则操纵，以及为了短期位置而损坏长期关系。

**无尤：** 不是永远不会受批评，更接近少造怨尤、少留下由争夺而来的过失。经济行为仍要接受事实、规则与后果的检验。

### 第7页

**价值创造：** 使产品、服务、知识或协作真正改善人的处境。它回答的是“增加了什么”。

**价值攫取：** 从经济活动形成的价值中取得收入。它本身并非错误；问题在于收入来自能力、投入与风险承担，还是主要来自控制别人无法绕开的关口。

**外部成本：** 生产或消费造成、却没有进入价格与决策，而由未获补偿的第三方承担的代价，例如污染、噪声、过度拥堵或难以修复的资源损耗。账面上的便宜，有时只是有人在账外付款。

## 画面描述

### 第8页

清晨的果蔬批发市场刚刚开门。两家相邻的摊位同时向采购者报出价格，谁都希望自己的货物先被运走。

市场公用冷库的警报忽然响起，卡住的库门正在漏出冷气。两位摊主放下账本，一起推紧库门，又把来不及入库的果箱搬上备用冷藏车。年轻的学徒问：“隔壁明天还会和我们争价，为什么现在要帮他？”

师傅扶稳最后一箱水果，说：“我们可以比谁选的货好，不能把整座市场烂掉当成胜利。”

冷库恢复运转，两位摊主回到各自的秤前，重新报出不同的价格。竞争并未消失，只是回到了它应在的位置。

## 正文

### 第9页

这座市场解释了一个常被遗忘的事实：竞争者也共享河床。餐馆会争晚餐的客人，却共同依赖食品安全；银行会争存款，却共同依赖货币信用；科技公司会争用户，却共同依赖教育体系培养的人才、公共研究留下的知识与稳定运转的基础网络。

物流、支付、保险、标准与基础软件尤其具有“水性”。它们常退居后台，不亲自生产货架上的大多数商品，却让无数商品得以流动。若这些基础环节只利用瓶颈抽取价值，而不再维护通道，整条经济水系都会变得迟缓而昂贵。

### 第10页

水往低处走，经济中的创新也常从被忽略之处开始：大企业不愿服务的小客户，利润不高却不可缺少的维修，手续繁琐的基础工作，以及长期没人认真听取的具体需要。这里可以看见“处众人之所恶”，但不能把低处浪漫化。低利润、低工资和高风险本身并不是美德。

水的力量还在于绕行、渗透与汇聚。企业可以避开对手最坚固的地方，寻找替代技术和新渠道；也可以依靠可靠体验与口碑，逐渐进入日常生活；微小而稳定的现金流、知识与信任，则可能经过时间汇成规模。关键不在于姿态柔软，而在于这些流向是否仍能“利物”。

### 第11页

水随器成形，却没有失去水性。企业当然可以改变产品、渠道、价格和组织方式；若连信用、品质与责任也随环境改变，那不是柔韧，而是机会主义。

“言善信”，是让报价、交付和合同可以相信。“正善治”，是让规则不因参与者强弱而随意改变。“事善能”，拒绝用善意掩盖产品与管理上的无能。“动善时”，则要求投资与扩张尊重需求、资金和人的承受能力，不把一时热度错认成长久趋势。方法可以变，承诺不能每天换一套含义。

### 第12页

现代企业最容易混淆的，是价值创造与价值攫取。一家公司改善产品，顾客愿意购买，它可能由此增加收入；但这仍不足以单独证明社会净价值增加，还要看成本是否被转嫁、选择是否真实存在。另一家公司让退出变得困难，把价格藏在复杂条款里，或借不可替代的位置不断加收费用，也可能获得增长，却没有创造相等的新价值。

无限增长在这里显出危险。规模扩大可以降低成本、支持研发，也可以让服务抵达更多人；可当季度数字挤干维护、员工恢复与供应商生存的余地，增长便开始消耗自己的河床。老子警惕的不是成就本身，而是“满”以后仍不肯辨认限度。

### 第13页

平台经济把这个问题放得更大。平台起初像河流，把原本难以相遇的买家与卖家连接起来；参与者越多，连接的价值也可能越高。但当人们难以绕开平台，或迁移数据、信誉和客户的成本过高，平台便可能从服务交易转向支配交易：规则可以被单方面、低透明度地改变，费用上涨也很难回避。

规模本身不是过错。“正善治”却不能只靠经营者自我克制，还需要透明规则、可申诉的程序与真实存在的选择。法律、合同、竞争政策、环境约束与社会保障，可以理解为河床：它们不必指挥每一滴水怎样运动，却要防止掌握闸门的人把河道据为己有。

### 第14页

价格还会遮住低处。我们买到一件异常便宜的商品，眼前只出现付款数字；没有出现在数字里的，可能是被压缩的休息时间、排入河流的废水、无法承担风险的小供应商，或留给下一代修复的土地。

战争倾向于把对方视为必须削弱的敌人，市场却不能照搬这种逻辑。竞争者、供应商、客户、劳动者乃至监管者，共同组成企业赖以生存的水系。若把军事上的灵活与瓦解原封不动地带进商业，只会得到更灵活的掠夺者；加入“善利万物而不争”，策略才开始受到关系与后果的约束。

### 第15页

亚当·斯密常被简化成一句关于自利能够推动市场的话。然而在《道德情操论》中，他从同情、判断与他人的目光出发讨论道德生活。他的经济思想并不要求我们相信，贪婪本身足以维持一个社会。

这与老子并非同一种理论。斯密关心分工、交换与商业社会；老子从水的性情出发，警惕争夺对人的塑形。但两者都让我们看见：个人利益只有进入可信的关系与规则，才可能形成较稳定的公共秩序。市场可以协调分散的计划、信息与资源，却不能凭空制造它所依赖的节制、公正与信用。

## 回扣

### 第16页

老子没有提供定价公式，也没有替现代社会写下公司法、劳动法或竞争政策。“不争”不能代替监管，“仁”不能代替合同，“信”也不能要求较弱的一方仅凭善意承担风险。无为更不等于放任强者随意改变河道。

第八章能提供的，是对经济目的的一次校准。竞争应帮助人们发现更好的办法，而不是帮助强者逃避后果；制度应让利润更多来自创新、承担风险和创造价值，更少来自外部化成本与控制瓶颈；增长也应扩大系统承载生活的能力，而不是只扩大中心抽取的速度。

### 第17页

市场像水，需要流动；也像河，需要边界。完全没有竞争，市场可能失去改进的压力；只剩竞争，参与者又可能挖空共同的河床。

一家值得长久存在的企业，不必假装自己没有利益。它只需更诚实地理解利益从哪里来：顾客多年以后仍愿意回来，员工没有被增长悄悄耗尽，供应商还能继续改进，环境负债也没有被送给陌生人和下一代。

“利万物而不争”没有要求经济离开现实。它只是为现实增加一个更长的尺度：你所获得的力量，究竟来自控制别人的流向，还是来自让更多人仍有能力继续流动？

# Chapter 008-3: Beneath Competition, a Common River Still Flows

## Original Text

### Page 1

The highest good is like water. Water benefits the ten thousand things without contending. It remains in places that people dislike, and so it comes close to Dao.

In dwelling, be good at choosing the ground. In mind, be deep. In giving, be humane. In speech, be trustworthy. In governing, bring clarity and evenness. In work, be capable. In movement, be timely.

Because water does not contend, it is free from blame.

— *Dao De Jing*, Chapter 8; Heshang Gong’s chapter title, “Changing One’s Nature”

### Page 2

The Way of Heaven benefits and does not harm. The way of the sage acts and does not contend.

— *Dao De Jing*, Chapter 81

In an age that measures success through growth, rankings, and market share, “benefiting the ten thousand things without contending” can sound like consolation from a less competitive world. If companies do not contend for customers, employees for opportunity, and investors for returns, how could an economy move?

Laozi does not ask us to stop acting. He speaks of acting without contending. Work must be done, ability exercised, and value created. What need not follow is the attempt to retain all value at the centre or turn every relationship into victory and defeat.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

The World Trade Organization reported in March 2026 that, by the end of February, about 72 percent of global merchandise trade was still conducted under most-favoured-nation tariffs. Common rules continue to support most cross-border trade. At the same time, new tariffs, industrial policies, geopolitics, and concern for supply-chain security have placed more gates across the channel.

This is neither an age of pure free trade nor one in which global exchange has ended. Goods, capital, knowledge, and risk still cross borders, while the boundaries governing their movement receive closer scrutiny. Chapter Eight therefore feels unusually close to the present. An economy needs the movement of water, but must also ask who controls the gate, who is held downstream, and where the costs finally settle.

### Page 4

Competition serves a real purpose. Two shops seeking the same customers may produce better goods, lower prices, and more attentive service. Rival proposals may expose an error earlier. Without choice, idleness and arrogance can shelter comfortably inside the only available position.

Yet a market has never been a collection of isolated people chasing one another across empty land. Every transaction borrows common conditions: usable money, contracts that can generally be enforced, roads that deliver goods, shared standards, and a minimum of trust that strangers will not deceive one another at every stage. Sustainable competition at scale takes place upon this cooperative riverbed.

### Page 5

“Winning without contending” is often presented as a subtler form of commercial warfare: yield in public, wait for the rival to relax, then claim a larger victory. Understood this way, non-contention is merely camouflage, and the final aim is still to occupy the whole river.

Chapter Eight follows a more demanding order. “Benefiting the ten thousand things” comes before “not contending.” Ask first whether a product resolves a real problem, whether both parties would willingly meet again, and whether an organisation becomes more capable through your participation. When competition ceases to serve the work and exists only to make someone else lose, it changes from a tool for discovering value into an end that consumes value.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 6

**Benefiting the ten thousand things:** This does not require companies to provide everything without payment. It places the creation of usable value before the collection of returns. Profit shows that revenue exceeds the costs recorded by the company, but cannot by itself prove an increase in net social value. Market power and unpriced costs may still stand behind it.

**Non-contention:** This does not abolish competition in price, ability, or design. It refuses to make the defeat of others the only aim. It resists needless rivalry, manipulation of rules, and damage to long relationships for the sake of short-term position.

**Free from blame:** This does not mean becoming immune to criticism. It means creating fewer resentments and fewer errors born from needless struggle. Economic conduct remains answerable to facts, rules, and consequences.

### Page 7

**Value creation:** Making a product, service, body of knowledge, or collaboration genuinely improve a human situation. It asks what has been added.

**Value capture:** Receiving income from value formed through economic activity. This is not wrong in itself. The question is whether income follows ability, investment, and risk-bearing, or mainly the control of a gate that others cannot avoid.

**External cost:** A cost created through production or consumption that does not enter the price or decision and is borne by an uncompensated third party, such as pollution, noise, congestion, or damage to resources that are difficult to restore. What looks cheap on the receipt may mean that someone else is paying beyond it.

## Visual Description

### Page 8

An early-morning produce market is opening. Two neighbouring stalls quote prices to the same buyers, and each hopes its goods will leave first.

An alarm sounds from the market’s shared cold room. The jammed door is leaking cold air. Both stallholders put down their account books, force the door shut together, and move the remaining crates into a backup refrigerated truck. A young apprentice asks, “They will bargain against us again tomorrow. Why are we helping them now?”

“We can compete over who selects better produce,” the stallholder says, steadying the last crate. “We cannot call it victory when the whole market rots.”

When the cold room is working again, both stallholders return to their scales and quote different prices. Competition has not disappeared. It has returned to its proper place.

## Main Text

### Page 9

The market reveals a fact that economies often forget: competitors share a riverbed. Restaurants compete for dinner guests but rely together on food safety. Banks compete for deposits but rely together on confidence in money. Technology companies compete for users but rely together on educated workers, knowledge inherited from public research, and stable infrastructure.

Logistics, payments, insurance, standards, and foundational software are especially water-like. They often remain in the background. They do not make most of the goods on the shelf, yet allow innumerable goods to move. When these foundations use bottlenecks only to extract value and cease maintaining the channel, the whole economic water system becomes slower and more expensive.

### Page 10

Water moves downward, and economic innovation often begins in neglected places: small customers that large companies do not wish to serve, low-margin but indispensable repairs, burdensome maintenance, and specific needs that no one has listened to carefully. Here we can hear an echo of “remaining where people dislike,” but the low place must not be romanticised. Low profit, low wages, and high risk are not virtues in themselves.

Water also works by going around, seeping through, and gathering. A company may avoid a rival’s strongest position by finding an alternative technology or channel. It may enter daily life gradually through dependable experience and reputation. Small but steady flows of cash, knowledge, and trust may gather into scale. The essential question is not whether the posture looks soft, but whether these flows continue to benefit life.

### Page 11

Water takes the shape of a vessel without losing its nature. A company may change its product, channel, price, or organisation. If credibility, quality, and responsibility change whenever the environment changes, this is not flexibility but opportunism.

Trustworthy speech makes quotations, deliveries, and contracts dependable. Good governance keeps rules from changing arbitrarily with the strength of a participant. Capable work refuses to disguise failures of product or management as goodwill. Timely action asks investment and expansion to respect demand, finance, and human capacity rather than mistake a passing enthusiasm for an enduring trend. Methods may change; promises cannot acquire a new meaning every day.

### Page 12

Modern companies easily confuse value creation with value capture. A company improves a product, and customers willingly buy it; revenue may rise as a result. Yet this alone does not prove an increase in net social value. We must still ask whether costs were shifted elsewhere and whether choice was real. Another company makes departure difficult, hides charges inside complicated terms, or uses an unavoidable position to add fees. It too may grow without creating equivalent new value.

The danger of endless growth appears here. Greater scale can lower costs, support research, and bring a service to more people. But when quarterly numbers consume maintenance, human recovery, and the ability of suppliers to survive, growth begins to erode its own riverbed. Laozi does not warn against achievement itself. He warns against the refusal to recognise a limit after fullness has arrived.

### Page 13

Platform economies enlarge the problem. A platform begins like a river, joining buyers and sellers who once struggled to meet. As participation grows, connection may become more valuable. But when people find the platform difficult to avoid, or face high costs in moving data, reputation, and customers, it can move from serving exchange to governing it. Rules may change unilaterally and with little transparency, while higher fees become difficult to escape.

Scale is not itself a fault. Yet good governance cannot depend on the operator’s restraint alone. It also requires transparent rules, procedures for appeal, and choices that exist in practice. Law, contracts, competition policy, environmental limits, and social protection can be understood as a riverbed. They need not direct every drop, but they must prevent whoever controls the gates from claiming the whole channel.

### Page 14

Prices can conceal the low ground. When we buy an unusually cheap product, the number paid is visible. What may remain invisible is compressed rest, wastewater entering a river, a small supplier unable to bear another risk, or land that the next generation must repair.

War tends to treat the other side as an enemy to be weakened. A market cannot simply copy that logic. Competitors, suppliers, customers, workers, and regulators form the water system upon which a company depends. If military flexibility and disruption are carried unchanged into commerce, they produce only a more agile form of extraction. Only when “benefiting the ten thousand things without contending” is restored do strategy and power become answerable to relationship and consequence.

### Page 15

Adam Smith is often reduced to a sentence about self-interest moving markets. Yet in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments*, he examines moral life through sympathy, judgment, and our awareness of other people. His economic thought does not require us to believe that greed alone can sustain a society.

This is not the same theory as Laozi’s. Smith studies division of labour, exchange, and commercial society; Laozi begins with the disposition of water and the way contention shapes human beings. Yet both help us see that private interest becomes socially durable only within trustworthy relationships and rules. Markets can coordinate dispersed plans, information, and resources, but they cannot manufacture from nothing the restraint, justice, and confidence upon which they depend.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

Laozi offers no pricing formula, company law, labour code, or competition policy. Non-contention cannot replace regulation, humanity cannot replace a contract, and trust cannot require the weaker party to carry risk on goodwill alone. Non-action does not mean allowing the powerful to redraw the river at will.

What Chapter Eight offers is a recalibration of economic purpose. Competition should help discover better ways, not help the powerful escape consequences. Institutions should make profit arise more from innovation, risk-bearing, and value creation, and less from externalised costs or control of bottlenecks. Growth should enlarge the system’s capacity to sustain life, not merely increase the centre’s rate of extraction.

### Page 17

A market resembles water because it needs to move. It resembles a river because it needs boundaries. Without competition, a market may lose pressure to improve. With nothing but competition, participants may dig away their common riverbed.

A company worthy of lasting need not pretend to have no interests. It needs only to understand more honestly where its interests come from: customers who still return years later, workers not quietly exhausted by growth, suppliers that remain able to improve, and environmental liabilities not sent to strangers or the next generation.

“Benefiting the ten thousand things without contending” does not ask economics to leave reality. It gives reality a longer measure: does power come from controlling where others may flow, or from enabling more people to retain the capacity to keep moving?
