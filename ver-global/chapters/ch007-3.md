# 第007-3章：公与私之间，谁成全了谁？

## 原文

### 第1页
天长地久。天地所以能长且久者，以其不自生，故能长生。

是以圣人后其身而身先，外其身而身存。

非以其无私邪？故能成其私。

——《道德经》第七章《韬光》

### 第2页
知常容，容乃公，公乃王，王乃天，天乃道，道乃久。

圣人不积，既以为人己愈有，既以与人己愈多。

——《道德经》第十六章、第八十一章

老子没有把“公”写成对“私”的处决。他更像是在追问：怎样的公共秩序，能够使个人不必靠侵夺他人来完成自己？

## 释义

### 第3页
第七章其实没有出现“公”字。它只说“无私”，继而说“成其私”。这两个短句若被匆匆道德化，就会变成一个可疑的劝告：你先牺牲自己，命运以后会奖励你。

但老子不是在兜售延迟兑现的报酬。他把“私”拆成了两个层次：一种私，把万物变成自己的工具；另一种私，是每个具体生命都需要保存、安顿和完成的自身。前者必须松开，后者才有可能长久。

### 第4页
在这一章里，“私”至少承担着三层意味。第一层是“身”：我的安全、名声、位置和寿命。第二层是“偏”：只从我的角度分配代价，让别人承担我不愿承担的部分。第三层则是“其私”：这个人不可替代的生活，他的身体、亲情、志趣与归宿。

老子警惕的是前两层无限膨胀，却没有消灭第三层。圣人“外其身”，最后不是无身；圣人“无私”，最后不是无所成。句末那个“私”仍在，只是已经不再以世界为燃料。

### 第5页
因此，“成其私”不是揭穿无私背后的精明算计，而是改变个人幸福的生成路径。一个人若直接把自己的安全置于所有决定之上，周围的人就会变成盾牌；若把声望置于一切合作之上，同伴就会变成观众。这样的自我越被保护，越显得孤立而脆弱。

另一种自我则从关系中长出来。它不否认需要，也不羞于接受照料；只是它明白，自己的生命依赖共同的空气、制度、语言与信任。它所完成的“私”，是一种不必吞没公共世界的个人生活。

### 第6页
那么，“公”是什么？第十六章说：“知常容，容乃公。”公首先不是把所有人压成同一种形状，而是一种能够容纳差异、不被单一私欲劫持的开阔。它近于公正、共同与不偏，却不能被简单等同于现代意义上的公有制。

一条干净的河、一座人人可进的图书馆、一套不因身份而改变的程序，都是“公”的现代形态。它们不要求人们放弃自己的房间、家庭和梦想；恰恰因为共同条件没有被少数人占尽，每个人才更可能拥有一段真正属于自己的生活。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第7页
**无私：** 王弼解释为“无为于身”，即不围绕自身刻意运作；河上公则强调“公正无私”。前者偏向存在方式，后者偏向政治德性，两种读法都指向同一点：掌握中心的人不能只替中心谋利。

**成其私：** 不是取消个人利益，而是使个人的正当存续成为公共关系运行后的结果。它是一种间接成全，不是一笔伪装成奉献的投资。

**公：** 在老子的语境中，更接近包容、不偏和通达。它所反对的不是个体差异，而是任何局部把自己冒充成整体。

### 第8页
**“我为人人，人人为我”：** 这是一句流传于合作与互助语境的通俗表达。若谈共产主义理想，马克思更准确的表述是“各尽所能，按需分配”，以及“每个人的自由发展是一切人的自由发展的条件”。

**共同体：** 不是一群人使用同一个口号，而是成员共同承担维持生活条件的责任，也拥有影响规则的声音。

**非支配：** 现代共和主义用这一概念描述不受任意权力摆布的自由。它与老子的“后其身”可以对谈：公职若沦为掌权者的私产，即使他暂时仁慈，人民仍处在他的意志之下。

## 正文

### 第9页
暴雨过后的清晨，一栋公寓停了电，水泵也不再工作。有人把储水搬到楼道，有人照看独居老人；懂电路的住户打开配电箱，孩子们把手电筒放在台阶上，楼下餐馆则把可能变坏的食物煮成一锅热汤。

这时，“我为人人，人人为我”不再是一句标语。每个人拿出自己能够拿出的东西，也从别人那里得到自己所缺少的东西。公共并不悬在众人头顶；它就在一双双手之间，像临时接通的电路，使每一户私人的灯重新有了亮起的可能。

### 第10页
这一愿景与共产主义最深的共鸣，在于拒绝把个人理解为一座自给自足的岛。马克思所设想的更高社会，不只是把财富重新切分，而是改变人与劳动、人与他人的关系：劳动不再只是谋生的强迫，个人能力得以充分发展，合作创造的财富也足以回应真实需要。

“每个人的自由发展是一切人的自由发展的条件”，这句话没有把个体献祭给集体。相反，它要求共同体以每一个人的展开为检验。若所谓公共事业只能靠一部分人永久沉默、贫困或服从来维持，它便还没有抵达自己的理想。

### 第11页
然而，老子不是一位提前出现的马克思。马克思从生产关系、阶级、所有制与劳动分工追问不平等怎样被制造，并把人的解放寄托于有意识的集体变革。老子更常追踪另一条因果链：统治者的欲望如何制造竞争，名位如何扰乱人心，繁苛的干预如何使社会失去自生能力。

一条道路希望重建生产与分配的制度，一条道路不断削减占有与支配的冲动；前者强调共同创造历史，后者怀疑任何自称能够替天下安排一切的意志。它们都反对少数人把共同世界据为己有，却对政治行动的规模、速度与主体抱有不同信心。

### 第12页
“我为人人，人人为我”保存着一个优美的循环，也隐藏着一种危险的账本：我今日为你，是不是为了确保你明日为我？若付出必须得到等额偿还，互助就仍是一场延迟结算的交易。

值得注意的是，马克思所谓“按需分配”本来也超出了等价交换：能力不同，贡献不会相同；需要不同，所得也不必相等。老子同样把账本放下，却走了另一条路。他没有建立一套需要理论，而是让行动不再被“我能得到什么”预先统治。两者都越过精密的报偿，只是一个诉诸共同生产的富足，一个诉诸不争而生的秩序。

### 第13页
第七章背后确实有一幅政治图景。首先，权位不能成为占据者身体的延长。所谓“后其身”，就是让统治者的名声、家族和安全退到公共职责之后；所谓“外其身”，就是不拿国家去加固一个人的不朽幻觉。

老子又说，圣人“以百姓心为心”；最好的统治者功成事遂，百姓却说“我自然”。这不是人民感谢英明领袖的画面，而是领导者逐渐从成果中隐去。政治最好的完成，或许是人们仍能把生活过成自己的，而不是永远把它当作某个伟大意志的作品。

### 第14页
这幅图景也有经济内容。老子说，人民饥饿，是因为在上者征取太多；又说天之道像拉弓，把高处压低，把低处举起，“损有余而补不足”。这还不是现代财政学，更不是一套完整的产权制度，却包含了清楚的方向感：中心不能靠抽干边缘来证明自己的繁荣。

因此，老子的“无私政治”至少要求三种克制：少把公共财富变成统治者的纪念物，少用奢侈标定社会的成功，少让普通人替权力的焦虑和荣耀买单。国家的富有若只表现为宫殿更高、仪式更盛、统计更漂亮，而百姓必须耗尽生命去维持它，那不是“公”，只是被放大了的私。

### 第15页
《道德经》第八十章的“小国寡民”，可以被看作老子的政治极限图景：规模不大，战争与远徙很少，食物、居所和习俗足以使人安顿。它珍视的是生活的可把握感——决定不在遥不可及之处，人的时间不被无休止的动员征用，欲望也不必由帝国的比较来点燃。

但我们不必把它改写成一份反技术、反旅行的政策纲领。它更像一道针对巨型权力的追问：社会是否大到无人能够影响规则，快到所有人只能追赶，强到每一代都必须为它献出安宁？老子的尺度不是人口数字，而是人还能否感到自己的生活属于自己。

### 第16页
老子的政治也有必须正视的阴影。谁来判断哪些欲望是自然的，哪些技术是不必要的，怎样的生活才算朴素？如果答案永远由统治者给出，“使民无知无欲”就可能从减少操控，滑向替人民决定他们应该知道什么、想要什么。

花园的安静与一间不许说话的房间，听起来同样没有噪音，政治意义却完全相反。把老子带进现代社会，不能只要“清静”，还需要权利、透明、异议和可更换的权力。真正的无为不是让人民失去行动能力，而是让权力停止垄断行动的资格。

### 第17页
现代公共治理还告诉我们，“公”不只等于国家，“私”也不必等于贪婪。埃莉诺·奥斯特罗姆研究过牧场、森林、渔场和灌溉水系：许多共同资源既没有被彻底私有化，也没有全由中央命令管理，而是由使用者共同制定边界、监督与纠错规则。

想象一条穿过村庄的水渠。每块田属于不同家庭，水却无法只属于上游。若上游取尽，下游便荒芜；若无人清淤，所有田地都会干裂。共同规则不是私人的敌人，而是每一块私人田地得以继续生长的河床。

### 第18页
老子理想中的个体，也不是没有需要、没有边界的奉献机器。他更像一个不再把自我当作绝对中心的人：能够说“这是我的责任”，却不说“一切功劳都是我的”；能够承认“我需要休息”，却不把别人的疲惫当作理所当然。

这样的人成长得更具体，而不是更模糊。他知道自己喜欢什么，也知道喜好不是天下的法律；他愿意接受共同体的帮助，也不因此把共同体当作永不枯竭的供应者。“成其私”最终成全的，是一个既有轮廓、又有窗户的自我。

## 回扣

### 第19页
在职场里，公与私的界线常被“使命”弄得模糊：领导者把个人野心称为团队愿景，员工的时间便成了可无限征用的公共资源。在家庭里，父母也可能把控制称作爱，把孩子的人生当作自己未竟愿望的续篇。

第七章给出的准则并非一味退让，而是检视代价的流向：谁获得名字，谁承担疲惫？谁决定共同目标，谁没有说不的权利？“无私”首先应当约束占据中心的人，而不是要求处在边缘的人继续牺牲。

### 第20页
公与私之间真正危险的，不是边界本身，而是任何一方吞没另一方。私人占有公共，政治便成为家产；公共吞没个人，共同体便成为祭坛。从现代政治经验出发，我们可以借这一章同时警惕这两种结局：它先让自我退出中心，又在最后把一个可以完成的自我还给我们。

一所好房子既有共同的屋顶，也有可以关上的房门。屋顶使每个房间免受风雨，房门则使共同生活不至于成为彼此侵入。也许这就是“无私而成其私”的政治含义：让共同世界不属于任何一个人，从而使其中的每一个人，都能真正拥有自己。

# Chapter 007-3: Between the Common and the Private—Who Completes Whom?

## Original Text

### Page 1
Heaven is enduring; Earth is lasting.

Heaven and Earth endure because they do not live for themselves. Therefore, they can go on living.

Thus the sage places himself behind, yet finds himself in front; puts himself outside consideration, yet remains secure.

Is it not because he is without private designs that what is truly his can be fulfilled?

— *Dao De Jing*, Chapter 7, “Concealing the Light”

### Page 2
Knowing constancy, one becomes capacious. Being capacious, one becomes impartial; being impartial, one becomes fit to lead; being fit to lead, one accords with Heaven, Dao, and duration.

The sage does not hoard. The more he does for others, the more he has; the more he gives to others, the more abundant he becomes.

— *Dao De Jing*, Chapters 16 and 81

Laozi does not make the common a sentence passed upon the private. He asks a subtler question: What kind of public order allows people to fulfill their own lives without taking the lives of others as raw material?

## Interpretation

### Page 3
The word *gong*—the common, public, or impartial—does not actually appear in Chapter 7. The chapter says “without private designs” and then “fulfills what is his.” Read too quickly as a moral lesson, this becomes dubious advice: sacrifice yourself now, and fate will reward you later.

Laozi is not selling a reward on deferred payment. He separates two senses of the private. One turns the world into an instrument of the self. The other is the particular life that every person must preserve, inhabit, and complete. The first must loosen before the second can endure.

### Page 4
In this chapter, the private has at least three layers. First comes the body: my safety, reputation, position, and lifespan. Second comes partiality: assigning costs from my point of view alone, so that others carry what I refuse to bear. Third comes what is genuinely one's own: an irreplaceable life, with its body, attachments, interests, and destination.

Laozi warns against the limitless expansion of the first two, but he does not abolish the third. The sage puts the body outside consideration, yet does not become bodiless. He abandons private designs, yet something of his is fulfilled. The self remains at the end of the sentence, but it no longer burns the world as fuel.

### Page 5
“Fulfilling what is his” does not expose selflessness as sophisticated calculation. It changes the path by which personal well-being arises. When someone places personal safety above every decision, other people become shields. When reputation stands above every collaboration, colleagues become an audience. The more heavily such a self is guarded, the more isolated and fragile it becomes.

Another kind of self grows from relationship. It does not deny need or feel ashamed of receiving care. It understands that its life depends upon shared air, institutions, language, and trust. What it fulfills is a private life that does not have to consume the common world.

### Page 6
What, then, is the common? Chapter 16 says, “Being capacious, one becomes impartial.” *Gong* begins not by pressing everyone into one shape, but as an openness able to contain difference without being captured by a single appetite. It approaches the meanings of impartiality and commonality, but it cannot simply be equated with modern public ownership.

A clean river, a library open to all, and a procedure that does not change with social status are modern forms of the common. None requires people to abandon their rooms, families, or aspirations. Precisely because shared conditions have not been exhausted by the few, more people can possess lives that are truly their own.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 7
**Without private designs:** Wang Bi reads this as not acting deliberately on behalf of the body; Heshang Gong emphasizes public-minded impartiality. The first concerns a mode of being, the second a political virtue. Both imply that whoever occupies the center must not make the center serve only itself.

**Fulfilling what is one's own:** This does not abolish individual benefit. It allows legitimate personal flourishing to arise from well-ordered public relationships. It is indirect fulfillment, not an investment disguised as generosity.

**The common or impartial:** In Laozi's context, *gong* suggests capaciousness, openness, and freedom from partiality. Its opposite is not individual difference, but any part masquerading as the whole.

### Page 8
**“One for all, all for one”:** This is a popular expression of cooperation and mutual aid. Marx's own formulations of the communist horizon are more exact: “From each according to his ability, to each according to his needs,” and “the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all.”

**Community:** Not a population repeating the same slogan, but members who share responsibility for sustaining the conditions of life and possess a voice in shaping the rules.

**Non-domination:** Modern republicanism uses this term for freedom from arbitrary power. It can converse with Laozi's “placing oneself behind”: when public office becomes the private possession of its holder, people remain subject to that will even when it happens to be benevolent.

## Main Text

### Page 9
On the morning after a violent storm, an apartment building has lost power, and the water pumps have stopped. Someone carries stored water into the corridor; someone checks on an older neighbor living alone. A resident who understands electricity opens the service panel, children place flashlights along the stairs, and the restaurant downstairs turns food that may spoil into a pot of hot soup.

Here, “one for all, all for one” is no longer a slogan. Each person offers what they can and receives what they lack. The public does not float above the residents. It runs between their hands like a temporary circuit, making it possible for the private light in every home to come on again.

### Page 10
This vision resonates most deeply with communism in its refusal to imagine the person as a self-sufficient island. Marx's higher society would do more than divide wealth differently. It would transform our relation to work and to one another: labor would cease to be merely an imposed means of survival, individual capacities could develop broadly, and cooperative abundance could answer real needs.

“The free development of each is the condition for the free development of all” does not sacrifice the individual to the collective. It asks the collective to be judged by the unfolding of every person. If a public undertaking can survive only through the permanent silence, poverty, or obedience of some, it has not yet reached its own ideal.

### Page 11
Laozi, however, is not Marx born too early. Marx asks how inequality is produced through property relations, class, and the division of labor, and places hope in conscious collective transformation. Laozi more often follows another causal chain: how the ruler's appetite produces competition, how rank disturbs the heart, and how elaborate intervention destroys society's capacity to generate order from within.

One path seeks to reconstruct the institutions of production and distribution; the other repeatedly subtracts the impulses to possess and command. One emphasizes our power to make history together; the other distrusts any will that claims to arrange everything under Heaven. Both oppose the capture of a shared world by the few, but they place different confidence in the scale, speed, and agents of political action.

### Page 12
“One for all, all for one” contains a beautiful circle, but it can also conceal a ledger: Do I serve you today in order to guarantee that you serve me tomorrow? If every gift demands an equivalent return, mutual aid remains a transaction with delayed settlement.

Marx's principle of distribution according to need already moves beyond equivalence: abilities differ, so contributions will not be equal; needs differ, so receipts need not be equal. Laozi also puts down the ledger, but by another route. He offers no theory of needs; he asks action not to be ruled in advance by the question, What will I get? Both move beyond exact repayment—one through cooperative abundance, the other through an order that grows without contention.

### Page 13
There is indeed a political landscape behind Chapter 7. First, office must not become an extension of its holder's body. To “place oneself behind” is to put the ruler's reputation, dynasty, and safety behind public responsibility. To “put oneself outside” is to refuse the use of a state to fortify one person's fantasy of immortality.

Elsewhere Laozi says that the sage takes the people's hearts as his own. Of the best ruler, once the work is complete, the people say, “We did it naturally, by ourselves.” This is not a crowd thanking a brilliant leader. The leader has begun to disappear from the achievement. Politics may be most complete when people can still experience their lives as their own, rather than as the artwork of a grand will.

### Page 14
This landscape has economic content as well. Laozi says that people go hungry because those above take too much in taxes. He compares the Way of Heaven to drawing a bow: what is high is lowered, what is low is raised; excess is diminished and deficiency repaired. This is not yet modern public finance or a comprehensive theory of property, but its direction is clear: the center must not prove its prosperity by draining the margins.

Laozi's politics without private designs therefore demands at least three restraints: do not turn public wealth into monuments to rulers; do not let luxury define social success; do not make ordinary people pay for power's anxiety and glory. If a state's wealth consists of taller palaces, grander ceremonies, and finer statistics while its people exhaust their lives maintaining them, this is not the common good. It is private appetite magnified.

### Page 15
The “small state with few people” of Chapter 80 can be read as Laozi's limiting political image: modest scale, little warfare or forced movement, and food, shelter, and custom sufficient to make life inhabitable. What it cherishes is a graspable life—decisions not infinitely distant, human time not continually requisitioned by mobilization, desire not endlessly inflamed by imperial comparison.

We need not turn this image into an anti-technology, anti-travel policy manual. It functions more fruitfully as a question addressed to gigantic power: Has society become too large for anyone to influence its rules, too fast for anyone to do more than chase it, too strong to let a generation keep its peace? Laozi's true scale is not a population count, but whether people can still feel that their lives belong to them.

### Page 16
Laozi's politics also contains a shadow that must be faced. Who decides which desires are natural, which technologies unnecessary, and which lives properly simple? If the ruler always supplies the answer, reducing manipulation can slide into deciding on behalf of the people what they should know and want.

The quiet of a garden and the quiet of a room in which no one may speak are equally free of noise, but politically they are opposites. A modern encounter with Laozi cannot ask only for stillness; it also needs rights, transparency, dissent, and replaceable power. Genuine *wuwei* does not deprive people of agency. It stops power from monopolizing the right to act.

### Page 17
Modern public governance also shows that the common is not identical with the state, and the private need not mean greed. Elinor Ostrom studied pastures, forests, fisheries, and irrigation systems. Many shared resources were neither fully privatized nor directed entirely from the center; users made rules together about boundaries, monitoring, and correction.

Imagine a channel running through a village. Each field belongs to a different family, but the water cannot belong only to those upstream. If the first farmers take everything, the lower fields become dust; if no one clears the channel, every field cracks. Common rules are not the enemy of private fields. They are the riverbed that lets each field continue to grow.

### Page 18
Laozi's ideal individual is not a machine of service without needs or boundaries. Such a person no longer treats the self as the absolute center: they can say, “This is my responsibility,” without saying, “All the credit is mine”; they can admit, “I need rest,” without treating another person's exhaustion as natural.

This person becomes more distinct, not less. They know what they love, while knowing that preference is not a law for the world. They receive help from the community without imagining the community as an inexhaustible provider. What Chapter 7 finally fulfills is a self with contours—and with windows.

### Page 19
At work, the line between public and private is often blurred by the language of mission. A leader calls personal ambition the team's vision, and employees' time becomes a common resource available for unlimited extraction. In families, parents may call control love and turn a child's future into a continuation of their own unfinished wishes.

Chapter 7 does not recommend endless yielding. It asks us to trace the direction in which costs flow. Who receives the name, and who carries the exhaustion? Who defines the common purpose, and who lacks the right to refuse? “Without private designs” should first restrain those who occupy the center, not demand further sacrifice from those already at the edge.

### Page 20
The danger between the common and the private is not the boundary itself, but either side swallowing the other. When the private captures the common, politics becomes household property. When the common consumes the person, community becomes an altar. Read through modern political experience, the chapter helps us resist both endings: it removes the self from the center, then returns to us a self capable of being fulfilled.

A good house has a shared roof and doors that can close. The roof protects every room from the storm; the doors prevent common life from becoming mutual invasion. This may be the political meaning of fulfilling the private by abandoning private designs: let the common world belong to no single person, so that every person within it may truly belong to themselves.
