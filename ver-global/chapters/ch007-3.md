# 第007-3章：公与私之间，谁成全了谁？

## 原文

### 第1页

天长地久。天地所以能长且久者，以其不自生，故能长生。

是以圣人后其身而身先，外其身而身存。

非以其无私邪？故能成其私。

——《道德经》第七章《韬光》

### 第2页

知常容，容乃公，公乃王，王乃天，天乃道，道乃久。

圣人不积，既以为人己愈有，既以与人己愈多。

——《道德经》第十六章、第八十一章

老子没有把“公”写成对“私”的处决。他提出的问题更贴近日常生活：一个人怎样与他人共同生活，既不把别人变成自己的工具，也不把自己消失在关系之中？

## 释义

### 第3页

第七章没有出现“公”字。它先说“无私”，继而说“成其私”。若把这两句匆匆读成道德劝告，便容易得到一个可疑的结论：先牺牲自己，将来总会获得补偿。

老子所说的并非延期兑现的报酬。“私”在这里至少有两种面目：一种把周围的人和事都用于保护自己；另一种则是每个具体生命都需要安顿和完成的自身。前一种私若无限扩张，后一种私反而难以长久。

### 第4页

“私”还可以从三个层面理解。第一是“身”：安全、名声、位置和寿命。第二是“偏”：只从自己的角度分配代价，让别人承担自己不愿承担的部分。第三是不可替代的个人生活，包括身体、亲情、志趣与选择。

老子警惕前两层成为唯一尺度，却没有抹去第三层。圣人“外其身”，最后并非无身；圣人“无私”，最后仍能“成其私”。自我仍然存在，只是不再要求整个世界为它供给燃料。

### 第5页

因此，“成其私”不是揭穿无私背后的精明算计，而是改变个人所得形成的路径。若一个人把安全置于每一次决定之上，周围的人便容易成为盾牌；若把声望置于所有合作之上，同伴便会沦为观众。被层层护住的自我，常常也最孤立。

另一种自我从关系中生长。它不否认需要，也不羞于接受照料；它只是明白，个人依赖语言、知识、信任和他人的劳动而生活。它所完成的“私”，是一段不必耗尽周围世界才能成立的人生。

### 第6页

那么，“公”是什么？第十六章说：“知常容，容乃公。”这里的“公”首先不是把所有人压成同一种形状，而是不让某一个人的欲望冒充所有人的尺度。

一条未被污染的河、一座向所有读者开放的图书馆、一套不因身份而随意改变的规则，都属于共同生活的条件。它们不要求人放弃自己的房间、家庭和愿望；正因为这些条件没有被少数人耗尽，每个人才更可能拥有自己的生活。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第7页

**无私：** 王弼解释为“无为于身”，即不围绕自身刻意运作；河上公则强调公正、不偏。两种读法虽着眼点不同，都在限制自我中心的无限扩张。

**成其私：** 不是取消个人利益，而是让个人的安顿与成长从良好的关系中产生。它是一种间接成全，不是一笔披着奉献外衣的投资。

**公：** 在老子的语境中，更接近包容、不偏和通达。它反对的不是个体差异，而是某个局部把自己当成全部。

### 第8页

**互助：** 不是要求每次付出都得到等额回报，而是在承认彼此依赖的前提下，使帮助能够流动。

**边界：** 无私不等于没有拒绝的权利。健康的关系既允许给予，也允许休息、保留和离开。

**非支配：** 现代政治学用这个词描述不受任意意志摆布的状态。它可以作为理解本章的背景：无论在组织还是公共生活中，占据中心的人都不应把共同事务变成个人意志的延伸。

## 正文

### 第9页

“公”与“私”并不是两个彼此敌对的容器，仿佛给一边增加一些，另一边就必然减少。更准确地说，它们互相规定：没有共同语言，个人的经验难以表达；没有基本信任，个人的选择会被无休止的防备吞没；没有私人边界，共同生活又会变成持续的侵入。

公所保护的，不是一个抽象整体，而是每个人得以生活的共同条件；私所保护的，也不是任性占有，而是一个具体的人不被关系抹去的权利。

### 第10页

我们出生时已经置身许多并非自己创造的事物之中：语言来自前人，道路由陌生人维护，知识经过一代代积累，信任则靠无数次守约慢慢形成。个人并不因此失去独特性。恰恰是在这些共同条件里，独特的能力和选择才有展开的地方。

老子说“圣人不积”。这不必理解成拒绝一切拥有，而是提醒人不要把流经自己的东西全部截留。知识若只用来巩固优越感，关系若只用来证明自己重要，所得越多，人与世界的联系反而越窄。

### 第11页

给予也可能暗藏账本：我今天帮助你，是否为了确保你明天偿还？若每次付出都预先规定回报，互助便只是延迟结算的交易。

但不计较并不等于任人索取。老子的“无私”首先改变的是行动的起点：做一件值得做的事时，不让“我能得到什么”成为唯一理由。至于一段关系能否持续，还要看双方是否尊重边界，是否有人长期只拿不还，是否允许疲惫的人停下来。

### 第12页

把这一章放进组织与公共生活中，可以得到一条有限的提醒：职位不应成为占据者身体的延长，共同成果也不必永远附着在某个人的名字上。

老子说，最好的统治者功成事遂，百姓却说“我自然”。若暂时放下政治色彩，这句话同样适用于许多协作：协调者让事情得以完成，却不把所有人的贡献收拢成自己的功劳。真正可靠的中心，使参与者保留判断和行动的能力。

### 第13页

老子理想中的个体，不是没有需要、没有边界的奉献者。他更像一个不再把自我当作绝对中心的人：能够承担责任，却不独占功劳；能够接受帮助，也不把别人的付出视为理所当然。

这样的人成长得更具体，而不是更模糊。他知道自己喜欢什么，也知道喜好不是所有人的法律；他愿意进入关系，也保留独处、休息和改变方向的能力。“成其私”最终成全的，是一个有轮廓而不封闭的自我。

## 回扣

### 第14页

在职场里，个人野心有时会被包装成团队使命，别人的时间便被当作可以无限调用的资源。在家庭里，关心也可能越过边界：父母把控制称作爱，把孩子的人生当作自己愿望的延续。

第七章并不要求一味退让。它让我们检视代价的流向：谁得到名声，谁承担疲惫？谁确定共同目标，谁可以表达不同意见？“无私”首先应约束拥有更多决定权的人，而不是要求已经退到边缘的人继续牺牲。

### 第15页

公与私之间真正危险的，不是边界本身，而是一方吞没另一方。私若占尽共同条件，关系便只剩索取；共同之名若抹去个人，亲近也会成为负担。

一所房子需要共同的屋顶，也需要可以关上的房门。屋顶使每个房间免受风雨，房门使共同生活不至于彼此侵入。也许这就是“无私而成其私”留给现代人的启发：不把自己变成世界的中心，也不把自己从世界中删去。

# Chapter 007-3: Between the Common and the Private—Who Completes Whom?

## Original Text

### Page 1

Heaven is enduring; Earth is lasting.

Heaven and Earth endure because they do not live for themselves. Therefore, they can go on living.

Thus the sage places himself behind, yet finds himself in front; puts himself outside consideration, yet remains secure.

Is it not because he is without private designs that what is truly his can be fulfilled?

— *Dao De Jing*, Chapter 7, “Concealing the Light”

### Page 2

Knowing constancy, one becomes capacious. Being capacious, one becomes impartial; being impartial, one becomes fit to lead; being fit to lead, one accords with Heaven, Dao, and duration.

The sage does not hoard. The more he does for others, the more he has; the more he gives to others, the more abundant he becomes.

— *Dao De Jing*, Chapters 16 and 81

Laozi does not make the common a sentence passed upon the private. His question is closer to daily life: how can a person live with others without turning them into instruments, yet without disappearing into the relationship?

## Interpretation

### Page 3

The word *gong*—the common, public, or impartial—does not appear in Chapter 7. The chapter first says “without private designs” and then “fulfills what is his.” Read too quickly as a moral lesson, this becomes dubious advice: sacrifice yourself now, and compensation will arrive later.

Laozi is not promising a reward on deferred payment. The private has at least two faces here. One uses everything nearby to protect the self. The other is the particular life that each person must inhabit and complete. When the first expands without limit, the second becomes harder to sustain.

### Page 4

The private can be understood at three levels. First comes the body: safety, reputation, position, and lifespan. Second comes partiality: assigning costs from one’s own viewpoint so that others carry what one refuses to bear. Third comes an irreplaceable personal life, including body, attachments, interests, and choices.

Laozi warns against making the first two the only measure, but he does not erase the third. The sage puts the body outside consideration, yet does not become bodiless. The sage abandons private designs, yet something of his is still fulfilled. The self remains, but no longer asks the whole world to feed it.

### Page 5

“Fulfilling what is his” does not expose selflessness as sophisticated calculation. It changes the path by which personal goods arise. When safety stands above every decision, other people easily become shields. When reputation stands above every collaboration, colleagues become an audience. The more heavily such a self is defended, the more isolated it becomes.

Another kind of self grows through relationship. It does not deny need or feel ashamed of receiving care. It simply understands that a life depends on language, knowledge, trust, and the labor of others. What is fulfilled is a life that need not exhaust the surrounding world in order to stand.

### Page 6

What, then, is the common? Chapter 16 says, “Being capacious, one becomes impartial.” Here the common does not begin by pressing everyone into one shape. It begins by refusing to let one person’s appetite masquerade as the measure for all.

An unpolluted river, a library open to every reader, and rules that do not change arbitrarily with status are all conditions of shared life. None asks people to abandon their rooms, families, or hopes. Precisely because these conditions have not been consumed by a few, more people can possess lives of their own.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 7

**Without private designs:** Wang Bi reads this as not acting deliberately on behalf of the body; Heshang Gong emphasizes impartiality. The two readings begin in different places, but both limit the boundless expansion of self-centeredness.

**Fulfilling what is one’s own:** This does not abolish personal benefit. It allows security and growth to arise from sound relationships. It is indirect fulfillment, not an investment disguised as generosity.

**The common or impartial:** In Laozi’s context, *gong* suggests capaciousness, openness, and freedom from partiality. Its opposite is not individual difference, but a part presenting itself as the whole.

### Page 8

**Mutual aid:** This does not require every contribution to receive an equal return. It begins by acknowledging interdependence and allowing help to circulate.

**Boundary:** Selflessness does not mean losing the right to refuse. A healthy relationship makes room for giving, rest, privacy, and departure.

**Non-domination:** Modern political thought uses this term for freedom from arbitrary will. It offers limited background for the chapter: in an organization or in public life, whoever occupies the center should not turn a shared undertaking into an extension of personal will.

## Main Text

### Page 9

The common and the private are not two hostile containers, as though anything added to one must be taken from the other. They define one another. Without shared language, personal experience is difficult to express. Without basic trust, choice is consumed by constant vigilance. Without private boundaries, shared life becomes continuous intrusion.

The common protects not an abstract whole, but the conditions in which people can live. The private protects not arbitrary possession, but the right of a concrete person not to be erased by relationship.

### Page 10

We are born among things we did not create. Language comes from those before us; roads are maintained by strangers; knowledge accumulates across generations; trust forms through countless promises kept. None of this abolishes individuality. These shared conditions are precisely what allow distinctive abilities and choices to unfold.

Laozi says that the sage does not hoard. This need not mean refusing every possession. It warns against intercepting everything that passes through us. When knowledge serves only to reinforce superiority, or relationships only to prove importance, accumulation narrows rather than enlarges our connection to the world.

### Page 11

Giving can conceal a ledger: do I help today only to guarantee repayment tomorrow? If every contribution specifies its return in advance, mutual aid becomes a transaction with delayed settlement.

Yet freedom from calculation does not mean accepting unlimited demands. Laozi’s “without private designs” first changes the starting point of action: when something is worth doing, “What will I get?” need not be the only reason. Whether a relationship can last still depends on respected boundaries, on noticing when one person only takes, and on allowing the exhausted to stop.

### Page 12

Placed in organizational and public life, the chapter offers a limited reminder: an office should not become an extension of its holder’s body, and shared achievements need not remain attached forever to one person’s name.

Laozi says that when the best ruler completes the work, the people say, “We did it naturally, by ourselves.” If we temporarily set aside the political setting, the line also describes many forms of collaboration. A coordinator enables the work without gathering everyone’s contribution into personal credit. A reliable center leaves participants with their own judgment and capacity to act.

### Page 13

Laozi’s ideal individual is not a giver without needs or boundaries. Such a person no longer treats the self as the absolute center. They can accept responsibility without claiming every success, and receive help without taking another person’s effort for granted.

This person becomes more distinct, not less. They know what they enjoy while knowing that preference is not a law for everyone. They enter relationships while retaining the capacity for solitude, rest, and a change of direction. What the chapter finally fulfills is a self with contours that is not sealed shut.

## Return to the Original

### Page 14

At work, personal ambition can be packaged as the team’s mission, turning other people’s time into a resource available without limit. Within families, care can also cross a boundary: parents may call control love and turn a child’s life into a continuation of their own wishes.

Chapter 7 does not recommend endless yielding. It asks us to trace where costs flow. Who receives the name, and who carries the exhaustion? Who defines the shared goal, and who may voice a different view? “Without private designs” should first restrain those with greater power to decide, rather than demand further sacrifice from those already at the edge.

### Page 15

The real danger between the common and the private is not the boundary itself, but either side swallowing the other. When private appetite consumes shared conditions, relationship becomes extraction. When the language of the common erases the person, closeness becomes a burden.

A house needs a shared roof and doors that can close. The roof protects every room from the storm; the doors keep common life from becoming mutual invasion. This may be what “fulfilling the private through freedom from private designs” offers a modern reader: do not make yourself the center of the world, and do not delete yourself from it.
