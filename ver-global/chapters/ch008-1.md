# 第008-1章：易性——上善若水

## 原文

### 第1页

上善若水。水善利万物而不争，处众人之所恶，故几于道。

白话：最好的善，如同水。水滋养万物，却不与万物争夺；它愿意停留在人们不喜欢的低处，所以最接近道。

### 第2页

居善地，心善渊，与善仁，言善信，正善治，事善能，动善时。

白话：选择位置，要像水一样安于合适之地；保持内心，要像深潭一样沉静；待人要仁厚，言语要可信，处理事务要平正，做事要胜任，行动要合乎时机。

### 第3页

夫唯不争，故无尤。

白话：正因为不把生命耗在无谓的争夺中，所以少有怨尤，也少给自己招来过失。

河上公把本章题为“易性”。它像是在提醒我们：人的习惯总想向高处挤，水却提供了另一种性情——向低处走，向需要它的地方走。

## 释义

### 第4页

“上善若水”常被读成一句温柔的赞美，仿佛老子只是在劝人随和。但水并不软弱。它能托起船，也能穿过石缝；遇到阻挡时，它不必证明自己比石头更硬，只需寻找仍可前行的地方。

水的力量不来自姿态，而来自作用。它使草木生长，使人解渴，使污物得到冲洗。事情完成以后，水没有留下自己的名字。

### 第5页

“处众人之所恶”不是要求人轻贱自己，更不是把忍受伤害说成美德。河上公所说的“卑湿垢浊”，是人们不愿停留的低处。水去那里，因为低处正需要汇集、清洗与滋养。

现实生活中也有许多这样的低处：无人愿意整理的混乱、没有掌声的维护工作、谈话里迟迟无人肯听的那一面。接近水德，不是故意吃亏，而是不只依照名声的高低选择事情。

### 第6页

老子接着写下七个“善”，把水的性情变成一套可以实践的尺度。

位置要合适，内心要深静，给予要仁厚，言语要可信，处理要平正，做事要有能力，行动要看时机。这七项并不要求一个人变得没有个性。它们只是让才能不再急着炫耀，让善意不再妨碍结果。

王弼特别提醒，水只是“几于道”，也就是接近道。水仍有可以看见的形态，道却不能被固定成某一种东西。我们学习的是水的作用方式，不是把水变成新的偶像。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第7页

**易性：** 河上公本为第八章所加的章题。可以把它理解为调整惯常的性情：从争高转向处下，从抢先转向适时。

**几于道：** 接近道，而不等于道。王弼说“道无水有”，提醒读者不要把譬喻当成本体。

**七善：** 居善地、心善渊、与善仁、言善信、正善治、事善能、动善时。河上公分别以水的向下、深清、养物、照形、清平、随形和应时解释。

**不争：** 不是放弃原则或能力，而是不为虚名抢夺位置，不用冲突证明自己的存在。

## 画面描述

### 第8页

雨后的清晨，一条引水沟沿山坡缓缓流向村舍。落叶堵住了转弯处，水没有撞击石壁，只从叶片之间寻找细小的空隙。溪声时断时续，木桥还带着潮湿的气味。

一位年轻的项目负责人卷起袖口，想把沟渠改得更直。守园的老人蹲下来，先取走一把湿叶。

年轻人问：“直一点，不是更快吗？”

老人说：“也许更快经过，却未必让沿路的树喝到水。”

水重新流动，先润湿树根，再进入厨房旁的蓄水池。老人听了一会儿水声，说：“水不急着第一个到。它只是不漏掉需要它的地方。”

## 正文

### 第9页

年轻人沿着水沟往下走，发现水每到一处，形状都不同。在窄石缝里，它细而急；进入浅池后，它便慢下来；落进木桶，又安静地贴着桶壁。

老人说：“这就是事善能。不是永远坚持一种姿势，而是到了什么地方，便把那里的事情做好。”

年轻人问：“那岂不是总在迁就？”

老人摇头：“水会改变形状，却没有失去水性。适应不是放弃自己，而是分清什么必须守住，什么可以改变。”

### 第10页

走到低处，沟边混着泥沙和折断的树枝。年轻人绕开那片泥泞，老人却停下来疏通出口。

“大家都不喜欢这里。”年轻人说。

“所以这里才最需要有人处理。”老人回答，“高处容易被看见，低处决定水能不能继续走。”

这句话没有把低微变成浪漫。老人并不赞美脏乱，他只是愿意完成清理，使水不被堵住。真正的处下，不是忍受一切，而是看见那些不显眼却不可缺少的工作。

### 第11页

太阳升高以后，山里的积水开始增多。老人没有立刻打开所有闸门。他摸了摸湿土，又听了听上游的水声，等蓄水池的水位稳定，才把木闸提起一寸。

“为什么刚才不放？”年轻人问。

“太早，田地留不住；太晚，水又会漫出来。”老人说，“能做一件事，还不够。要在它应该发生的时候做。”

这便是“动善时”。时机不是犹豫的借口，也不是凭感觉碰运气。它来自持续观察，并在条件成熟时愿意行动。

### 第12页

在现代职场里，水德最难的部分，也许不是谦虚，而是让自己的能力真正进入事情。

一位负责人若过早说出答案，团队很可能只剩附和；若始终不作决定，所谓包容又会变成逃避。像水一样工作，是先让信息汇集，听见不同意见，再在需要承担责任的时刻明确开口。

“心善渊”使人听得深，“言善信”使承诺可以依靠，“事善能”则要求善意经得起专业检验。老子从未说，只要态度柔和，事情自然就会成功。

### 第13页

在家庭里，不争也不是永远沉默。沉默有时只是把不满留到以后爆发。水的方式更诚实：它不靠提高音量夺取胜利，却会把真实需要带进谈话。

父母可以承认孩子有自己的节奏，伴侣也可以在照顾对方时说出自己的疲惫。仁厚若没有边界，容易变成委屈；边界若没有仁厚，又容易变成拒绝。水给我们的不是标准答案，而是一种调节能力：既不堵塞，也不泛滥。

### 第14页

十二世纪的英格兰史家亨廷顿的亨利记录过一则克努特王与潮水的故事。后世常把它讲成国王狂妄地命令海水退去；较早的叙述却更像一场劝诫。国王把座椅放在海边，命令涨潮停止，海水仍按时漫过沙滩，浸湿他的脚和衣袍。

克努特借此让奉承者明白，王位也不能命令潮汐。这个西方故事与“动善时”形成了清楚的呼应：水不与国王争辩，也不因权力而提前或推迟。它只依照比人的愿望更大的时序来去。

### 第15页

“不争”最容易被误解成没有雄心。其实，水从高处到低处，从细流到江海，从未停止前进。它只是不把每一块石头都当成必须击败的敌人。

现代生活里，许多耗损来自争错了对象：会议里争最后一句，社交中争谁更受羡慕，家庭里争谁先承认错误。即使赢了，原本要解决的问题仍留在原处。

水让人重新辨认目的。若目标是抵达，绕行并非失败；若目标是滋养，让别人先得到好处也不等于失去自己。

## 回扣

### 第16页

回到七个“善”，它们并不是七条装饰人格的标签。

居善地，是找到真正需要自己的位置；心善渊，是不让情绪立刻占满内心；与善仁，是使给予抵达对方；言善信，是让话语经得起时间；正善治，是把混乱整理得平正；事善能，是拥有完成事情的本领；动善时，是知道何时等待、何时出手。

### 第17页

水流过低处，并没有变得低贱；它改变形状，也没有失去自己。

它不向花草索取感谢，不同石头争论道路，却始终在做一件明确的事：把生命带到能够生长的地方。

老子所说的上善，或许正是这种安静而可靠的存在。不是让所有人看见你多么善良，而是当你经过以后，事情得以继续，人也有了喘息和生长的余地。

# Chapter 008-1: Changing One’s Nature—The Highest Good Is Like Water

## Original Text

### Page 1

The highest good is like water. Water benefits the ten thousand things without competing. It remains in places that people dislike, and so it comes close to Dao.

In plain language: the best kind of goodness resembles water. It nourishes life without fighting for recognition and accepts the low places others avoid. For this reason, it approaches the way of Dao.

### Page 2

In dwelling, be good at choosing the ground. In mind, be deep. In giving, be humane. In speech, be trustworthy. In ordering, be just. In work, be capable. In movement, be timely.

In plain language: choose a fitting place; keep the mind deep and still; treat others with care; speak reliably; bring fairness to disorder; do the work competently; and act when the time is right.

### Page 3

Because water does not compete, it is free from blame.

In plain language: when life is not spent on needless rivalry, we create fewer resentments and fewer causes for regret.

Heshang Gong titles this chapter “Changing One’s Nature.” The title suggests a turn in temperament. Human habit presses upward; water offers another disposition—move downward, toward the place where one is needed.

## Interpretation

### Page 4

“The highest good is like water” is often heard as a gentle compliment, as though Laozi were advising us merely to be agreeable. But water is not weak. It carries boats and passes through stone. When blocked, it need not prove that it is harder than the rock. It only needs to find where movement remains possible.

Its strength lies not in display, but in effect. Water raises plants, relieves thirst, and washes away dirt. When the work is done, it leaves no signature.

### Page 5

To “remain where people dislike” does not mean despising oneself, nor does it turn the endurance of harm into a virtue. Heshang Gong speaks of low, damp, and soiled places—places people prefer to avoid. Water goes there because low ground is where gathering, cleansing, and nourishment are needed.

Daily life has low places too: confusion no one wants to sort out, maintenance that earns no applause, or the part of a conversation no one has been willing to hear. To approach the virtue of water is not to seek loss. It is to stop choosing work solely by the prestige attached to it.

### Page 6

Laozi then gives seven forms of goodness, turning the disposition of water into a practical measure.

Choose a fitting place. Keep the mind deep. Give with care. Speak reliably. Bring fairness to disorder. Work with competence. Act at the right time. None of these asks a person to lose individuality. They allow talent to work without demanding display, and goodwill to serve without obstructing the result.

Wang Bi adds an important restraint: water only “comes close to Dao.” Water still has visible form, while Dao cannot be fixed as one particular thing. We are learning from the way water works, not making water into a new idol.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 7

**Changing One’s Nature:** Heshang Gong’s title for Chapter Eight. It can be read as adjusting habitual temperament: from striving upward to accepting the lower place, and from rushing first to acting in time.

**Close to Dao:** Near Dao, but not identical with it. Wang Bi writes that Dao is without fixed form while water has form, warning us not to mistake the image for what it points toward.

**The seven forms of goodness:** fitting ground, a deep mind, humane giving, trustworthy speech, just ordering, capable work, and timely action. Heshang Gong explains them through water’s descent, depth, nourishment, reflection, evenness, adaptability, and response to season.

**Non-contention:** Not the abandonment of principle or ability, but a refusal to seize position for empty prestige or use conflict to prove that one exists.

## Visual Description

### Page 8

Morning follows a night of rain. An irrigation channel runs down a hillside toward a cluster of homes. Fallen leaves block a bend. The water does not batter the stone wall; it searches through narrow spaces between the leaves. The stream’s voice comes and goes, and the wooden bridge still smells of rain.

A young project leader rolls up his sleeves, ready to make the channel straighter. The old gardener crouches and first removes a handful of wet leaves.

“Wouldn’t a straight channel be faster?” the younger man asks.

“It might pass more quickly,” says the gardener, “but would the trees along the way still drink?”

The water moves again. It wets the roots before entering a storage pool beside the kitchen. The gardener listens for a moment and says, “Water is not trying to arrive first. It is trying not to miss what needs it.”

## Main Text

### Page 9

Walking downstream, the young man notices that water takes a different shape in every place. Through a narrow gap it becomes quick and fine. In a shallow pool it slows. Inside a wooden bucket it rests against the curve of the vessel.

“This is being capable in work,” says the gardener. “Do not insist on one posture everywhere. Enter a place and do what that place requires.”

“Isn’t that simply giving in?” the young man asks.

The gardener shakes his head. “Water changes shape without losing its nature. Adaptation is not self-betrayal. It is knowing what must remain and what may change.”

### Page 10

At the lowest part of the channel, mud and broken branches have gathered. The young man steps around the mess. The gardener stops to clear the outlet.

“No one likes this place,” says the young man.

“That is why it needs attention,” the gardener replies. “The high ground is easy to notice. The low ground decides whether the water can continue.”

The words do not romanticize lowliness. The gardener does not praise disorder. He removes it so the water can move. To occupy the lower place is not to endure everything. It is to notice work that is inconspicuous and indispensable.

### Page 11

As the day grows warmer, more runoff comes down the mountain. The gardener does not raise every gate at once. He touches the wet soil and listens upstream. Only when the storage pool steadies does he lift the wooden gate by an inch.

“Why not release it earlier?” asks the young man.

“Too early, and the fields cannot hold it. Too late, and it spills over,” the gardener says. “Knowing how to act is not enough. One must act when the action belongs.”

This is timeliness. Timing is neither an excuse for hesitation nor a lucky guess. It grows from sustained attention and the willingness to act when conditions are ready.

### Page 12

In modern work, the hardest part of water’s virtue may not be humility, but allowing ability to enter the task in the right way.

When a leader states the answer too early, a team may be left with nothing to do but agree. When the leader never decides, openness becomes avoidance. To work like water is to let information gather, hear different judgments, and then speak clearly when responsibility must be taken.

A deep mind allows us to listen. Trustworthy speech makes promises dependable. Capability requires goodwill to survive a professional test. Laozi never says that a gentle manner alone will make the work succeed.

### Page 13

Within a family, non-contention does not mean perpetual silence. Silence may merely store resentment until it breaks out later. Water offers a more honest approach: it does not raise its volume to seize victory, but it still carries real need into the conversation.

Parents can respect a child’s pace. Partners can care for one another while admitting fatigue. Care without boundaries becomes grievance; boundaries without care become rejection. Water gives us no universal script. It teaches regulation: neither blockage nor flood.

### Page 14

The twelfth-century English historian Henry of Huntingdon records a story about King Cnut and the tide. Later retellings often present a vain king ordering the sea to retreat. The earlier account is closer to a lesson. Cnut places his seat by the shore and commands the rising tide to stop. The water continues on time, crossing the sand and wetting his feet and robe.

Cnut uses the scene to show his flatterers that a throne cannot command the tide. The Western story speaks clearly to Laozi’s timeliness. Water does not argue with the king, nor does it arrive early or late for power. It follows an order larger than human preference.

### Page 15

Non-contention is easily mistaken for lack of ambition. Yet water never stops moving—from high ground to low, from stream to river, from river to sea. It simply refuses to treat every stone as an enemy that must be defeated.

Much modern exhaustion comes from contending with the wrong thing: fighting for the final word in a meeting, for the more enviable life in public, or for the right to receive the first apology at home. Even when we win, the original problem remains.

Water helps us recover the purpose. If the aim is to arrive, going around is not failure. If the aim is to nourish, letting another benefit first does not erase the self.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

The seven forms of goodness are not labels with which to decorate a personality.

To choose the ground is to find where one is truly needed. A deep mind does not let emotion fill every space at once. Humane giving reaches the recipient. Trustworthy speech survives the passage of time. Just ordering brings fairness to confusion. Capability finishes the task. Timeliness knows when to wait and when to move.

### Page 17

Water passes through the low place without becoming lowly. It changes shape without losing itself.

It asks no gratitude from the grass and argues with no stone about the road. Yet it continues to do one definite thing: carry life toward the places where life can grow.

The highest good may be this quiet reliability. It does not require everyone to notice how virtuous we are. It leaves the work able to continue and other people with enough room to breathe and grow.
