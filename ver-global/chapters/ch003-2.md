# 第003-2章：无为与自发秩序

## 原文

### 第1页

1776年，亚当·斯密在《国富论》讨论国内投资时写道，个人追求自身利益，可能“受着一只看不见的手的指导”，促成并非出于本意的社会效果。这句话后来被广泛用来概括分散行动如何产生非预期秩序，但它并不保证每一次逐利都会带来整体福祉。

### 第2页

这与老子“我无事而民自富”的说法形成了可供比较的呼应。

斯密对重商主义的批判同样反对政府掌控市场的每一个细节。后来的自由主义传统常以“守夜人”政府概括更狭义的国家职能；这一说法不是斯密本人对其全部政治经济思想的总结。

### 第3页

20世纪，弗里德里希·哈耶克在《通往奴役之路》等著作中警告：试图以中央计划统摄复杂社会，可能损害自由。

他在多部著作中阐发“自发秩序”：语言、道德、市场与普通法等秩序，往往由许多个体行动逐步形成，而非出自一个中央设计者的完整蓝图。

### 第4页

20世纪80年代英美的新自由主义改革减少了若干经济领域的国家干预，让更多资源分配交由市场。其价值基础与政策后果都不能等同于老子的“无为”；可比较之处，仅在于两者都对全面控制抱有疑虑。

### 第5页

进入21世纪，理查德·塞勒与卡斯·桑斯坦推广了“助推”（nudge）策略：通过默认选项和选择架构影响行为，同时保留退出或另选的可能，而非直接命令。

## 释义

### 第6页

本章把老子的“无为”与近代社会科学中的“自发秩序”、市场协调和“助推”并置。它们并非同一理论，只共享一个有限的问题：治理者何时应当克制干预，为分散行动留下形成秩序的空间？

### 第7页

需要区分价值立场与治理技术：斯密与哈耶克强调个体自主与规则边界；新自由主义在实践上依赖市场机制；塞勒的“助推”则位于完全放手与强力干预之间，提供一种温和的政策镜像。

## 术语与考据简注

### 第8页

- **看不见的手**：斯密用以描述个体利益追求如何在无意间带来公共利益的机制。
- **自发秩序（Spontaneous Order）**：哈耶克概念，指复杂社会秩序通过去中心化的个体行动自然演化而成。
- **助推（Nudge）**：行为经济学用语，通过设计默认选项与选择架构微调行为，而避免直接命令式干预。

## 正文

### 第9页

比较这些思想，可以看到治理中的一个实际难题：规则太密，行动可能僵化；规则太少，强者又可能占据空缺。老子说“我无为而民自化”，首先要求统治者减少对欲望和权力的刺激。

### 第10页

斯密的“看不见的手”说明，市场参与者的分散行动可能产生并非刻意设计的社会效果。它可以与“无为而治”比较，但不能由此推出逐利必然等于公共利益。

### 第11页

哈耶克进一步指出，中央设计容易低估分散知识的复杂性，强制规划也可能产生未被预见的后果。这与老子对过度干预的警告有相近之处。

### 第12页

但差异同样重要。道家的“无为”连着不争、少私与对统治欲的节制；现代自由主义内部则同时讨论个人自由、分散知识、权利边界与效率，不能只归结为自利假设。

### 第13页

“助推”位于完全放手与直接命令之间：政策设计者改变默认选项，却仍让人保有选择。它可与“少用强制”的治理思路对照，但并不是“无为”的现代译本。

### 第14页

需要警惕的是：任何以“放手”为名的政策，都可能被利益力量利用。因此，制度设计仍需关注公平、权力结构与弱势群体保护。

### 第15页

“无为”与“自发秩序”理论并非简单同义，它们在价值根基、目标取向与制度工具上各有差别，但都提示治理应谨慎对待“全知全能的设计者”幻想。

## 回扣

### 第16页

将老子的“无为”放回当代语境，需要既吸收自发秩序的启示，也警惕放任带来的不平等。有效治理是边界清晰的规则加上对自组织能力的尊重。

### 第17页

古今的制度实践都在反复面对同一个时机问题：何时退手，何时设置必要的保护性规则。治理的分寸，正在于让自发秩序不因过度控制而窒息，也不因缺少约束而被强者占据。

# Chapter 003-2: Non-Action and Spontaneous Order

## Original Text

### Page 1

In 1776, while discussing domestic investment in *The Wealth of Nations*, Adam Smith wrote that a person pursuing private advantage may be “led by an invisible hand” to promote an end that was no part of the intention. The phrase was later generalized as an account of unintended order, but it does not promise that every pursuit of profit produces the public good.

### Page 2

This offers a useful comparison with Laozi’s claim that when the ruler refrains from interference, the people prosper. Smith’s critique of mercantilism likewise resists state control of every market detail. A later liberal tradition used the image of a “night-watchman” state for a narrower public role; the phrase is not Smith’s own summary of his entire political economy.


### Page 3

In the twentieth century, Friedrich Hayek warned in *The Road to Serfdom* and other works that attempts to govern a complex society through central planning could damage freedom. Across several books he developed the idea of spontaneous order: language, morals, markets, and common law often grow from many decentralized actions rather than from one complete design.


### Page 4

The neoliberal reforms of the 1980s in the United States and United Kingdom reduced state intervention in several economic fields and left more allocation to markets. Their values and consequences should not be equated with Laozi’s non-action. The limited point of comparison is their suspicion of comprehensive control.

### Page 5

In the twenty-first century, Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein popularized “nudges”: changes to defaults and choice architecture that influence behavior while preserving the option to choose otherwise.

## Interpretation


### Page 6

This chapter places Daoist non-action beside modern ideas of spontaneous order, market coordination, and nudging. They are not one theory. They share a narrower question: when should government restrain intervention and leave room for decentralized action to form an order?


### Page 7

The core distinction lies between normative foundations and governance techniques: Smith and Hayek stress individual autonomy and rule-bound frameworks; neoliberal practice relies more practically on market mechanisms; nudging (Thaler) sits between full laissez-faire and heavy-handed regulation, offering a pragmatic policy tool.

## Key Terms and Notes

### Page 8

- **Invisible hand**: Smith’s metaphor for how self-interested behavior can lead to public benefits.
- **Spontaneous order**: Hayek’s idea that complex social orders arise without central design.
- **Nudge**: Behavioral economics technique of influencing choices subtly via defaults and framing.

## Main Text

### Page 9

The comparison reveals a practical difficulty. Rules that are too dense can make action rigid; rules that are too weak can leave space for the powerful to capture. Laozi’s “I do nothing, and the people transform themselves” first restrains the ruler’s stimulation of desire and power.

### Page 10

Smith’s invisible hand shows how decentralized market actions may have unintended social effects. It can be compared with non-coercive governance, but it does not prove that private gain always equals public benefit.

### Page 11

Hayek added that centralized plans can misjudge dispersed knowledge and produce unintended consequences; his caution parallels Laozi’s warnings against excessive intervention.

### Page 12

Yet differences matter. Daoist non-action is tied to non-contention, fewer private designs, and restraint of the urge to rule. Modern liberal traditions also debate liberty, dispersed knowledge, rights, and efficiency; they cannot be reduced to self-interest alone.

### Page 13

Nudging sits between laissez-faire and direct command: a policy designer changes defaults while leaving people a choice. It can be compared with governance that uses less coercion, but it is not a modern translation of *wu wei*.

### Page 14

Policymakers must guard against using "hands-off" rhetoric to justify capture by powerful interests; institutional design must still protect equity and the vulnerable.

### Page 15

In short, non-action and spontaneous order are related but not identical; both warn against the fantasy of an all-knowing designer and invite humility in governance.

## Return to the Original

### Page 16

Putting Laozi into contemporary debate means learning from spontaneous processes while building safeguards that ensure fairness.

### Page 17

The recurring problem of governance is one of timing: when to step back and when to establish protective rules. Spontaneous order must be kept from suffocating under excessive control, but also from being captured by the powerful when safeguards are absent.
