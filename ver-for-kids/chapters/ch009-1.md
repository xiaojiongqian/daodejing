# 第009-1章：满了，就端不稳

## 原文

### 第1页

持而盈之，不如其已；
揣而棁之，不可长保。

金玉满堂，莫之能守；
富贵而骄，自遗其咎。

功遂身退，天之道。

## 释义

### 第2页

第九章先说一个很简单的动作：端杯子。

杯子八分满，可以稳稳端走；满到边上，还硬要端，就不是“多得一点”，而是每一步都怕洒。

### 第3页

“持而盈之”的麻烦，不只在东西太多。

更在于：满了以后，你就被它管住了。手要僵着，脚要慢着，眼睛只盯着杯沿，别的事都顾不上。

### 第4页

“揣而棁之”，像把笔尖削得太尖。

尖到极处，看起来厉害，却没有厚度承受力量。刚碰到纸，可能就断。

### 第5页

金玉满堂，看起来很富，可满屋宝物也带来满屋担心。

富贵本身不是罪过。危险在于有了东西、有了位置，就骄傲起来，以为自己永远不会失去。

### 第6页

“功遂身退”，不是事情没做就躲开。

它说的是：事情已经成了，名声也已经够了，就别再扒着功劳不放。太阳过了正午会偏西，月亮圆了也会慢慢缺，这是天的节奏。

## 难词注解

### 第7页

**持而盈之**：端着一个已经太满的东西。这里指贪多、不肯停。

**已**：停止。不是失败，而是知道够了。

**棁**：削尖、弄得锋利。太锋利，有时反而容易折损。

### 第8页

**金玉满堂**：金子玉石堆满屋子，比喻财富很多。

**咎**：麻烦、过失、后果。

**功遂身退**：事情完成以后，把位置让出来，不死抱着功劳。

## 正文

### 第9页

空空村晚上要点灯读书，还请了邻村的老人来讲故事。

阿禾被分到一件大事：准备灯盏、笔和小书桌。他心里亮了一下，像自己也被点成了一盏灯。

### 第10页

“今晚一定要最好。”阿禾想。

他把第一盏灯倒得满满的，油鼓到边上，像一层薄薄的月光。小石头提醒：“是不是太满了？”

### 第11页

“多一点，灯就亮一点。”阿禾说。

他刚把灯盏端起，手腕一抖，油沿着边滴下来。为了不让油落到书上，他整个人僵住，像端着一只不敢呼吸的小碗。

### 第12页

老人递来一块布，没有责备。

他说：“灯需要油，也需要一点空。太满以后，灯还没亮，你已经先被它牵住了。”

### 第13页

阿禾有点不服，又去削铅笔。

他把每支笔都削得尖尖的，尖到能在灯下闪一下光。第一笔刚落到纸上，笔尖“咔”的一声断了。

### 第14页

第二支也断了，第三支划破了纸。

小石头小声说：“你的笔太厉害，厉害到写不了字。”这回阿禾没有笑出来。

### 第15页

读书会开始前，阿禾又把书一本本叠得很高，想让大家一眼看见他准备得多。

可是最上面的几本滑下来，纸页散了一地。孩子们赶来捡，故事老人只好等在门口。

### 第16页

阿禾终于停住了。

他把灯油倒出一点，把铅笔削得短而稳，把书分成几小摞。东西少了一点，桌面反而能用；光不满到边，灯反而稳稳亮起来。

### 第17页

夜里，故事讲到一半，邻村老人说：“今晚准备得细。”

阿禾的心跳了一下，差点站起来说“是我”。可他看见大家的脸都在灯下亮着，谁也没有盯着他。

### 第18页

他忽然觉得，这样正好。

灯的事成了，故事也顺了。如果他非要把自己的名字再倒进去，杯子又要满出来了。

## 回扣

### 第19页

第九章不是说不要努力，也不是说不要把事情做好。

它说的是：努力到合适处，要知道停；锋利到可用处，不必再削；事情成了，就别把自己钉在功劳上。

### 第20页

阿禾写下：够了，不是少了。停下，也不是输了。

把灯点亮，然后让灯照大家；把事做好，然后让事情自己往前走。这也许就是“功遂身退”的一点意思。

# Chapter 009-1: When It Is Full, It Cannot Be Carried Steadily

## Original Text

### Page 1

It is better to leave a vessel unfilled, than to attempt to carry it when it is full.  If you keep feeling a point that has been sharpened, the point cannot long preserve its sharpness.

When gold and jade fill the hall, their possessor cannot keep them safe.  When wealth and honours lead to arrogancy, this brings its evil on itself.  When the work is done, and one's name is becoming distinguished, to withdraw into obscurity is the way of Heaven.

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Chapter Nine begins with a simple action: carrying a cup.

If a cup is partly full, it can be carried steadily. If it is filled to the edge and we still try to carry it, the little extra is no longer gain; every step becomes fear of spilling.

### Page 3

The trouble of fullness is not only that there is too much inside.

It is that once something is too full, it begins to rule you. The hand stiffens, the feet slow, the eyes stare at the rim, and other things are forgotten.

### Page 4

"A point that has been sharpened" is like a pencil shaved too fine.

At its sharpest it looks powerful, but it has no thickness to bear force. The moment it touches paper, it may break.

### Page 5

Gold and jade filling a hall look rich, but a house full of treasure also brings a house full of worry.

Wealth and honor are not crimes. The danger begins when having things or position makes a person proud, as if loss could never come.

### Page 6

"Withdraw when the work is done" does not mean hiding before the work is finished.

It means: when the matter is accomplished and the name is enough, do not cling to the credit. The sun moves west after noon; the full moon slowly wanes. This is the rhythm of heaven.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 7

**vessel** — A container, such as a cup or bowl.

**unfilled** — Not filled all the way to the top; leaving space.

**point** — The sharp tip of something.

### Page 8

**gold and jade** — Precious things, used here for wealth.

**arrogancy** — Pride that looks down on others and forgets danger.

**withdraw into obscurity** — Step back from fame and attention after the work is done.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Kongkong Village would read by lamplight that night, and an elder from the next village was coming to tell stories.

Ahe was given an important task: prepare the lamps, pencils, and small reading tables. His heart lit up, as if he too had become a lamp.

### Page 10

"Tonight must be the best," Ahe thought.

He filled the first lamp to the very brim. The oil swelled at the edge like a thin layer of moonlight. Little Stone warned him, "Isn't that too full?"

### Page 11

"A little more oil makes a brighter lamp," Ahe said.

As soon as he lifted the lamp, his wrist shook and oil dripped down the side. To keep it from falling on the books, he froze, as if carrying a tiny bowl that must not breathe.

### Page 12

The old man handed him a cloth and did not scold.

"A lamp needs oil," he said, "and it also needs a little space. Before the lamp even shines, its overfullness has already tied you up."

### Page 13

Ahe was not quite convinced, so he sharpened the pencils.

He made every point thin and bright under the lamplight. The first stroke touched the paper, and the point snapped.

### Page 14

The second point snapped too. The third tore the paper.

Little Stone whispered, "Your pencils are so powerful they cannot write." This time Ahe did not laugh.

### Page 15

Before the reading began, Ahe stacked the books high, wanting everyone to see how much he had prepared.

But the top books slid down, and loose pages scattered across the floor. The children rushed to pick them up, while the visiting storyteller waited at the doorway.

### Page 16

At last Ahe stopped.

He poured out a little oil, sharpened the pencils shorter and steadier, and divided the books into small stacks. There was a little less of everything, yet the tables became usable; the lamps no longer brimmed, and their flames stood steady.

### Page 17

At night, halfway through the story, the visiting elder said, "Tonight was prepared carefully."

Ahe's heart jumped. He nearly stood up to say, "It was me." But he saw everyone's faces shining under the lamps, and no one was looking at him.

### Page 18

Suddenly he felt that this was just right.

The lamps were lit, and the story was moving. If he insisted on pouring his own name into it, the cup would overflow again.

## Return to the Original

### Page 19

Chapter Nine does not tell us not to work hard, and it does not tell us not to do things well.

It says: when effort reaches the fitting point, know how to stop; when sharpness is useful, do not keep sharpening; when the work is done, do not nail yourself to the credit.

### Page 20

Ahe wrote: Enough is not lack. Stopping is not losing.

Light the lamp, then let the lamp shine on everyone. Finish the work, then let the work go on. This may be one meaning of withdrawing when the work is done.
