# 第015-1章：像冬天过河的人

## 原文

### 第1页

古之善为士者，微妙玄通，深不可识。
夫唯不可识，故强为之容。

豫焉若冬涉川，犹兮若畏四邻，俨兮其若容，涣兮若冰之将释，敦兮其若朴，旷兮其若谷，混兮其若浊。

孰能浊以静之徐清？
孰能安以久动之徐生？

保此道者不欲盈，夫唯不盈，故能蔽不新成。

## 释义

### 第2页

第十五章说，古时候真正懂道的人，很深，很通，很难用外表看出来。

因为看不清，老子只好勉强用一串比喻描写他们。

### 第3页

他们像冬天过河的人，小心，不莽撞。

像怕四周都有动静的人，警觉，不自大。像到别人家做客的人，庄重，不放肆。

### 第4页

他们又像快要融化的冰，不僵硬。

像没有雕刻过的木头，厚实朴素。像山谷一样空旷，能容回声。也像浑水一样，不把自己装得很清亮。

### 第5页

“孰能浊以静之徐清”，是说浑水若安静下来，泥沙会慢慢沉下去。

人心也一样。越急着清楚，越容易搅浑；肯静一静，清楚会慢慢回来。

### 第6页

“孰能安以久动之徐生”，是说安定不是死住不动。

真正安稳以后，还要能慢慢起作用，像春水化开，像种子发芽，慢而有生机。

### 第7页

最后说“不欲盈”。

这样的人不把自己装满，不急着表现成完整、崭新、了不起。正因为不满，反而能长久。

## 难词注解

### 第8页

“微妙玄通”指的是思想细微、深远，又能贯通许多事情。

“豫焉”说的是小心迟疑，不贸然冲过去的样子。

“冬涉川”指的是冬天过河。那时水冷冰滑，必须谨慎。

### 第9页

“俨兮”指的是庄重、端正的样子。

“敦兮其若朴”的意思是厚实得像未雕刻的木头。

“混兮其若浊”的意思是外表像浑水，不故意显得清高。

## 正文

### 第10页

冬天，空空村的小溪结了一层薄冰。

阿禾的纸鸢被风吹到对岸的柳枝上，红尾巴在冷风里一摆一摆。

### 第11页

小石头跺脚：“我跑过去拿！”

他刚迈出去，冰面发出细细的“喀”一声。老人立刻伸手拦住他。

### 第12页

“河不像晒谷场。”老人说。

“晒谷场你跑快一点，最多摔一跤；冬天过河，脚下一错，冷水会从鞋口灌进来。”

### 第13页

老人拿一根长竹竿，先探一探冰边。

他没有马上下脚，而是看水色，听冰声，又找石头露出的浅处。孩子们站在岸边，第一次觉得“慢”也有重量。

### 第14页

老人一步一步走。

每一步都不像害怕得不敢动，也不像逞强地乱冲。他把竹竿点在前面，脚跟着过去，像是在和河商量。

### 第15页

到了对岸，他没有急着摘纸鸢。

他先站稳，再伸手。柳枝弹了一下，雪粒落到他的肩上。他笑了笑，把纸鸢解下来。

### 第16页

回来时，阿禾问：“您是不是很怕？”

老人说：“怕一点，正好。完全不怕，脚就会替头脑做主；怕得不能动，也过不了河。”

### 第17页

回到屋里，老人倒了一杯井水。

刚打上来的水有点浑，细沙在杯里翻。小石头想用筷子搅：“我帮它快点清。”

### 第18页

老人轻轻按住他的手。

“你一搅，它更浑。让它放一会儿。”孩子们看着杯子，沙粒慢慢落下，水面一点一点亮起来。

### 第19页

阿禾忽然懂了。

冬天过河要慢，浑水变清也要慢。不是拖拉，是不让急心把路弄坏。

### 第20页

晚上，他把纸鸢放在桌上。

红尾巴有一点破，竹骨也旧了。阿禾没有急着做一个全新的，只用细线把破处补好。旧纸鸢第二天照样能飞。

## 回扣

### 第21页

第十五章说“豫焉若冬涉川”。

阿禾再想那条溪，知道这不是胆小。真正懂水的人，知道什么时候可以快，什么时候必须慢。

### 第22页

“孰能浊以静之徐清”，也不只是杯里的水。

心里有气、有急、有怕，就像泥沙翻起来。越搅越浑，肯停一停，事情会露出底。

### 第23页

“保此道者不欲盈”，像那只旧纸鸢。

不必每次破一点就丢掉，不必样样都要崭新圆满。能修补，能留余地，旧东西也能继续飞。

### 第24页

阿禾写下：真正稳的人，不抢着显得勇敢。

他像冬天过河，心里有敬畏，脚下有分寸。慢一点，不是少了力量，是让力量找到路。

# Chapter 015-1: Like Someone Crossing a River in Winter

## Original Text

### Page 1

The skilful masters (of the Tao) in old times, with a subtle and exquisite penetration, comprehended its mysteries, and were deep (also) so as to elude men's knowledge.  As they were thus beyond men's knowledge, I will make an effort to describe of what sort they appeared to be.

Shrinking looked they like those who wade through a stream in winter; irresolute like those who are afraid of all around them; grave like a guest (in awe of his host); evanescent like ice that is melting away; unpretentious like wood that has not been fashioned into anything; vacant like a valley, and dull like muddy water.

Who can (make) the muddy water (clear)?  Let it be still, and it will gradually become clear.  Who can secure the condition of rest?  Let movement go on, and the condition of rest will gradually arise.

They who preserve this method of the Tao do not wish to be full (of themselves).  It is through their not being full of themselves that they can afford to seem worn and not appear new and complete.

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Chapter Fifteen says that those who truly understood the Dao in ancient times were deep, subtle, and hard to know from the outside.

Because they could not be fully seen, Laozi could only describe them with a string of images.

### Page 3

They were like people crossing a river in winter: careful, not reckless.

Like people alert to movements around them: watchful, not arrogant. Like guests in another's home: grave and respectful, not careless.

### Page 4

They were also like ice about to melt: not rigid.

Like uncarved wood: thick and plain. Like a valley: open enough to hold echoes. Like muddy water: not pretending to be bright and clear.

### Page 5

"Let muddy water be still and it will gradually become clear" means that when muddy water rests, the silt slowly sinks.

The heart is similar. The more we hurry to become clear, the more we stir it. If we can be still, clarity slowly returns.

### Page 6

"Rest and movement gradually arise" means that steadiness is not dead stillness.

After true rest, one must be able to act slowly and livingly, like spring water melting and seeds sprouting.

### Page 7

Finally, Laozi says such people do not wish to be full.

They do not fill themselves up or hurry to look complete, new, and impressive. Because they are not full, they can last.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 8

The phrase “subtle and exquisite” means very fine, deep, and difficult to notice.

Here, the word “shrinking” means being cautious, as if not rushing forward.

The phrase “wade through a stream in winter” means crossing cold water carefully.

### Page 9

The word “grave” means respectful and serious.

The phrase “uncarved wood” means plain wood that has not been shaped into a display object.

The phrase “muddy water” means water with silt stirred up. Here, it also describes a person who does not show off as pure or bright.

## Main Text

### Page 10

In winter, a thin skin of ice covered the stream in Kongkong Village.

Ahe's paper kite had been blown to a willow branch on the other side, its red tail swinging in the cold wind.

### Page 11

Little Stone stamped his foot. "I'll run over and get it!"

He had just stepped out when the ice made a thin cracking sound. The old man reached out and stopped him.

### Page 12

"A river is not the drying ground," the old man said.

"On the drying ground, if you run too fast, you may only fall. When crossing a winter stream, one wrong step may send cold water into your shoes."

### Page 13

The old man took a long bamboo pole and first tested the ice edge.

He did not step out at once. He looked at the color of the water, listened to the ice, and found the shallow places where stones showed. The children stood on the bank and felt for the first time that slowness had weight.

### Page 14

The old man walked step by step.

Each step was neither too frightened to move nor too bold to rush. He placed the pole ahead, then moved his foot, as if discussing the crossing with the river.

### Page 15

When he reached the other side, he did not hurry to pull down the kite.

He first stood firmly, then reached out. The willow branch sprang, and snow grains fell on his shoulder. He smiled and untied the kite.

### Page 16

When he returned, Ahe asked, "Were you afraid?"

The old man said, "A little fear is just right. With no fear at all, the feet make decisions for the head. With too much fear, one cannot cross."

### Page 17

Back indoors, the old man poured a cup of well water.

The newly drawn water was a little muddy, and fine sand turned inside the cup. Little Stone wanted to stir it with chopsticks. "I'll help it clear faster."

### Page 18

The old man gently held his hand.

"If you stir it, it becomes muddier. Let it sit for a while." The children watched the sand slowly fall and the water brighten little by little.

### Page 19

Ahe suddenly understood.

Crossing a winter stream takes time, and muddy water clearing takes time. This is not laziness; it is not letting hurry spoil the road.

### Page 20

That night, he laid the kite on the table.

The red tail was torn, and the bamboo frame was old. Ahe did not hurry to make a completely new kite. He mended the tear with fine thread. The old kite could still fly the next day.

## Return

### Page 21

Chapter Fifteen says, "like those who wade through a stream in winter."

When Ahe thought again of the stream, he knew this was not cowardice. One who understands water knows when to be quick and when to be slow.

### Page 22

"Let muddy water be still and it will gradually become clear" is not only about water in a cup.

Anger, hurry, and fear in the heart are like silt stirred up. The more we stir, the muddier it becomes. If we pause, the bottom of the matter can appear.

### Page 23

"They do not wish to be full" is like the old kite.

We do not have to throw something away whenever it is a little torn, and not everything must be brand new and complete. With repair and room, an old thing can keep flying.

### Page 24

Ahe wrote: a truly steady person does not rush to look brave.

He is like someone crossing a river in winter, with respect in the heart and measure under the feet. Slower is not weaker; it lets strength find the road.
