# 第014-3章：安静里听见的声音

## 典故引入

### 第1页

二十世纪有位美国作曲家约翰·凯奇，写过一首很特别的作品，名字常写作《4′33″》。

特别的地方在于：演出时，钢琴家坐在钢琴前，很久没有弹出一个音符。

### 第2页

小石头一听就笑了。

“不弹，也算一首曲子吗？”

### 第3页

老人没有急着回答。

他把一只空碗放在桌上，用筷子轻轻一碰。清清的一声响过，屋里忽然更静了。

### 第4页

“先别说话。”老人说。

孩子们坐下来。起初，安静像一件不合身的衣服，大家都想动一动，咳一声，笑一下。

### 第5页

过了一会儿，他们听见柴火在灶里轻轻裂开。

又听见窗外竹叶碰到竹叶，听见远处有人把水桶放到地上，桶底“咚”地一声。

### 第6页

这些声音刚才也在。

只是说话声、笑声、脚步声把它们盖住了。安静一来，它们才像小鱼一样浮到水面。

### 第7页

小石头压低声音：“原来安静不是空的。”

老人说：“安静像一间空屋。它自己不说话，却让别的声音进来。”

### 第8页

阿禾闭上眼。

他听见自己的呼吸，一进一出；又听见衣袖碰到膝盖的轻响。声音很小，小到平时根本不会被他当成声音。

### 第9页

他忽然想到第十四章。

“听之不闻”，不一定是完全没有声音。也可能是太细，太远，太不抢人，只有耳朵不忙时才遇得到。

### 第10页

老人说，凯奇那首作品像一个问题。

当我们等不到熟悉的旋律，会不会以为一切都没有发生？当舞台上没有明显的声音，会不会忘了整座厅堂其实正在响？

### 第11页

孩子们在学堂里也试了一次。

大家坐好，不故意制造声音。前几息，总有人想笑。后来，笑意落下去，学堂一点一点打开。

### 第12页

他们听见扫帚掠过地面。

听见一只笔从桌边滚到书上。听见阿禾的肚子咕噜一声。全班笑了，那笑声短短的，也成了这段安静的一部分。

### 第13页

这一次，小石头没有说“这不算”。

他小声说：“如果一直敲鼓，我就听不见这些。”

### 第14页

老人点头：“所以，不是只有响亮的才存在。”

“有些东西很细，细到你追它，它就退；你安静，它才来。”

## 回扣

### 第15页

第十四章说“听之不闻名曰希”。

“希”不是死寂，也不只是没有。它像很稀很细的声音，平时被热闹盖住，静下来才露一点边。

### 第16页

“视之不见”“搏之不得”也像这样。

你看不见安静的形状，抓不住它的身体。可没有它，柴火、竹叶、水桶、呼吸，就没有地方被听见。

### 第17页

这就是“无状之状，无物之象”的一点影子。

没有固定样子，不等于没有作用。没有站到舞台中央，不等于不在场。

### 第18页

阿禾写下：耳朵太忙，就听不见细的声音。

心也一样。安静一点，世界不是变少了，是有些很轻的真实终于走近了。

# Chapter 014-3: Sounds Heard Inside Quiet

## Allusion

### Page 1

In the twentieth century, the American composer John Cage made a very unusual work, often written as 4'33".

What made it unusual was this: during the performance, the pianist sat at the piano for a long time without playing a note.

### Page 2

Little Stone laughed as soon as he heard this.

"If he did not play, can it still be a piece of music?"

### Page 3

The old man did not hurry to answer.

He set an empty bowl on the table and touched it lightly with a chopstick. After the clear sound passed, the room suddenly seemed quieter.

### Page 4

"Do not speak yet," the old man said.

The children sat down. At first, quiet felt like clothing that did not fit. Everyone wanted to move, cough, or laugh.

### Page 5

After a while, they heard firewood crack softly in the stove.

Then they heard bamboo leaves touching bamboo leaves outside, and someone far away setting a bucket on the ground with a low thump.

### Page 6

These sounds had already been there.

They had only been covered by talking, laughing, and footsteps. When quiet came, they rose to the surface like small fish.

### Page 7

Little Stone lowered his voice. "So quiet is not empty."

The old man said, "Quiet is like an empty room. It does not speak, but it lets other sounds enter."

### Page 8

Ahe closed his eyes.

He heard his own breath going in and out, and the small sound of his sleeve touching his knee. The sounds were so small that he usually would not have counted them as sounds.

### Page 9

He suddenly thought of Chapter Fourteen.

"Listening and not hearing" does not always mean there is no sound at all. It may mean something is so fine, far, and unpushy that busy ears never meet it.

### Page 10

The old man said Cage's work was like a question.

When the familiar melody does not come, do we think nothing has happened? When the stage gives no obvious sound, do we forget that the whole hall is sounding?

### Page 11

The children tried it once in the schoolroom too.

Everyone sat without making sounds on purpose. For the first few breaths, someone always wanted to laugh. Then the laughter settled, and the room opened little by little.

### Page 12

They heard a broom pass over the floor.

They heard a pencil roll from the desk edge onto a book. They heard Ahe's stomach rumble. The whole class laughed, and that brief laughter became part of the quiet too.

### Page 13

This time, Little Stone did not say, "That does not count."

He whispered, "If drums kept beating, I would not hear these."

### Page 14

The old man nodded. "So it is not only loud things that exist."

"Some things are very fine. When you chase them, they move away. When you become quiet, they come."

## Return

### Page 15

Chapter Fourteen says, "We listen to it, and we do not hear it."

The inaudible is not dead silence, and not merely nothing. It is like a very thin sound, usually covered by liveliness, showing only a small edge when things grow quiet.

### Page 16

"We look and do not see" and "we grasp and do not hold" are like this too.

You cannot see quiet's shape or grasp its body. But without it, firewood, bamboo leaves, buckets, and breath have no room to be heard.

### Page 17

This is a small shadow of "the form of the formless" and "the semblance of the invisible."

Having no fixed shape does not mean having no effect. Not standing at the center of the stage does not mean not being present.

### Page 18

Ahe wrote: when ears are too busy, they cannot hear fine sounds.

The heart is the same. When it becomes a little quieter, the world has not become smaller; some very light truths have finally come near.
