# 第011-1章：空出来，才用得上

## 原文

### 第1页

三十辐共一毂，当其无，有车之用。
埏埴以为器，当其无，有器之用。

凿户牖以为室，当其无，有室之用。
故有之以为利，无之以为用。

## 释义

### 第2页

第十一章专门夸“空”。

它不是说什么都没有才好。它说：许多看得见的东西，真正能用，常常靠中间那一点看不见的空。

### 第3页

车轮有木头，有辐条，有外圈。

可车轮能转，是因为中心留了一个洞，让车轴穿过去。那个洞没有木头，却让整只车轮活了。

### 第4页

陶土很有用，能捏成碗、罐、缸。

可是如果一只罐子是实心的，再好看的花纹也装不了水。罐子能装东西，是因为肚子里空着。

### 第5页

房子有墙、有梁、有屋顶。

可人能住进去，是因为房子里面有空处，也因为门窗留了空，让人进出，让光和风进来。

### 第6页

“有之以为利”，是说木头、泥土、墙壁这些看得见的东西，能给我们便利。

“无之以为用”，是说空洞、空肚、空屋这些看不见的地方，才让便利真正派上用场。

## 难词注解

### 第7页

**三十辐**：古代车轮上的许多辐条，从外圈伸向中心。

**毂**：车轮中心的部分，里面有孔，车轴从这里穿过。

**当其无**：正是因为那里空着。

### 第8页

**埏埴**：揉泥、捏泥，用泥土做器物。

**户牖**：门和窗。

**利**：带来方便的形体。

**用**：真正发生作用的地方。

## 正文

### 第9页

秋收前，空空村要修一辆旧木车。

这辆车要拉稻束、南瓜和一筐筐豆荚。它站在晒谷场边，两个车轮都歪着，好像一位老人正在揉酸腿。

### 第10页

阿禾看见车轮中间有一个黑黑的洞，皱起眉头。

“这里最危险。”他说，“洞就是缺口。缺口不补，车轮一定不结实。”

### 第11页

他找来一块木头，削成圆塞子，塞进车轮中心。

小石头还帮他往缝里抹泥。两个孩子拍着手说：“这下实在了！”

### 第12页

牛被套上车，老人轻轻一喊，牛往前走。

车没动。牛又走一步，绳子绷紧，车轮却像生了根，死死赖在原地。

### 第13页

阿禾急得去推。

车身晃了一下，装在车上的空竹筐全倒了，咚咚咚滚了一地。小石头摸摸后脑勺：“它结实是结实，可是不会转了。”

### 第14页

修车的老木匠把圆塞子拔出来，露出原来的孔。

他把车轴穿进去，车轮轻轻一推，就转了半圈。老木匠说：“车轮中心若不空，车轴从哪里过？”

### 第15页

阿禾有点不服气。

下午，他跟着陶匠学捏罐子。他把泥团拍得厚厚实实，捏出一个圆圆的罐形，还在外面按了几朵花。

### 第16页

陶匠舀水往里倒。

水一碰罐口就溅了出来。阿禾这才发现，自己捏的是一个实心泥疙瘩，外头像罐子，里面却没有肚子。

### 第17页

陶匠把泥重新揉软，手指按进去，慢慢转，慢慢提。

泥壁变薄，肚子空出来。一只不那么花哨的罐子站在桌上，却能稳稳接住半瓢水。

### 第18页

傍晚起风，孩子们把工具搬进新修的小屋。

这间屋子墙很厚，门也做得紧。可大家刚进去，就觉得闷。油灯烟子散不开，眼睛被熏得直眨。

### 第19页

老人拿来凿子，在墙上开了一扇小窗。

风先进来，烟先出去，接着一条晚光落在地上。屋子还是那间屋子，却忽然像醒了。

### 第20页

阿禾站在门口，想起车轮的洞、陶罐的肚子、小屋的窗。

他这才明白：空不是破，空也不是少。有些空，是让东西转起来、装进去、住得下。

## 回扣

### 第21页

第十一章说“三十辐共一毂，当其无，有车之用”。

阿禾再看那只车轮，不只看见木头和辐条，也看见中心那个安安静静的空孔。它不抢眼，却把整车的重量接住了。

### 第22页

“埏埴以为器，当其无，有器之用。”

泥土成了罐子，是“有”；罐子里面空出来，是“无”。没有泥壁，水会散；没有空肚，水也进不去。

### 第23页

“凿户牖以为室，当其无，有室之用。”

墙能挡风雨，门窗却让人和光进来。只有墙，没有空处，那不是房子，像一只大盒子。

### 第24页

阿禾写下：有形的东西给我边界，空着的地方给我用处。

一颗心也要这样。全塞满了自己的想法，就听不进别人；留一点空，新的风才进得来。

# Chapter 011-1: Empty, So It Can Be Used

## Original Text

### Page 1

The thirty spokes unite in the one nave; but it is on the empty space (for the axle), that the use of the wheel depends.  Clay is fashioned into vessels; but it is on their empty hollowness, that their use depends.

The door and windows are cut out (from the walls) to form an apartment; but it is on the empty space (within), that its use depends.  Therefore, what has a (positive) existence serves for profitable adaptation, and what has not that for (actual) usefulness.

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Chapter Eleven praises emptiness.

It does not say that having nothing is always best. It says that many visible things become useful because of an unseen space inside them.

### Page 3

A wheel has wood, spokes, and an outer rim.

But the wheel can turn because the center has an opening for the axle. That opening has no wood in it, yet it makes the whole wheel come alive.

### Page 4

Clay is useful. It can be shaped into bowls, jars, and vats.

But if a jar is solid inside, even beautiful patterns cannot make it hold water. A jar can carry things because its belly is empty.

### Page 5

A house has walls, beams, and a roof.

But people can live in it because there is space inside, and because doors and windows leave openings for people, light, and air.

### Page 6

"What has existence" means the wood, clay, and walls we can see and touch.

"What has not" means the hollow, the empty belly, and the open room. These unseen spaces let the visible things truly work.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 7

**spokes** — The rods of a wheel, reaching from the rim toward the center.

**nave** — The center part of a wheel, where the axle passes through.

**empty space** — A space left open so something can move, enter, or be held.

### Page 8

**vessel** — A bowl, jar, or pot that can hold something.

**apartment** — Here, a room or dwelling space.

**profitable adaptation** — The helpful shape or material of a thing.

**usefulness** — The real working power of the thing.

## Main Text

### Page 9

Before the autumn harvest, Kongkong Village had to repair an old wooden cart.

The cart would carry rice bundles, pumpkins, and baskets of beans. It stood beside the drying ground, its two wheels tilted like an old man rubbing sore legs.

### Page 10

Ahe saw the dark hole in the center of a wheel and frowned.

"This is the weakest place," he said. "A hole is a gap. If we do not fill it, the wheel will not be strong."

### Page 11

He found a piece of wood, carved it into a round plug, and pushed it into the center of the wheel.

Little Stone helped smear mud around the cracks. The two children clapped. "Now it is solid!"

### Page 12

The ox was harnessed to the cart. The old man called softly, and the ox stepped forward.

The cart did not move. The ox stepped again, the rope tightened, but the wheel stayed rooted to the ground.

### Page 13

Ahe hurried over and pushed.

The cart shook, and all the empty bamboo baskets rolled out with a hollow thumping sound. Little Stone rubbed the back of his head. "It is solid, but it cannot turn."

### Page 14

The old wheelwright pulled out the round plug and uncovered the original hole.

He slid the axle through it. With one light push, the wheel turned half a circle. "If the wheel's center is not empty," he said, "where can the axle pass?"

### Page 15

Ahe was not fully convinced.

That afternoon, he learned to make a jar with the potter. He patted the clay thick and firm, shaped it into a round jar, and pressed little flowers on the outside.

### Page 16

The potter poured water toward it.

The water splashed off the mouth. Only then did Ahe see that he had made a solid lump of clay. It looked like a jar outside, but had no belly inside.

### Page 17

The potter softened the clay again, pressed fingers into it, turned it slowly, and lifted it slowly.

The wall grew thinner, and the belly opened. A less fancy jar stood on the table, yet it could steadily receive half a ladle of water.

### Page 18

At dusk, the children carried tools into a newly repaired shed.

The walls were thick, and the door shut tight. But as soon as everyone went inside, the room felt stuffy. Lamp smoke could not leave, and eyes began to blink.

### Page 19

The old man brought a chisel and opened a small window in the wall.

Wind entered first, smoke went out, and then a stripe of evening light fell on the floor. The shed was still the same shed, but it seemed to wake up.

### Page 20

Ahe stood at the doorway and thought of the wheel's hole, the jar's belly, and the shed's window.

He understood at last: emptiness is not always damage, and not always lack. Some emptiness lets things turn, receive, and be lived in.

## Return

### Page 21

Chapter Eleven says that thirty spokes share one nave, and the wheel works because of the empty space.

When Ahe looked again at the wheel, he saw not only wood and spokes, but also the quiet opening at the center. It did not show off, yet it received the weight of the whole cart.

### Page 22

Clay becomes a jar: that is the visible part.

The hollow inside lets water enter: that is the useful emptiness. Without clay walls, water scatters. Without an empty belly, water cannot enter.

### Page 23

Walls can keep out wind and rain, but doors and windows let people and light come in.

A house with only walls and no openings is not truly a house. It is a large box.

### Page 24

Ahe wrote: visible things give me edges; empty spaces give me use.

A heart needs this too. If it is packed full of its own thoughts, it cannot hear others. Leave a little space, and a new wind can enter.
