# 第000-3章：这本书来到耳边

## 第1页

第三天，阿禾带来一片树叶。

叶子边缘有一点卷，叶脉像细细的小路。他把它举给老人看："同一棵树上的叶子，每片都不一样。可是一看，又知道它们是同一棵树的叶子。同一本书，也会这样吗？"

老人笑了："会。昨天我们讲书怎样留下来，今天讲人怎样读懂它。"

## 第2页

在很长一段时间里，人们主要靠传世本读《老子》。

"传世本"不是刚从地下挖出来的古本，而是靠一代代人抄写、收藏、刊刻、校对，慢慢传到今天的本子。

这些本子能走到我们手里，除了抄书的人，也离不开一代代解释它的人。其中有两条读法特别重要：一条和河上公有关，一条和王弼有关。

## 第3页

河上公是汉代传说中的隐士。

故事说，他住在河边的小屋里。就算皇帝来请教，他也不慌不忙，不急着摆出很大的排场。

这个故事多半带着传说的光。不过，河上公注本确实流传很广，很多人就是顺着它的解释，第一次走进《老子》的。

## 第4页

河上公常把《老子》读成一本教人治国、养身、守心的书。

遇到难懂的句子，他一章一章解释，像在弯弯曲曲的小路旁插上木牌：这里慢一点，那里别踩空；这里讲少贪欲，那里讲别扰民；这里提醒人把身体和心照看好。

阿禾说："这样后来的人走过去，就不容易迷路了。"

## 第5页

另一个重要人物叫王弼。

他生活在三国时代，很年轻，也很聪明。他只活了二十三岁，却写下影响很深的《老子注》。

小鸟睁大眼睛："二十三岁就能让后来那么多人记住？"

老人点点头："有些人的一生不长，但留下的想法能走很远。"

## 第6页

王弼特别重视"有"和"无"，也重视"自然"和"无为"。

"无为"不是躺着什么都不做。老人说，"该做的事还是要做，只是别乱伸手，别凭自己的急脾气添乱。"

"比如照顾一棵小苗，你要浇水、松土，挡住要踩过来的脚；但你不能天天捏着叶子往外拉，替它长大。"

## 第7页

老人接着说："我们这本书里的中文原文，主要依据今天常见的王弼系统。"

"遇到差异比较重要的地方，也会参考马王堆帛书、郭店竹简和校勘本。古书走了这么远，不能假装它只有一条来路。"

阿禾写下：读古书，要尊重它走过的路。

## 第8页

"每个年代的人，读到的都一样吗？"小兔问。

"不完全一样。"老人说，"乱世里的人，可能先读到怎样让心安静；做官的人，可能先读到不要贪、不要扰民；总想赢过同学的人，可能会被它提醒：慢一点，不必每次都抢在最前面。"

"同一本书，放到不同的人手里，会先碰到他们心里最需要的地方。"

## 第9页

小石头问："别的国家的人也读《道德经》吗？"

老人说："读。从十七、十八世纪开始，传教士和汉学家陆续把它介绍到欧洲。后来，它被译成许多语言，走到世界很多地方。"

阿禾想，一本从竹简和丝帛上来的书，竟然能跑得比青牛还远。

## 第10页

"这本书里的英文原文，采用英国汉学家理雅各的译本。"

老人在地上写下这个名字："一八九一年，他出版了完整英译本。"

"这个译本年代早，译得认真，而且已经进入公共领域，大家可以放心使用。我们一边读它，一边也记得：翻译只是过河的一座桥，不是河本身。"

## 第11页

"翻译'道'尤其难。"老人说。

"有的译者用英文里的'道路'或'小径'来翻译，都像在说一条路。可'道'不只是脚下能走的路。它也像万物运行的方式，像事情本来会生长、变化、回到安静的样子。"

"所以有些译者干脆按照中文读音写成'道'，让这个字保留一点陌生感，也给读者留下慢慢体会的空间。"

## 第12页

阿禾又想起那头大象。

摸到腿的人叫它"粗柱子怪"，不算全错，可是不够。叫它"大象"好一些，但"大象"也只是名字。

真正的大象会呼吸，会走路，会用鼻子卷青草，还会在热天往身上甩泥水。名字能帮我们开始认识它，却不能替我们真的看见它。

## 第13页

"那我们读《道德经》，是为了背出标准答案吗？"阿禾问。

老人摇摇头："不是。是为了学老子看水。"

"不急着抢第一，不急着给别人下结论。遇到一句话，先多看一会儿；遇到一件事，先多想一层。能轻一点处理的时候，就别把事情弄得太重。"

## 第14页

夕阳下，大家沿着田埂往回走。

老人说："阿禾，你有点像尹喜。你总请一个老人把话留下来。"

阿禾低头看着自己的名字本。那些空白的页，忽然像一片片摊开的竹简。它们等着字，也等着他慢慢长大。

## 第15页

夜里，阿禾写下最后一行：

*老子不是把道理讲完的人。他更像把门推开的人。*

门开着。

明天，他们就要正式走进第一章。

# Chapter 000-3: This Book Reaches Our Ears

## Page 1

On the third day, Ahe brought a leaf.

Its edge curled a little, and its veins looked like tiny paths. He held it up to the old man. "Leaves on the same tree are all different. But when you see them, you still know they came from the same tree. Can the same book be like that too?"

The old man smiled. "Yes. Yesterday we talked about how the book stayed in the world. Today we will talk about how people learned to read it."

## Page 2

For a very long time, people read the *Laozi* through received texts.

A received text is not an old manuscript newly dug from the ground. It is a version passed down through copying, collecting, printing, and checking, generation after generation.

For these texts to reach us, copyists mattered. So did the people who explained the book. Two lines of reading became especially important: one connected with Heshang Gong, and one connected with Wang Bi.

## Page 3

Heshang Gong was a legendary hermit of the Han dynasty.

Stories say he lived in a small hut by the river. Even when an emperor came to ask for teaching, he did not hurry to make a grand show.

That story probably carries the glow of legend. Still, the Heshang Gong commentary truly spread widely, and many people first entered the *Laozi* by following its explanations.

## Page 4

Heshang Gong often read the *Laozi* as a book about governing, caring for the body, and keeping the heart steady.

When a sentence was hard to understand, he explained it chapter by chapter, like placing wooden signs beside a winding path: slow down here, be careful there; here it speaks of wanting less, there it speaks of disturbing people less; here it reminds us to care for the body and the heart.

"Then later readers would not lose their way so easily," Ahe said.

## Page 5

Another important reader was Wang Bi.

He lived during the Three Kingdoms period. He was very young and very brilliant. He died at only twenty-three, but his commentary on the *Laozi* shaped readers for centuries.

Little Bird opened her eyes wide. "Only twenty-three, and later people still remembered him?"

The old man nodded. "Some lives are not long, but their ideas can travel far."

## Page 6

Wang Bi cared deeply about "being" and "nonbeing," and about "naturalness" and *wuwei*.

"*Wuwei* does not mean lying around and doing nothing," said the old man. "The things that should be done still need doing. It means not grabbing at everything, and not making trouble because of your own impatience."

"When you care for a seedling, you water it, loosen the soil, and keep feet from stepping on it. But you cannot pinch its leaves and pull them every day, trying to grow in its place."

## Page 7

The old man went on. "The Chinese original in this book mainly follows the common Wang Bi line."

"Where differences matter, we also check the Mawangdui silk manuscripts, the Guodian bamboo slips, and collated editions. An old book has traveled a long way. We should not pretend it came by only one path."

Ahe wrote: to read an old book, respect the road it has traveled.

## Page 8

"Does every age find the same thing in it?" Little Rabbit asked.

"Not exactly," said the old man. "People in troubled times may first notice how it quiets the heart. Officials may first hear its warning against greed and disturbing the people. Someone always trying to beat classmates may feel it saying: slow down; you do not have to be first every time."

"The same book, placed in different hands, often touches first what those hands most need."

## Page 9

Little Stone asked, "Do people in other countries read the *Dao De Jing* too?"

"Yes," said the old man. "From the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries onward, missionaries and sinologists introduced it to Europe. Later, it was translated into many languages and traveled to many parts of the world."

Ahe thought: a book that began on bamboo slips and silk had gone even farther than the blue-green ox.

## Page 10

"For the English original text, this book uses James Legge's translation."

The old man wrote another name in the dirt. "His Chinese name was Li Yage. In eighteen ninety-one, he published a complete English translation."

"His version is early, careful, and now in the public domain, so people may use it freely. We can read it while remembering this: a translation is a bridge across the river, not the river itself."

## Page 11

"Still, translating Dao is especially difficult," said the old man.

"Way sounds like a road. Path also sounds like a road. But Dao is more than a road under your feet. It is also like the way things move, grow, change, and return to quiet."

"That is why some translators simply write Dao. The word keeps a little strangeness, and gives readers room to understand it slowly."

## Page 12

Ahe remembered the elephant again.

The person who touched its leg and called it a "thick-pillar creature" was not completely wrong, but the name was not enough. "Elephant" was better, but "elephant" was still only a name.

The real elephant breathes, walks, curls grass with its trunk, and splashes mud on itself on hot days. A name can help us begin to know it, but it cannot take the place of really seeing it.

## Page 13

"Then do we read the *Dao De Jing* to memorize the right answers?" Ahe asked.

The old man shook his head. "No. We read it to learn how Laozi watched water."

"Do not rush to be first. Do not rush to decide what someone else is. When you meet a sentence, look a little longer. When you meet a problem, think one layer deeper. When something can be handled lightly, do not make it heavier than it needs to be."

## Page 14

At sunset, they walked home along the raised path between the fields.

"Ahe, you are a little like Yin Xi," said the old man. "You keep asking an old man to leave words behind."

Ahe looked down at his book of names. Its blank pages suddenly seemed like bamboo slips spread open before him. They were waiting for words, and also waiting for him to grow.

## Page 15

That night, Ahe wrote one last line:

*Laozi was not the person who finished explaining Dao. He was more like someone who opened a door.*

The door stood open.

Tomorrow, they would finally enter Chapter One.
