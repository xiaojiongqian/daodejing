# 第000-3章：这本书来到耳边

## 第1页

第三天，阿禾带来一片树叶。

叶子边缘有一点卷，叶脉像细细的小路。他把它举给老人看："同一棵树上的叶子，每片都不一样。同一本书，也会这样吗？"

老人笑了："会。今天我们讲一讲，这本书怎样被人读懂。"

## 第2页

在很长一段时间里，人们主要靠传世本读《老子》。

"传世本"就是没有从地下新挖出来，而是靠一代代人抄写、收藏、刊刻，慢慢传到今天的本子。

这些本子里，有两条读法特别重要：一条和河上公有关，一条和王弼有关。

## 第3页

河上公是汉代传说中的隐士。

故事说，他住在河边的小屋里。就算皇帝来请教，他也不慌不忙，不急着出来迎接。

这个故事多半带着传说的光，可河上公注本确实流传很广，很多人通过它读《老子》。

## 第4页

河上公常把《老子》读成一本教人治国、养身、守心的书。

遇到难懂的句子，他一章一章解释，像在弯弯曲曲的小路旁插上木牌：这里要慢一点，那里别踩空。

阿禾说："这样后来的人走过去，就不容易迷路了。"

## 第5页

另一个重要人物叫王弼。

他生活在三国时代，年轻得让人惊讶，也聪明得让人惊讶。他只活了二十三岁，却写下影响很深的《老子注》。

有些人的一生不长，但留下的光能照很久。

## 第6页

王弼特别重视"有"和"无"，也重视"自然"和"无为"。

"无为"不是躺着不做事。老人说，"它更像不乱伸手、不凭私心添乱。"

"比如照顾一棵小苗，你要浇水、松土、挡住踩来的脚；但你不能天天把叶子往外拉，替它长大。"

## 第7页

老人接着说："我们这本书里的中文原文，主要依据通行的王弼系统。"

"遇到有差异的地方，也会参考马王堆帛书、郭店竹简和校勘本。古书走了很远，不能假装只有一条来路。"

阿禾写下：读古书，要尊重它走过的路。

## 第8页

"每个年代的人，读到的都一样吗？"小兔问。

"不完全一样。"老人说，"乱世里的人，可能先读到安静；做官的人，可能先读到不要贪；急着赢的人，可能被它提醒慢下来。"

"好书像光。它照进不同的屋子，照亮的地方也会不同。"

## 第9页

小石头问："别的国家的人也读《道德经》吗？"

老人说："读。从十七、十八世纪开始，传教士和汉学家把它介绍到欧洲。后来，它被译成许多语言，走到世界很多地方。"

阿禾想，一本从竹简上来的书，竟然能跑得比青牛还远。

## 第10页

"这本书的英文原文，采用 James Legge 的译本。"

老人在地上写下另一个名字："他的中文名叫理雅各。1891年，他出版了完整英译本。"

"这个译本年代早，译得认真，而且已经进入公共领域，大家可以放心使用。"

## 第11页

"不过，翻译'道'很难。"老人说。

"译成 Way，像路；译成 Path，也像路。可'道'不只是脚下能走的路。"

"所以有些译者干脆写 Dao，让这个字保留一点陌生感，也给读者留下慢慢体会的空间。"

## 第12页

阿禾又想起那头大象。

摸到腿的人叫它"粗柱子怪"，不算全错，可是不够。叫它"大象"好一些，但"大象"也只是名字。

真正的大象会呼吸，会走路，会用鼻子卷青草，还会在热天往身上甩泥水。

## 第13页

"那我们读《道德经》，是为了背出标准答案吗？"阿禾问。

老人摇摇头："不是。是为了学老子看水。"

"不急着抢第一，不急着给别人下结论。多看一会儿，多想一层。能轻一点的时候，就别把事情弄得太重。"

## 第14页

夕阳下，大家沿着田埂往回走。

老人说："阿禾，你有点像尹喜。你总请一个老人把话留下来。"

阿禾低头看着自己的名字本。那些空白的页，忽然像一片片摊开的竹简。

## 第15页

夜里，阿禾写下最后一行：

*老子不是把道理讲完的人。他更像把门推开的人。*

门开着。

明天，他们就要正式走进第一章。

# Chapter 000-3: This Book Reaches Our Ears

## Page 1

On the third day, Ahe brought a leaf.

Its edge curled a little, and its veins looked like tiny paths. He held it up to the old man. "Leaves on the same tree are all different. Can the same book be like that too?"

The old man smiled. "Yes. Today we will talk about how people learned to read this book."

## Page 2

For a very long time, people read the *Laozi* through received texts.

A received text is not a manuscript newly dug from the ground. It is a version passed down through copying, collecting, printing, and reading, generation after generation.

Among these versions, two lines of interpretation became especially important: one connected with Heshang Gong, and one connected with Wang Bi.

## Page 3

Heshang Gong was a legendary hermit of the Han dynasty.

Stories say he lived in a small hut by the river. Even when an emperor came to ask for teaching, he did not hurry out to greet him.

That story probably carries the glow of legend. But the Heshang Gong commentary truly spread widely, and many people read the *Laozi* through it.

## Page 4

Heshang Gong often read the *Laozi* as a book about governing, caring for the body, and keeping the heart steady.

When a sentence was hard to understand, he explained it chapter by chapter, like placing wooden signs beside a winding path: slow down here, be careful there.

"Then later readers would not lose their way so easily," Ahe said.

## Page 5

Another important reader was Wang Bi.

He lived during the Three Kingdoms period. He was surprisingly young and surprisingly brilliant. He died at only twenty-three, but his commentary on the *Laozi* shaped readers for centuries.

Some lives are not long, but the light they leave can travel far.

## Page 6

Wang Bi cared deeply about "being" and "nonbeing," and about "naturalness" and *wuwei*.

"*Wuwei* does not mean lying around and doing nothing," said the old man. "It is more like not grabbing at things, and not meddling out of selfishness."

"When you care for a seedling, you water it, loosen the soil, and keep feet from stepping on it. But you cannot pull its leaves every day and grow in its place."

## Page 7

The old man went on. "The Chinese original in this book mainly follows the common Wang Bi line."

"Where readings differ, we also check the Mawangdui silk manuscripts, the Guodian bamboo slips, and collated editions. An old book has traveled a long road. We should not pretend it came by only one path."

Ahe wrote: to read an old book, respect the road it has traveled.

## Page 8

"Does every age find the same thing in it?" Little Rabbit asked.

"Not exactly," said the old man. "People in troubled times may first notice its stillness. Officials may first hear its warning against greed. People in a hurry to win may feel it asking them to slow down."

"A good book is like light. It enters different rooms and brightens different corners."

## Page 9

Little Stone asked, "Do people in other countries read the *Dao De Jing* too?"

"Yes," said the old man. "From the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries onward, missionaries and sinologists introduced it to Europe. Later, it was translated into many languages and traveled to many parts of the world."

Ahe thought: a book that began on bamboo slips had gone even farther than the blue-green ox.

## Page 10

"For the English Original Text, this book uses James Legge's translation."

The old man wrote another name in the dirt. "His Chinese name was Li Yage. In 1891, he published a complete English translation."

"His version is early, careful, and now in the public domain, so people may use it freely."

## Page 11

"Still, translating Dao is difficult," said the old man.

"Way sounds like a road. Path also sounds like a road. But Dao is more than a road under your feet."

"That is why some translators simply write Dao. The word keeps a little strangeness, and gives readers room to understand it slowly."

## Page 12

Ahe remembered the elephant again.

The person who touched its leg and called it a "thick-pillar creature" was not completely wrong, but the name was not enough. "Elephant" was better, but "elephant" was still only a name.

The real elephant breathes, walks, curls grass with its trunk, and splashes mud on itself on hot days.

## Page 13

"Then do we read the *Dao De Jing* to memorize the right answers?" Ahe asked.

The old man shook his head. "No. We read it to learn how Laozi watched water."

"Do not rush to be first. Do not rush to decide what someone else is. Look a little longer. Think one layer deeper. When something can be handled lightly, do not make it heavier than it needs to be."

## Page 14

At sunset, they walked home along the raised path between the fields.

"Ahe, you are a little like Yin Xi," said the old man. "You keep asking an old man to leave words behind."

Ahe looked down at his book of names. Its blank pages suddenly seemed like bamboo slips spread open before him.

## Page 15

That night, Ahe wrote one last line:

*Laozi was not the person who finished explaining Dao. He was more like someone who opened a door.*

The door stood open.

Tomorrow, they would finally enter Chapter One.
