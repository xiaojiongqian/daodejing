# 第012-1章：眼睛吃太饱，心就饿了

## 原文

### 第1页

五色令人目盲，五音令人耳聋，五味令人口爽，驰骋畋猎令人心发狂，难得之货令人行妨。

是以圣人为腹不为目，故去彼取此。

## 释义

### 第2页

第十二章不是说颜色不好，音乐不好，饭菜的味道也不好。

老子说的是：如果眼睛、耳朵、舌头天天被太强、太多、太新奇的东西追着跑，人会越来越不安，反而看不清、听不清、尝不出。

### 第3页

“五色令人目盲”，不是眼睛真的一下子瞎了。

是说太多颜色晃来晃去，眼睛只顾追热闹，就看不见真正重要的东西。

### 第4页

“五音令人耳聋”，也不是耳朵马上坏了。

是说声音太满、太吵、太刺激，耳朵就听不见轻轻的提醒，也听不见别人话里的难过。

### 第5页

“五味令人口爽”的“爽”，在古文里有“差失”的意思。

味道太重，舌头会被惯坏。吃惯了浓甜浓咸，白粥的米香和清水的甘味就容易被错过。

### 第6页

“为腹不为目”，不是只管吃饱。

这里的“腹”，是身体和心真正需要的根本；“目”，是外面闪闪发亮、让人想追的诱惑。

## 难词注解

### 第7页

“五色”指的是各种好看的颜色。

“五音”指的是各种声音和音乐。

“五味”指的是酸、甜、苦、辣、咸等各种味道。

### 第8页

“驰骋畋猎”说的是骑马奔跑、打猎追逐。在这里，可以理解成过度追求刺激。

“难得之货”指的是稀奇、少见，让人特别想拥有的东西。

“行妨”的意思是让行为走偏，挡住正路。

## 正文

### 第9页

空空村一年一次的集市到了。

一早，村口挂起彩布，铜铃叮叮响，糖炒栗子的香味从街头滚到街尾。阿禾的脚刚踏进集市，眼睛就忙不过来了。

### 第10页

左边有会变色的纸风车，右边有能发亮的小玻璃珠。

前面有人敲鼓，后面有人吹笛，旁边还有卖糖人的喊：“龙形糖人，只剩三条！”

### 第11页

阿禾先买了一串最红的糖葫芦，又尝了一口最甜的蜜糕。

甜还没咽下去，他又被一面会反光的小镜子吸住了。镜子里太阳一闪，像有一条小鱼跳进他的眼睛。

### 第12页

小石头在后面喊：“阿禾，老人叫我们去帮忙搬米袋！”

鼓声一响，喊声被盖住。阿禾只听见摊主说：“这颗蓝珠子很少见，今天不买，明天就没了。”

### 第13页

他摸摸口袋，里面还有一小包豆种。

那是春天留给菜畦的种子。阿禾犹豫了一下，还是用它换了那颗蓝珠子。

### 第14页

傍晚，集市散了。

阿禾抱着一堆小玩意儿回家，头却胀胀的。晚饭端上来，是白米饭、青菜和一碗淡汤。他皱皱鼻子：“一点味道也没有。”

### 第15页

老人夹起一片青菜，慢慢嚼。

“你今天的舌头，被糖和蜜吵了一天。”老人说，“现在米香站在门口，它听不见了。”

### 第16页

阿禾想把蓝珠子拿出来给大家看，却发现小布袋空了。

他忽然想起那包豆种。心里一凉：春天能长出一畦豆苗的东西，被他换成了一个晚上闪一闪的东西。

### 第17页

第二天，他拿着蓝珠子去找摊主，摊主早已走了。

阿禾站在空摊位前，手心里的珠子还是很亮，可他一点也不想看了。

### 第18页

老人没有责备他，只递给他一碗热粥。

阿禾这一次吃得很慢。米粒软软地散开，一点淡淡的甜从舌头底下出来。他才发现，淡味不是没有味道，只是需要安静一点的舌头。

### 第19页

饭后，他把那些买来的小玩意儿收进盒子。

纸风车留一个，糖纸丢掉，蓝珠子放到窗边。他又去找小石头，一起把没有搬完的米袋挪进仓里。

### 第20页

仓门关上时，阿禾听见米袋落地的闷响，也听见老人轻轻咳了一声。

昨天那些声音都被鼓和叫卖声盖住了。今天村子安静下来，耳朵才又回到自己身边。

## 回扣

### 第21页

第十二章说“五色令人目盲，五音令人耳聋，五味令人口爽”。

阿禾知道，这不是反对颜色、音乐和好吃的东西。集市有它的热闹，糖也有它的甜。

### 第22页

问题在于，眼睛若一直追亮的，耳朵若一直追响的，舌头若一直追重的，心就会被外面牵着跑。

一跑急了，就会错过别人的呼唤，也会把能发芽的种子换成一颗会闪的珠子。

### 第23页

“圣人为腹不为目”，是把真正养人的东西放在前面。

米饭、睡眠、身体的安稳、心里的清楚、答应别人的事，这些看起来不花哨，却让人站得住。

### 第24页

阿禾写下：眼睛可以看热闹，但不能让热闹牵走我。

肚子知道什么能养人；心安静时，也会知道什么该留下，什么该放过。

# Chapter 012-1: When the Eyes Eat Too Much, the Heart Goes Hungry

## Original Text

### Page 1

Colour's five hues from th' eyes their sight will take;
Music's five notes the ears as deaf can make;
The flavours five deprive the mouth of taste;
The chariot course, and the wild hunting waste
Make mad the mind; and objects rare and strange,
Sought for, men's conduct will to evil change.

Therefore the sage seeks to satisfy (the craving of) the belly, and not the (insatiable longing of the) eyes.  He puts from him the latter, and prefers to seek the former.

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Chapter Twelve does not say that colors are bad, music is bad, or flavors are bad.

Laozi is saying that if the eyes, ears, and tongue are chased all day by things too strong, too many, and too new, a person becomes restless. Then seeing, hearing, and tasting become less clear.

### Page 3

"Five hues take away sight" does not mean the eyes suddenly become blind.

It means that when too many colors flash and pull, the eyes chase excitement and miss what truly matters.

### Page 4

"Five notes make the ears deaf" does not mean the ears break at once.

It means that when sound is too full, too loud, and too exciting, the ears cannot hear a quiet reminder or the sadness hidden in another person's words.

### Page 5

The line about flavors means that heavy tastes can make the mouth lose its natural knowing.

If the tongue is trained only by strong sweetness and saltiness, it may miss the fragrance of plain rice and the clean sweetness of water.

### Page 6

"For the belly, not for the eyes" does not mean only filling the stomach.

Here, the belly means what body and heart truly need. The eyes mean the shiny things outside that keep asking to be chased.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 7

The phrase “five hues” means many kinds of colors.

The phrase “five notes” means many kinds of sounds and music.

The phrase “five flavors” means sour, sweet, bitter, spicy, salty, and other tastes.

### Page 8

The phrase “wild hunting” means chasing excitement too fiercely.

The phrase “objects rare and strange” means unusual things that make people want to own them.

The word “conduct” means how a person behaves and chooses.

## Main Text

### Page 9

The yearly market came to Kongkong Village.

Early in the morning, colored cloths hung at the village gate, copper bells rang, and the smell of roasted chestnuts rolled from one end of the street to the other. As soon as Ahe stepped in, his eyes had too much work to do.

### Page 10

On the left were paper pinwheels that changed color. On the right were small glass beads that shone.

Ahead, someone beat a drum. Behind him, someone played a flute. Beside him, a candy seller called, "Dragon candy, only three left!"

### Page 11

Ahe first bought the reddest candied hawthorn, then tasted the sweetest honey cake.

Before the sweetness had gone down, a tiny shining mirror caught him. Sunlight flashed in it like a fish leaping into his eyes.

### Page 12

Little Stone called from behind, "Ahe, the old man asked us to help carry rice sacks!"

The drum covered the call. Ahe only heard the seller say, "This blue bead is rare. If you do not buy it today, it will be gone tomorrow."

### Page 13

He touched his pocket. Inside was a small packet of bean seeds.

They were seeds saved for the spring vegetable patch. Ahe hesitated, then traded them for the blue bead.

### Page 14

At dusk, the market closed.

Ahe carried a heap of small things home, but his head felt swollen. Supper was plain rice, greens, and light soup. He wrinkled his nose. "There is no taste."

### Page 15

The old man picked up a leaf of greens and chewed slowly.

"Your tongue has been shouted at by sugar and honey all day," he said. "Now the fragrance of rice stands at the door, and your tongue cannot hear it."

### Page 16

Ahe reached for the blue bead to show everyone, but found the cloth packet empty.

He suddenly remembered the bean seeds. His heart went cold. Something that could have grown a row of bean plants in spring had become a bead that flashed for one evening.

### Page 17

The next day, he took the blue bead to find the seller, but the stall was gone.

Ahe stood before the empty place. The bead still shone in his palm, but he no longer wanted to look at it.

### Page 18

The old man did not scold him. He only handed him a bowl of warm porridge.

This time Ahe ate slowly. The rice grains softened and opened, and a faint sweetness came from under his tongue. He discovered that plain flavor is not no flavor; it needs a quieter tongue.

### Page 19

After the meal, he put the little market things into a box.

He kept one paper pinwheel, threw away the candy wrappers, and placed the blue bead by the window. Then he found Little Stone, and together they carried the remaining rice sacks into the storehouse.

### Page 20

When the storehouse door closed, Ahe heard the dull sound of the sacks landing and the old man coughing softly.

Yesterday, those sounds had been covered by drums and shouting. Today, the village grew quiet, and his ears came back to themselves.

## Return

### Page 21

Chapter Twelve says that colors can blind, sounds can deafen, and flavors can take away taste.

Ahe knew this was not against colors, music, or good food. The market had its liveliness, and sugar had its sweetness.

### Page 22

The trouble begins when the eyes always chase brightness, the ears always chase loudness, and the tongue always chases heaviness. Then the heart is pulled around by outside things.

When it runs too fast, it misses another person's call and may trade sprouting seeds for a shining bead.

### Page 23

"For the belly, not for the eyes" means putting what truly nourishes first.

Rice, sleep, bodily steadiness, a clear heart, and promises kept may not be fancy, but they help a person stand.

### Page 24

Ahe wrote: my eyes may enjoy liveliness, but liveliness must not lead me away.

The belly knows what nourishes. When the heart is quiet, it also knows what to keep and what to let pass.
