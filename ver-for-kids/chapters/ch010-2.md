# 第010-2章：柔软的人，手不会乱抓

## 典故引入

### 第1页

相传，张三丰创太极拳，讲究以柔克刚。

这个传说不必当成每一个细节都能考证的历史来读。适合孩子记住的，是那幅画面：慢慢转身，手臂像水流，脚步却很稳。

### 第2页

后人讲太极，常画一个壮汉猛冲过来，一拳打得很重。

老人不硬挡，也不迎着拳头撞上去。他侧身、转腰、轻轻一引，壮汉自己的力气找不到落脚处，反而往前扑空。

### 第3页

这里的柔，不是没力量。

硬碰硬，是拿自己的骨头去撞别人的骨头；柔，是先不把自己变成一堵墙，让对方的力气有地方过去，再找到合适的一点。

### 第4页

太极的慢，也不是懒。

慢，是让气息有地方走；柔，是不把身体拧成一根硬木棍。硬木棍一折就断，水流却能绕过去。

### 第5页

所以“专气致柔，能婴儿乎”，不是叫人变得软弱。

它问的是：你能不能不被急气推着跑？能不能让身体和心都不僵？能不能有力，却不硬顶？

### 第6页

佛家的天台宗里，智者大师教人从呼吸下手，有“数息”“随息”的方法。

一开始，人可以数自己的呼吸：一、二、三。心跑了，就知道它跑了，再轻轻回来。

### 第7页

有人会把数息数成比赛：我今天数到一千，我比昨天厉害。

这样一来，呼吸又变成新的骄傲，心还是紧的。数息不是做账，不是数得越多越有道。

### 第8页

它真正的用处，是给乱跑的心一条小路。

心回到呼吸里，身体就不那么硬；身体不那么硬，眼睛和手也不那么急。

### 第9页

孟子也讲养气。他说，一个人里面真的充实，脸上、背上、手脚都会自然露出来。

这不是抹出来的亮，也不是摆出来的样子。心里有根，身上就少一点飘。

### 第10页

老子讲得更简。

他不先排出许多台阶，只问：能不能抱一？能不能致柔？能不能像婴儿那样，不把自己拧成一团？

### 第11页

婴儿柔软，不是因为懂很多道理。

他饿了会哭，饱了会安，困了会睡。大人常常一边生气，一边装没事；一边害怕，一边撑得很硬。

### 第12页

第十章最后说“生而不有，为而不恃，长而不宰”。

这句话看起来很大，其实也能落在一件很小的事上：教人骑车。

### 第13页

阿禾教小石头骑车。他一开始扶着车后座，嘴里不停指挥：“看前面！别歪！脚用力！手不要抖！”

小石头越听越紧，肩膀耸起来，车头像喝醉了一样晃来晃去。

### 第14页

阿禾怕他摔，又把车抓得更紧。

车不倒了，可也不像小石头在骑，倒像阿禾的手推着一辆不会长大的车。

### 第15页

老人说：“扶可以，别把他的手也扶成你的。”

阿禾闭上嘴，只在车快倒时轻轻托一下。小石头喘着气，摔了两次，膝盖沾了土，却慢慢找到自己的平衡。

### 第16页

第三次，车真的往前去了。

阿禾跟着跑了几步，手还悬在车后。要不要再抓住？他心里一紧。

### 第17页

就在那一刻，小石头自己踩住了踏板。

阿禾把手放下。小石头回头喊：“我会了！”风把他的声音吹得很亮。

### 第18页

阿禾心里有一点舍不得，又有一点高兴。

如果他一直扶着，那辆车永远不会真正属于小石头的身体。教会，不是把人留在自己手里。

## 回扣

### 第19页

太极的柔、数息的慢、孟子的养气、小石头学骑车，都不是同一件事。

可它们都碰到第十章的一条线：身心不要散，气息不要硬，帮助不要变成控制。

### 第20页

“天门开阖，能为雌乎”，可以先不讲得神秘。

开合之间，能不能柔顺、安静、接得住？像门会开，也会关；像呼吸会进，也会出，不必每一下都用力证明自己。

### 第21页

“明白四达，能无为乎”，不是叫人装糊涂。

它问的是：你看得很明白时，还能不能不乱插手？你有能力时，还能不能让别人长出自己的能力？

### 第22页

“为而不恃”，是做了，不拿来压人。

“长而不宰”，是扶过，不一直抓着。真正的帮助，不把对方变成自己的功劳。

### 第23页

“玄德”深，是因为它常常不站在亮处。

它像扶车时那只及时松开的手，像呼吸里那一点慢下来的空，像水边不说话的根。做了，却不抓；照看，却不占有。

### 第24页

阿禾写下：柔软的人，不一定没有力量。

有时，真正的力量不是抓紧，而是稳住自己，然后在该放手的时候放手。

# Chapter 010-2: A Soft Person Does Not Grab Wildly

## Allusion

### Page 1

Tradition says that Zhang Sanfeng created tai chi, a practice that uses softness to meet hardness.

We do not need to treat every detail of the legend as certain history. What children can remember is the picture: a person turning slowly, arms like flowing water, feet steady on the ground.

### Page 2

When later people explain tai chi, they often picture a strong man rushing in with a heavy punch.

The old practitioner does not block hard or crash into the fist. He turns aside, turns the waist, and lightly leads the force. The strong man's own strength finds no place to land, and he stumbles forward.

### Page 3

This softness is not weakness.

Hard against hard is bone striking bone. Softness first refuses to become a wall, lets the other force pass, and then finds the fitting point.

### Page 4

Tai chi's slowness is not laziness either.

Slowness gives breath room to move. Softness keeps the body from twisting into a hard stick. A hard stick can snap; flowing water can curve around.

### Page 5

So "giving undivided attention to the breath and becoming pliant as a baby" does not tell us to become weak.

It asks: can you stop being pushed around by rushed breath? Can body and heart become less stiff? Can you have strength without hard collision?

### Page 6

In the Tiantai school of Buddhism, Master Zhiyi taught people to begin with breathing, using methods such as counting the breath and following the breath.

At first, one may count the breaths: one, two, three. When the mind runs away, one sees that it has run, and gently returns.

### Page 7

Some people turn counting the breath into a contest: today I counted to a thousand; I am better than yesterday.

Then breath becomes a new pride, and the heart is still tight. Counting breath is not bookkeeping, and counting more is not the Way.

### Page 8

Its true use is to give the wandering heart a small path home.

When the heart returns to breath, the body is less hard. When the body is less hard, the eyes and hands are less hurried too.

### Page 9

Mencius also spoke of nourishing inner energy. He said that when a person is truly full inside, it naturally shows in the face, back, hands, and feet.

This is not brightness painted on, and not a pose put on for others. When the heart has roots, the body drifts less.

### Page 10

Laozi speaks more simply.

He does not first list many steps. He asks: can one hold to oneness? Can one become pliant? Can one be like a baby, not twisting oneself into a knot?

### Page 11

A baby is soft, not because he knows many teachings.

When hungry, a baby cries; when full, rests; when sleepy, sleeps. Grown people often feel angry while pretending nothing is wrong, or feel afraid while holding themselves stiff.

### Page 12

At the end, Chapter Ten says: produce and nourish, yet do not claim, boast, or control.

This large sentence can fit inside a small matter: teaching someone to ride a bicycle.

### Page 13

Ahe taught Little Stone to ride. At first he held the back of the seat and kept giving orders: "Look ahead! Don't tilt! Push harder! Don't shake your hands!"

The more Little Stone heard, the tighter he became. His shoulders rose, and the front wheel wobbled as if it were dizzy.

### Page 14

Ahe was afraid he would fall, so he held the bicycle even tighter.

The bicycle no longer fell, but it was not quite Little Stone riding. It was more like Ahe's hand pushing a bicycle that would never grow up.

### Page 15

The old man said, "You may support him, but do not turn his hands into yours."

Ahe closed his mouth. He only steadied the bicycle lightly when it was about to fall. Little Stone breathed hard, fell twice, got dirt on his knees, and slowly found his own balance.

### Page 16

The third time, the bicycle truly moved forward.

Ahe ran behind it, his hand still hanging near the seat. Should he grab it again? His heart tightened.

### Page 17

At that moment, Little Stone caught the pedals by himself.

Ahe lowered his hand. Little Stone looked back and shouted, "I can do it!" The wind carried his voice brightly.

### Page 18

Ahe felt a little reluctant, and also glad.

If he kept holding on, the bicycle would never truly belong to Little Stone's own body. To teach someone is not to keep that person in your hand.

## Return to the Original

### Page 19

Tai chi's softness, the slowness of counting breath, Mencius's nourishing of inner energy, and Little Stone learning to ride are not the same thing.

But they all touch one line in Chapter Ten: do not let body and heart scatter; do not let breath harden; do not let help become control.

### Page 20

"In the opening and shutting of the gates of heaven, can one be like the female?" need not be made mysterious at first.

In opening and closing, can one be gentle, quiet, and receptive? Like a door that opens and closes; like breath that enters and leaves, not proving itself with force each time.

### Page 21

"While intelligence reaches everywhere, can one appear without knowledge?" does not mean pretending to be foolish.

It asks: when you see clearly, can you still avoid meddling? When you have ability, can you let others grow ability too?

### Page 22

"Doing without boasting" means acting without using the action to press others down.

"Presiding without controlling" means having supported without always holding on. True help does not turn the other person into one's own credit.

### Page 23

"The mysterious Quality" is deep because it often does not stand in the bright place.

It is like the hand that lets go at the right time while teaching a bicycle, like the small space that appears when breath slows, like an unseen root by the water. It acts without grabbing; it cares without possessing.

### Page 24

Ahe wrote: A soft person is not necessarily without strength.

Sometimes true strength is not holding tighter. It is steadying yourself, then letting go when it is time.
