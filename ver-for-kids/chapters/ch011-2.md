# 第011-2章：空竹才会唱

## 故事引入

### 第1页

春末，麦子快熟了。

空空村有个老规矩：傍晚收工时，要有人在田埂上吹一声竹笛。笛声一响，远处割草的人、放牛的人、捉虫的孩子，都知道该往家走了。

### 第2页

那天，老笛子忽然裂了。

声音从裂缝里漏出去，吹出来像一只打喷嚏的鸭子。小石头笑得蹲在地上，阿禾却很认真：“我来做一支新的。”

### 第3页

他跑到竹林里，挑了一根最粗、最绿、最结实的竹子。

“要声音大，就得用最实在的竹子。”他说，“空空的东西，哪里有力气？”

### 第4页

阿禾削平竹节，把外皮擦得发亮。

他舍不得把里面打通，也舍不得开孔。好好一根竹子，为什么要挖空它、戳伤它呢？

### 第5页

他把竹管放到嘴边，用力一吹。

脸鼓起来了，脖子也红了。竹子却只发出一点闷闷的“噗”，像有人在袖子里叹气。

### 第6页

小石头说：“是不是吹得还不够用力？”

阿禾吸了一大口气，吹得眼睛都眯起来。还是没有笛声，只有他的头越来越晕。

### 第7页

老人拿起那根竹子，轻轻敲了敲。

“它很结实。”老人说，“所以它只能当棍子。要做笛子，里面得有一条路，让气走过去。”

### 第8页

阿禾不舍得：“挖空了，不就少了吗？”

老人把裂掉的旧笛递给他：“你听见过它唱歌，是因为它少了一些竹肉，多了一条空路。”

### 第9页

他们把竹节慢慢通开。

竹屑一点一点落下来，竹管里亮出一条长长的空。阿禾从一头望进去，看见另一头有一点天光。

### 第10页

老人又量好位置，开了几个小孔。

阿禾心疼地摸着孔边：“这些洞也是有用的？”

### 第11页

老人说：“洞不是伤口，是门。”

“气从这边进，遇到这些门，就会停一停、转一转、变一变。没有门，只有一阵风；有了门，才有曲子。”

### 第12页

阿禾再吹。

这一次，他没有把气全砸进去。气轻轻进竹管，先是一声细细的哨音，接着抖了一下，变成一段很短的调子。

### 第13页

田埂上的孩子都安静了。

声音不大，却走得很远。它从麦芒上掠过去，穿过桑树影子，落到放牛人耳边。

### 第14页

放牛人抬头笑了：“新笛子？”

阿禾的脸红了，却不是因为用力吹。他低头看那根竹子，忽然觉得它不像少了什么，倒像多了一条看不见的路。

### 第15页

第二天，他又试着多开一个孔。

这次孔开偏了，调子一下歪了。阿禾才知道，空也不能乱空。车轮的孔要在中心，窗要开在合适处，笛孔也有自己的位置。

### 第16页

老人说：“无，不是随便没有。”

“能让气走、让声成、让人听见远处，这样的空，才叫用。”

## 回扣

### 第17页

第十一章说“有之以为利，无之以为用”。

竹子是“有”。没有竹身，气就散了。可是竹子若全是实心，气也无路可走。

### 第18页

笛子真正会唱，是因为竹身里空着，孔也空着。

那些空处不发光，不出头，却让一口气变成了能叫人回家的声音。

### 第19页

阿禾想起车轮、陶罐和小屋。

车轮的空，让车轴通过；陶罐的空，让水住下；屋子的空，让人安身；竹笛的空，让风变成歌。

### 第20页

他在本子上写：空，不是没有。

空是给路，给气，给光，给别人进来的地方。心里也要留一条空路，不然再多话，也吹不成一支好曲子。

# Chapter 011-2: Only Hollow Bamboo Can Sing

## Story

### Page 1

At the end of spring, the wheat was almost ripe.

Kongkong Village had an old habit: at dusk, someone stood on the field ridge and played one note on a bamboo flute. When the note sounded, people cutting grass, herding cattle, and catching insects knew it was time to go home.

### Page 2

That day, the old flute cracked.

Sound leaked from the crack, and the note came out like a duck sneezing. Little Stone laughed so hard he crouched down, but Ahe was serious. "I will make a new one."

### Page 3

He ran to the bamboo grove and chose the thickest, greenest, strongest bamboo.

"If the sound should be strong, the bamboo must be solid," he said. "How can an empty thing have strength?"

### Page 4

Ahe smoothed the bamboo joints and polished the skin until it shone.

He did not want to open the inside, and he did not want to cut holes. Why hollow out and pierce a perfectly good piece of bamboo?

### Page 5

He put the bamboo to his mouth and blew hard.

His cheeks puffed, and his neck reddened. The bamboo made only a dull puff, like someone sighing inside a sleeve.

### Page 6

Little Stone said, "Maybe you did not blow hard enough."

Ahe took a huge breath and blew until his eyes narrowed. Still there was no flute note, only a dizzy head.

### Page 7

The old man picked up the bamboo and tapped it lightly.

"It is very solid," he said. "So it can only be a stick. To be a flute, it needs a path inside for breath to travel."

### Page 8

Ahe did not want to let go. "If we hollow it out, won't there be less?"

The old man handed him the cracked old flute. "You heard it sing because it had less bamboo flesh and more empty road."

### Page 9

They slowly opened the bamboo joints.

Shavings fell little by little, and a long hollow space appeared inside the tube. Ahe looked through one end and saw a point of sky at the other.

### Page 10

The old man measured carefully and cut several small holes.

Ahe touched the edge of one hole. "Are these holes useful too?"

### Page 11

The old man said, "A hole is not always a wound. Sometimes it is a door."

"Breath enters here. When it meets these doors, it pauses, turns, and changes. Without doors, it is only wind. With doors, it can become a tune."

### Page 12

Ahe blew again.

This time, he did not throw all his breath into the bamboo. Air entered gently. First came a thin whistle; then it trembled and became a short melody.

### Page 13

The children on the field ridge grew quiet.

The sound was not loud, yet it traveled far. It skimmed over wheat tips, passed through mulberry shadows, and reached the ears of the cattle herder.

### Page 14

The herder looked up and smiled. "A new flute?"

Ahe's face reddened, but not from blowing too hard. He looked at the bamboo and felt that it had not lost something. It had gained an invisible road.

### Page 15

The next day, he tried cutting one more hole.

This time the hole was in the wrong place, and the tune bent badly. Ahe learned that emptiness cannot be made anywhere at random. The wheel's opening belongs at the center, the window belongs in the right wall, and flute holes have their own places.

### Page 16

The old man said, "Emptiness does not mean careless absence."

"The useful empty place is the one that lets breath travel, sound form, and people hear from far away."

## Return

### Page 17

Chapter Eleven says that what exists gives benefit, and what is empty gives use.

The bamboo is the visible thing. Without a bamboo body, breath scatters. But if the bamboo is solid all the way through, breath has no road.

### Page 18

The flute can sing because the tube and the holes are empty.

Those empty places do not shine or show off, yet they turn one breath into a sound that calls people home.

### Page 19

Ahe thought of the wheel, the jar, and the small house.

The wheel's emptiness lets the axle pass. The jar's emptiness lets water stay. The house's emptiness lets people live. The flute's emptiness lets wind become song.

### Page 20

He wrote in his notebook: empty does not mean nothing.

Empty gives a road to breath, light, and other people. The heart needs an empty road too. Without it, even many words cannot become a good tune.
