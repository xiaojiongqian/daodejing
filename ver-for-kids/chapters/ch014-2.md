# 第014-2章：蜜蜂撞着亮窗纸

## 典故引入

### 第1页

禅宗里有一个“蜂子投窗”的公案。

故事说，古灵神赞禅师悟道以后，回去看从前教他的老和尚。老和尚还在窗下读经，读得很用功。

### 第2页

那时的窗，不像今天这样全是透明玻璃。

窗上糊着纸，外面的光透进来，白白亮亮。屋子旁边其实有门，门是开着的。

### 第3页

一只蜜蜂飞进屋里，想出去。

它看见窗纸很亮，以为那就是外面的天空。于是它直直撞过去，嗡的一声，又弹回来。

### 第4页

它不死心。

再撞一次，再弹回来。翅膀急急地振，身体一次次贴到纸上。亮光就在眼前，可就是出不去。

### 第5页

古灵神赞看见了，借蜜蜂提醒老和尚。

他的大意是：空门就在旁边，蜜蜂却偏要钻旧窗纸；人若只在旧纸堆里打转，不肯回头看活路，也很难出头。

### 第6页

这不是说读书没用。

老和尚读的经书很宝贵。可是如果眼睛只盯着纸上的亮，不肯转身看一看开着的门，再好的字也可能变成一层挡路的纸。

### 第7页

蜜蜂被“看得见的亮”骗了。

它的眼睛说：这里最亮，这里就是路。可真正能出去的门，不在它一直撞的地方。

### 第8页

阿禾听完，立刻想到自己画“道”的事。

他问：“所以，看见的不一定是真路？”

### 第9页

老人说：“看见的也可能是真路，只是不能只靠眼睛。”

“窗纸亮，门也开。眼睛看见亮，还要让心问一问：这条路通不通？”

### 第10页

小石头问：“那蜜蜂为什么不回头？”

老人说：“急的时候，谁都容易这样。越撞越痛，越痛越觉得再撞一下就能出去。”

### 第11页

阿禾想起很多事。

做题做不出时，他常常盯着同一行字不放；找东西找不到时，他只翻同一个抽屉；和朋友吵架时，他只想证明自己没错。

### 第12页

这些时候，旁边也许就有一扇门。

可是心里急，眼睛就只看见那张亮亮的窗纸。

### 第13页

老人带孩子们去学堂。

门边有一只小虫，也在窗纸上乱撞。阿禾没有用手去抓它，只轻轻把门再推开一点，又把窗边的灯挪远。

### 第14页

小虫在纸上停了一会儿。

屋里没有那么亮了，门口的风却清楚起来。它绕了一个小圈，忽然飞向门外。

### 第15页

小石头低声说：“原来帮它，不是替它撞破纸。”

阿禾说：“是让它看见门。”

## 回扣

### 第16页

第十四章说“视之不见名曰夷，听之不闻名曰希，搏之不得名曰微”。

蜜蜂的故事提醒我们：眼睛看见的亮，不一定就是最真实的路。耳边最大的嗡嗡声，也不一定告诉你出口在哪里。

### 第17页

“搏之不得”，也很像这只蜜蜂。

它拼命用身体去撞，好像只要更用力，就能抓到自由。可有些问题不是靠更用力，而是靠换一个方向。

### 第18页

“执古之道，以御今之有”，不是叫人死钻旧纸。

古书、经文、经验，都像前人留下的线索。拿线索是为了走出去，不是为了把头埋在纸里。

### 第19页

阿禾写下：亮的地方，要看；开着的门，也要看。

读书要读，生活也要走进去。若一直撞同一张窗纸，就算窗纸很亮，也不是道。

# Chapter 014-2: The Bee Striking the Bright Window Paper

## Allusion

### Page 1

In Chan tradition, there is a story of a bee striking a paper window.

The story says that after the monk Guling Shenzan awakened, he returned to visit the old monk who had once taught him. The old monk was still reading scriptures by the window, working very hard.

### Page 2

Windows then were not all clear glass like many windows today.

Paper covered the frame, and outside light shone through it in a pale brightness. Beside the room, there was actually a door, and the door was open.

### Page 3

A bee flew into the room and wanted to get out.

It saw the bright window paper and thought that must be the sky outside. It flew straight at it, buzzed, struck, and bounced back.

### Page 4

It did not give up.

It struck again and bounced back again. Its wings beat urgently, and its body hit the paper again and again. The brightness was right before it, but it could not get out.

### Page 5

Guling Shenzan saw this and used the bee to awaken the old monk.

His meaning was: the open way is beside you, but the bee insists on drilling into old window paper. If people keep circling in old paper and never turn toward the living way, they cannot easily come out.

### Page 6

This does not mean reading is useless.

The scriptures the old monk read were precious. But if the eyes stare only at brightness on paper and refuse to turn toward the open door, even good words may become a sheet that blocks the road.

### Page 7

The bee was fooled by visible brightness.

Its eyes said, "This is brightest, so this must be the way." But the door that truly led outside was not where it kept striking.

### Page 8

When Ahe heard this, he immediately thought of drawing the Dao.

"So what we see is not always the true road?" he asked.

### Page 9

The old man said, "What we see may also be a true road, but we cannot rely only on the eyes."

"The window paper is bright, and the door is open. After seeing brightness, the heart must ask: does this road go through?"

### Page 10

Little Stone asked, "Why did the bee not turn around?"

The old man said, "When we are anxious, we all become like that. The more it hurts, the more we feel that one more strike will get us out."

### Page 11

Ahe remembered many things.

When a problem would not solve, he often stared at the same line. When he could not find something, he searched the same drawer. When he argued with a friend, he only wanted to prove he was right.

### Page 12

At such times, there may be an open door nearby.

But when the heart is anxious, the eyes see only the bright paper.

### Page 13

The old man brought the children to the schoolroom.

Near the door, a small insect was striking the paper window. Ahe did not catch it with his hand. He gently pushed the door wider and moved the lamp away from the window.

### Page 14

The insect paused on the paper.

The room was less bright there now, and the wind at the doorway became clearer. It circled once, then suddenly flew outside.

### Page 15

Little Stone whispered, "So helping it did not mean breaking the paper for it."

Ahe said, "It meant helping it see the door."

## Return

### Page 16

Chapter Fourteen says that we look and do not see, listen and do not hear, grasp and do not hold.

The bee story reminds us that the brightness seen by the eyes is not always the truest road. The loudest buzzing near the ear does not always tell us where the exit is.

### Page 17

"We try to grasp it and do not get hold of it" also sounds like this bee.

It struck with its whole body, as if greater force could seize freedom. But some problems are not solved by more force; they are solved by turning.

### Page 18

"Holding the Tao of old to direct what exists today" does not mean drilling into old paper.

Old books, scriptures, and experience are clues left by those before us. We hold the clue in order to walk out, not to bury our heads in paper.

### Page 19

Ahe wrote: look at the bright place, but also look at the open door.

Read books, and also enter life. If I keep striking the same window paper, even bright paper is not the Dao.
