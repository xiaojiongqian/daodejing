# 第008-2章：王座前的潮水

## 典故引入

### 第1页

有一次，孔子站在河边，看水日夜不停地流。

他说，逝去的时间就像这条河，不分白天黑夜，一直往前。

### 第2页

孔子看水，看见的是“不断”：不要白白放过日子，要向前，要珍惜。

老子看水，又看见另一面：水不争高处，不急着显眼，却能利万物、处低下。

### 第3页

同一条水，可以照见不同的心。

心急的人看见奔跑，心静的人看见深处；想争的人看见前后，想养人的人看见滋润。

### 第4页

很远很远的北方，也流传着一个关于水的传奇。

中世纪有一位国王，名字常译作克努特。他统治过英格兰、丹麦等地方，权力很大，身边也有许多爱说好听话的人。

### 第5页

那些人常夸他：“陛下，您的命令谁敢不听？陆地听您的，城堡听您的，军队听您的，连大海也该听您的。”

这样的夸奖听多了，会让人忘记自己只是人，不是天地的主人。

### 第6页

传说有一天，克努特让人把王座搬到海边。

那时正是涨潮的时候。天空低低的，海面一层一层推来，白色浪花像一队沉默的士兵。

### 第7页

国王坐在王座上，对海潮说：“到此为止，不许再往前，不许弄湿我的脚和袍子。”

侍臣们站在后面，屏住呼吸。谁也不知道海会不会给国王让路。

### 第8页

海没有发怒，也没有回答。

它只是按自己的时候继续上来。先湿了沙子，再碰到王座的脚，最后漫过国王的鞋，沾上他的袍边。

### 第9页

克努特站起来，退到干处。

传说里，他对身边的人说：你们看，国王的权力也是有限的。真正让海潮来去的，不是我的命令。

### 第10页

这个故事不是说克努特傻到真以为能命令大海。

更好的读法是：他借大海教训那些只会奉承的人。海水一寸一寸上来，比一百句劝告更清楚。

### 第11页

潮水没有和国王争吵。

它没有说：“你不配命令我。”它也没有为了讨好王冠而停下。它只是到了时候就来，到了时候就退。

### 第12页

这正好照见第八章里的“动善时”。

水的行动有时节。涨潮不是逞强，退潮不是认输；该来时来，该去时去，不被掌声和命令拉偏。

### 第13页

也照见“不争”。

不争，不是听谁乱指挥；不争，是不把力气耗在吵赢上。水不和王座辩论，却让所有人看见：王座不能替天时下令。

### 第14页

再看“水唯能下方成海，山不矜高自及天”。

海为什么大？不是因为它坐在最高的王座上，而是因为它在低处，河流才有地方汇入。

### 第15页

如果每条小河都要争着爬到山顶，就没有大海。

正因为水肯往下走，山泉、溪流、江河才有一个共同的归处。

### 第16页

山也不天天说“我最高”。

它只是稳稳在那里，经风，经雨，经雪。真正高的东西，不一定需要把“高”挂在嘴边。

## 回扣

### 第17页

第八章说“居善地”。海的地，是最低的地方；王座的地，是人抬高出来的地方。

可最后，众水归海，不归王座。能接住万流的，常常不是最高处，而是最低处。

### 第18页

“心善渊”，也能从潮水里看见。

深水不急着解释自己。人越在岸上喊，大海越显得宽；人越把权力说满，潮水越安静地显出边界。

### 第19页

“言善信”，在这个故事里，是潮水给出的回答。

国王说“不许湿我”，潮水没有说谎，也没有奉承。它用湿到鞋边的事实告诉大家：自然不会因为好听的话改变自己的时候。

### 第20页

“夫唯不争，故无尤”，也要读得深一点。

潮水没有争吵，所以不显得凶；潮水没有奉承，所以不显得假。它只是照着自己的时节来去，让人自己明白。

### 第21页

阿禾想起后山那条小溪，又想起海边的王座。

小溪不抢漂亮石阶，潮水不听王座命令。水的德，不在于会赢一场争吵，而在于清楚自己该往哪里去、什么时候动、能滋养什么。

### 第22页

他写下：做水一样的人，不是把自己变小。

是脚下低，心里深，行动合时；别人夸你、命令你、催你，你都不必乱。像潮水一样，安静地按自己的道来去。

# Chapter 008-2: The Tide Before the Throne

## Allusion

### Page 1

Once, Confucius stood beside a river and watched the water flow day and night.

He said that passing time was like this river, moving forward without stopping by day or night.

### Page 2

Confucius looked at water and saw continuance: do not waste the days; move forward; cherish time.

Laozi looked at water and saw another side: water does not fight for the high place or hurry to be seen, yet it benefits all things and goes low.

### Page 3

The same water can reflect different hearts.

A hurried heart sees running. A quiet heart sees depth. A striving heart sees front and back. A nourishing heart sees what can be watered.

### Page 4

Far away in the north, there is also a legend about water.

In the Middle Ages, there was a king often called Cnut or Canute. He ruled England, Denmark, and other lands, and many people around him loved to praise him.

### Page 5

They said, "Your Majesty, who dares disobey your command? The land listens to you, the castles listen to you, the armies listen to you, and even the sea should listen to you."

After hearing such praise again and again, a person may forget that he is only human, not the master of heaven and earth.

### Page 6

The legend says that one day Cnut ordered his throne to be carried to the shore.

The tide was rising. The sky hung low, and the sea came in layer after layer, white foam like a silent line of soldiers.

### Page 7

The king sat on the throne and said to the tide, "Stop here. Come no farther. Do not wet my feet or my robe."

The courtiers stood behind him, holding their breath. No one knew whether the sea would make way for a king.

### Page 8

The sea did not rage, and it did not answer.

It simply kept coming at its own time. First it wet the sand, then touched the legs of the throne, and finally washed over the king's shoes and the edge of his robe.

### Page 9

Cnut stood up and stepped back to dry ground.

In the legend, he told the people around him: see, even a king's power has limits. It is not my command that makes the tide come and go.

### Page 10

This story does not have to mean that Cnut was foolish enough to believe he could command the sea.

A better reading is that he used the sea to teach those who only flattered him. The water rising inch by inch was clearer than a hundred warnings.

### Page 11

The tide did not argue with the king.

It did not say, "You are unworthy to command me." Nor did it stop to please the crown. When the time came, it came; when the time passed, it withdrew.

### Page 12

This shows "good movement is timely" in Chapter Eight.

The movement of water has its season. Rising is not showing off, and withdrawing is not defeat. It comes when it is time and goes when it is time, not pulled crooked by applause or orders.

### Page 13

It also shows not striving.

Not striving does not mean obeying every wild command. It means not wasting strength on winning an argument. Water did not debate the throne, yet everyone saw that a throne cannot command the tide.

### Page 14

Now look at the old saying: water becomes the sea because it is willing to go low; a mountain reaches the sky without boasting of height.

Why is the sea vast? Not because it sits on the highest throne, but because it is low enough for rivers to enter.

### Page 15

If every little river tried to climb to the mountain top, there would be no sea.

Because water is willing to go down, springs, streams, and rivers have one wide place to return to.

### Page 16

A mountain does not say every day, "I am the highest."

It simply stands through wind, rain, and snow. What is truly high does not always need to wear the word "high" on its mouth.

## Return to the Original

### Page 17

Chapter Eight says a good dwelling is in the fitting place. The sea's place is the lowest place; the throne's place is one raised up by people.

In the end, all waters return to the sea, not to the throne. What receives many streams is often not the highest place, but the lowest.

### Page 18

"A good mind is deep" can also be seen in the tide.

Deep water does not hurry to explain itself. The louder people shout on shore, the wider the sea appears; the more people fill power with words, the more quietly the tide shows the boundary.

### Page 19

"Good words are trustworthy" appears in the answer the tide gave.

The king said, "Do not wet me," but the tide did not lie and did not flatter. With water over the shoes, it showed everyone that nature does not change its timing because words sound grand.

### Page 20

"Because it does not wrangle, no one finds fault" must also be read deeply.

The tide did not quarrel, so it did not seem fierce. It did not flatter, so it did not seem false. It simply came and went by its time, letting people understand for themselves.

### Page 21

Ahe thought of the stream behind the hill, and then of the throne by the sea.

The stream did not fight for the pretty stone steps. The tide did not obey the throne. Water's virtue is not winning an argument; it is knowing where to go, when to move, and what can be nourished.

### Page 22

He wrote: To be like water is not to make myself small.

It is to stay low in posture, deep in heart, and timely in action. Whether others praise, command, or rush you, you do not have to scatter. Like the tide, move quietly according to the Way.
