# 第013-3章：脚落到地上

## 典故引入

### 第1页

尼泊尔加德满都谷地有一种传统：人们会从小女孩中选出“库玛丽”，尊她为活女神。

这是别处的文化，讲给孩子听时要带着尊重。我们只借这个故事看一件事：当一个人被放到很高的位置，她的心怎样才不被那个位置带走？

### 第2页

被选中的女孩还很小。

她穿红衣，坐在宫殿的窗前。节日到来时，街上挤满人，花落在台阶上，鼓声一阵一阵涌上来。

### 第3页

人们抬头看她。

有人合掌，有人低头，有人把愿望轻轻说出来。一个孩子被这么多人仰望，很容易觉得自己真的住在云上。

### 第4页

可是云上的日子，也有安静得发空的时候。

她不能像别的孩子那样跑出巷子，不能把鞋子踩进雨后的泥水，不能大笑到肚子疼。她坐得很直，连表情也要小心。

### 第5页

有一天，远处传来学校的铃声。

那声音很轻，却从窗缝里进来。她看见一群孩子背着书包经过，有人边走边啃饼，有人把铅笔夹在耳朵后面。

### 第6页

这些普通得不能再普通的事，忽然像小灯一样亮。

她低头看自己的脚。那双脚被照顾得很好，却很少自己踩在石板路上。

### 第7页

库玛丽的身份不是一生不变的。

到了一定时候，她要从高座上下来。红衣会收起，窗前会有下一位女孩。人们的目光，也会转向新的座位。

### 第8页

那一天，她第一次以普通学生的身份走进学校。

门口没有人跪拜。老师叫她的名字，不是称号，只是名字。同学递给她一张纸：“这里可以坐。”

### 第9页

她伸手接纸，手心微微出汗。

没有鼓声，没有花，没有人提醒她要坐得像神。她听见自己的呼吸，也听见鞋底轻轻碰到地面。

### 第10页

如果她把“女神”当成全部的自己，退位就像从高处掉下来。

可如果她慢慢知道，那是一段尊贵而临时的角色，那么走下来虽然会痛，却不是把自己弄丢。

### 第11页

下课时，操场上有人跑。

她先站着看。后来，一个女孩回头喊：“来呀！”她犹豫了一下，迈出第一步。

### 第12页

脚踩到土地，灰尘轻轻起来。

她跑得不快，裙角也有点乱。可她忽然笑了。那笑不需要给任何人解释，也不需要像节日那样端正。

### 第13页

老人讲到这里，孩子们都安静。

小石头低声问：“从那么高的地方下来，会不会很难过？”

### 第14页

老人说：“会。被人仰望，会让人心里发亮；忽然没人仰望，也会让人心里发空。”

“所以老子说，宠会让人惊，失去宠也会让人惊。”

### 第15页

阿禾想起自己的红绸带。

他只当了一晚领鼓人，心就上上下下。若一个人从小被很多人当作神女，她要学会回到自己，当然更不容易。

### 第16页

老人说：“难的不是坐在高处。”

“难的是坐在高处时，不忘自己有脚；走下来时，也不觉得自己只剩空。”

### 第17页

阿禾低头看自己的脚。

它们沾过泥，踢过石子，也因为跑太快摔疼过。它们很普通，却能带他回家。

## 回扣

### 第18页

第十三章说“宠辱若惊”。

被捧到高处，是一种惊；从高处下来，也是一种惊。人的心若全放在别人仰不仰望自己上，就会一直被风吹。

### 第19页

“得之若惊，失之若惊”，是因为人常把临时的角色当成自己。

红绸带会取下，女神座位会换人，别人的目光会转向别处。角色会来，也会走。

### 第20页

“贵以身为天下”，不是把自己看得最大。

是知道真实的生命比称号更根本。能吃饭、写字、走路、奔跑、呼吸，这些不亮，却把人稳稳接住。

### 第21页

老子不是轻轻一句“别在乎”。

他知道人心会惊，才提醒我们：不要把整颗心交给会来会走的宠，也不要在失去宠时把自己一起丢掉。

### 第22页

阿禾写下：座位会换，称号会换，掌声会换。

脚要落到地上，心也要落到地上。这样，被看见时不飞走，不被看见时也不散掉。

# Chapter 013-3: Feet Touching the Ground

## Allusion

### Page 1

In Nepal's Kathmandu Valley, there is a tradition of choosing a young girl as Kumari, honoring her as a living goddess.

This is another culture, and it should be spoken of with respect. Here we use the story to look at one question: when a person is placed very high, how can the heart avoid being carried away by that place?

### Page 2

The chosen girl is still very young.

She wears red clothes and sits at a palace window. During festivals, the street fills with people, flowers fall on the steps, and drums rise in waves.

### Page 3

People look up at her.

Some join their hands, some bow their heads, and some whisper wishes. When so many people look up to a child, it is easy to feel as if one truly lives among clouds.

### Page 4

But life among clouds can also be strangely empty.

She cannot run through alleys like other children, step into muddy rainwater, or laugh until her stomach hurts. She sits very straight, and even her face must be careful.

### Page 5

One day, a school bell sounded far away.

It was a small sound, but it slipped through the window. She saw children passing with schoolbags. One ate a flatbread while walking; another held a pencil behind the ear.

### Page 6

These very ordinary things suddenly shone like little lamps.

She looked down at her own feet. They had been cared for carefully, yet they had seldom stepped by themselves on the stone road.

### Page 7

The role of Kumari does not last for a whole life.

At a certain time, she must come down from the high seat. The red clothes are put away, another girl sits at the window, and people's eyes turn toward the new seat.

### Page 8

On that day, she enters school as an ordinary student.

No one kneels at the door. The teacher calls her name, not a title, just her name. A classmate hands her a sheet of paper. "You can sit here."

### Page 9

She reaches for the paper, and her palm is damp.

There are no drums, no flowers, and no one reminding her to sit like a goddess. She hears her own breath and the soft touch of her shoes on the floor.

### Page 10

If she has taken "goddess" as her whole self, stepping down feels like falling from a height.

But if she slowly knows it was a noble and temporary role, then stepping down may hurt, yet it is not losing herself.

### Page 11

At recess, children run on the playground.

At first she only watches. Then a girl turns back and calls, "Come!" She hesitates, then takes the first step.

### Page 12

Her feet touch the earth, and dust rises softly.

She does not run fast, and her skirt is a little messy. But suddenly she laughs. The laugh needs no explanation and does not have to be solemn like a festival.

### Page 13

The children were quiet when the old man reached this part.

Little Stone asked softly, "Would it hurt to come down from so high?"

### Page 14

The old man said, "It would. Being looked up to can make the heart shine. Suddenly not being looked up to can make the heart feel empty."

"That is why Laozi says favor startles, and losing favor startles too."

### Page 15

Ahe remembered his red drum band.

He had been lead drummer for only one night, and his heart had gone up and down. If someone has been treated as a goddess since childhood, learning to return to herself is surely harder.

### Page 16

The old man said, "The hard thing is not sitting high."

"The hard thing is remembering, while high, that you still have feet; and when you come down, not believing that you have become nothing."

### Page 17

Ahe looked down at his own feet.

They had been muddy, had kicked stones, and had hurt after running too fast. They were ordinary, but they could bring him home.

## Return

### Page 18

Chapter Thirteen says that favor and disgrace both startle.

Being lifted high is one kind of startle. Coming down from high is another. If the heart lives only in whether others look up to us, it will always be blown about.

### Page 19

"Getting it is frightening, losing it is frightening" happens because people often mistake a temporary role for the self.

The red band is taken off. The goddess seat passes to another. Other people's eyes turn elsewhere. Roles come, and roles go.

### Page 20

"Honoring the body as the world" does not mean making oneself the greatest.

It means knowing that real life is more basic than titles. Eating, writing, walking, running, breathing: these are not bright, but they hold a person steady.

### Page 21

Laozi is not lightly saying, "Do not care."

He knows the heart is startled. So he reminds us not to hand the whole heart to favor that comes and goes, and not to lose ourselves when favor leaves.

### Page 22

Ahe wrote: seats change, titles change, applause changes.

Let the feet touch the ground, and let the heart touch the ground too. Then when seen, I do not fly away; when unseen, I do not fall apart.
