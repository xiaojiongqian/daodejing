# 第010-1章：先把自己收回来

## 原文

### 第1页

载营魄抱一，能无离乎？
专气致柔，能婴儿乎？
涤除玄览，能无疵乎？
爱国治民，能无知乎？

天门开阖，能为雌乎？
明白四达，能无为乎？
生之，畜之。
生而不有，为而不恃，长而不宰，是谓玄德。

## 释义

### 第2页

第十章一连问了好多“能不能”。

它不是考试题，也不是吓人的修炼口诀。它像老人轻轻问：你能不能先把散开的自己收回来？

### 第3页

“载营魄抱一”，可以先理解成身体在这里，心也在这里。

人一急，身体明明在桌边，心却跑去争输赢、想面子、怕出错。抱一，就是别让自己散成一地碎片。

### 第4页

“专气致柔”，是让气息不乱、不硬、不急。

像婴儿那样柔，不是装小，也不是没有力气。婴儿哭的时候哭，笑的时候笑，身子软，心里不藏那么多弯。

### 第5页

“涤除玄览”，像把心里的镜子擦一擦。灰尘少一点，看东西就少一点歪。

镜子脏时，不是世界全脏了；心急时，也不是每件事都真的坏了。

### 第6页

最后几句最深：“生而不有，为而不恃，长而不宰。”

养大它，却不把它据为己有；帮了忙，却不拿帮助压人；看着它长，却不把自己当成它的主人。

## 难词注解

### 第7页

“营魄”是古人用来讲人的身心、气血和精神的词。给孩子读时，可以把它理解成“身体和心神”。

“抱一”的意思是抱住一个中心，不让心乱跑。

“致柔”的意思是变得柔和。柔不是软塌塌，而是不僵、不硬顶。

### 第8页

“玄览”指的是很深的观看。这里可以把它想成心里那面照见事情的镜子。

“无为”的意思是不乱插手，也不硬控制。该做的做，不该扯的别扯。

“玄德”指的是很深、很不显眼的德。它像土里的根，看不见，却养着树。

## 正文

### 第9页

春天，老人给孩子们一盘小苗，让他们分种到村口。

阿禾被大家推出来带队。他端着木盘走在最前面，嘴上不停：“慢一点！别碰叶子！这一排要直！”

### 第10页

他越想负责，心越忙。

脚下有块小石头，他没有看见。木盘一斜，几团湿土滚到边上，小苗东倒西歪，像被惊醒的孩子。

### 第11页

阿禾的脸一下红了。他赶紧伸手，把一株小苗往直里扳。

“别歪，别歪。”他越急，手越重。细细的茎“啪”地折了一点。

### 第12页

孩子们都不说话了。

阿禾也愣住。他本来想照顾它，结果自己的急，把它弄伤了。

### 第13页

老人蹲下来，没有先碰小苗。

他说：“先别忙着管它们。你先回来。”

### 第14页

“我不是在这里吗？”阿禾低声说。

老人指了指他的脚，又指了指他的胸口：“脚在这里，心跑到‘我负责’和‘不能出错’里去了。心不在，手就会重。”

### 第15页

老人让他坐在石阶上，把木盘放稳。

阿禾听见自己的气息，一口进来，一口出去。刚才硬硬的肩膀，慢慢松了一点；攥紧的手，也松了一点。

### 第16页

老人拿来一面旧铜镜，上面蒙着薄灰。

阿禾用布轻轻擦。灰还在的时候，他看见自己的眉眼都是花的；擦过以后，天光出来了，他的脸也清楚了。

### 第17页

“心急的时候，看什么都像歪的。”老人说，“镜子先清一点，手才不乱。”

阿禾再去看那株小苗。它受了伤，但根还在。它不是需要命令，它需要土、水和一点安静。

### 第18页

阿禾给小苗添土，浇水，又找一根细竹签轻轻扶住。

这一次，他没有命令它“快快长好”。他只把能做的做好，然后把手收回来。

### 第19页

几天后，小苗没有立刻变得很高，却也没有死去。

新叶从旁边慢慢冒出来。有人经过，夸阿禾照顾得细。阿禾看见叶子向着阳光，心里反而不想把功劳拿出来晃。

## 回扣

### 第20页

第十章问“抱一，能无离乎”，阿禾想到那天的木盘。

身体和心在一起，手才稳；气息柔下来，眼睛才看得清；心里的镜子擦干净，事情才不会被急心照歪。

### 第21页

“明白四达，能无为乎”，也藏在照顾小苗里。

看见问题，不等于马上乱抓；知道方法，不等于把每一片叶子都管住。有时少一点硬管，生命才有自己的路。

### 第22页

“生而不有，为而不恃，长而不宰”，就是最深的德。

帮它立住，不等于它归我所有；照顾它长，不等于我要当它的主人。阿禾写下：真正深的德，常常做了很多，却不把万物攥在手里。

# Chapter 010-1: First Gather Yourself Back

## Original Text

### Page 1

When the intelligent and animal souls are held together in one embrace, they can be kept from separating.  When one gives undivided attention to the (vital) breath, and brings it to the utmost degree of pliancy, he can become as a (tender) babe.  When he has cleansed away the most mysterious sights (of his imagination), he can become without a flaw.

In loving the people and ruling the state, cannot he proceed without any (purpose of) action?  In the opening and shutting of his gates of heaven, cannot he do so as a female bird?  While his intelligence reaches in every direction, cannot he (appear to) be without knowledge?

(The Tao) produces (all things) and nourishes them; it produces them and does not claim them as its own; it does all, and yet does not boast of it; it presides over all, and yet does not control them. This is what is called 'The mysterious Quality' (of the Tao).

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Chapter Ten asks many "can you?" questions.

They are not exam questions, and not frightening magic words. They sound like an elder asking softly: can you first gather your scattered self back?

### Page 3

"Held together in one embrace" can first mean: the body is here, and the heart is here too.

When we hurry, the body may be beside the table, but the heart has run off to winning, face, or fear of mistakes. To hold one is not to scatter into pieces.

### Page 4

"Giving undivided attention to the breath" means letting breath become less rushed, less hard, less broken.

To be soft like a baby does not mean pretending to be small or having no strength. When a baby cries, the whole body cries; when a baby smiles, the whole face smiles. The body is soft, and the heart has fewer twists hidden inside.

### Page 5

"Cleansed away" is like wiping the mirror inside the heart. With less dust, we see things with less distortion.

When a mirror is dusty, the whole world has not become dirty. When the heart is rushed, not every matter has truly gone wrong.

### Page 6

The last lines go deepest: produce and nourish, yet do not claim, boast, or control.

Help something grow, but do not possess it. Help someone, but do not press them down with your help. Watch life grow, but do not make yourself its ruler.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 7

The phrase “held together in one embrace” means gathered into one, not scattered.

The phrase “undivided attention” means attention that is not split among many worries.

The word “breath” means the air moving in and out. Here it also points to inner steadiness.

### Page 8

The word “pliancy” means softness and flexibility, not stiffness.

The phrase “cleansed away” means wiped clean.

The phrase “mysterious Quality” means a deep virtue that is hard to see, like roots under the soil.

## Main Text

### Page 9

In spring, the old man gave the children a tray of young shoots to plant by the village gate.

Ahe was chosen to lead the group. He carried the wooden tray at the front and kept saying, "Slow down! Don't touch the leaves! This row must be straight!"

### Page 10

The more responsible he wanted to be, the busier his heart became.

There was a small stone at his feet, but he did not see it. The tray tilted, clumps of wet soil rolled to the edge, and the shoots leaned in every direction like startled children.

### Page 11

Ahe's face reddened. He hurried to straighten one shoot with his fingers.

"Don't lean, don't lean." The more anxious he became, the heavier his hand grew. The thin stem snapped a little.

### Page 12

All the children fell silent.

Ahe froze too. He had wanted to care for the shoot, but his own hurry had hurt it.

### Page 13

The old man crouched down, but he did not touch the shoot first.

"Do not rush to manage them," he said. "Come back first."

### Page 14

"Aren't I here?" Ahe said softly.

The old man pointed to his feet and then to his chest. "Your feet are here, but your heart has run into 'I am responsible' and 'I must not make a mistake.' When the heart is absent, the hand grows heavy."

### Page 15

He told Ahe to sit on the stone step and set the tray down steadily.

Ahe heard his own breathing: one breath entering, one breath leaving. His hard shoulders slowly loosened, and so did his clenched hands.

### Page 16

The old man brought an old bronze mirror, covered with a thin layer of dust.

Ahe wiped it gently. While the dust remained, his own brows and eyes looked blurred; after he wiped it, daylight appeared, and his face appeared clearly too.

### Page 17

"When the heart is rushed, everything looks crooked," said the old man. "Let the mirror clear first, and the hands will not be so wild."

Ahe looked again at the hurt shoot. It was injured, but its root was still there. It did not need commands. It needed soil, water, and a little quiet.

### Page 18

Ahe added soil, watered it, and placed a thin bamboo stick beside it for support.

This time, he did not command it, "Grow well quickly." He did what he could do, then drew back his hands.

### Page 19

A few days later, the shoot had not grown tall at once, but it had not died.

A new leaf slowly came out from the side. Someone passing by praised Ahe for caring for it carefully. Ahe saw the leaf turning toward the sun, and he no longer wanted to wave his credit around.

## Return to the Original

### Page 20

When Chapter Ten asks whether one can hold the souls together, Ahe thought of the wooden tray.

When body and heart are together, the hands steady. When breath softens, the eyes see more clearly. When the inner mirror is clean, things are not bent by haste.

### Page 21

"While intelligence reaches everywhere, can one proceed without action?" was also hidden in caring for the shoot.

Seeing a problem does not mean grabbing at once. Knowing a method does not mean controlling every leaf. Sometimes less hard management gives life room for its own way.

### Page 22

"Producing and nourishing, yet not claiming or controlling" is the deepest virtue.

Helping it stand does not mean it belongs to me. Caring for its growth does not mean I am its ruler. Ahe wrote: True deep virtue often does much, yet does not clutch living things in its hands.
