# 第009-2章：功成以后，手还抓着什么

## 典故引入

### 第1页

有一个古老的比喻，说一位富人田地大丰收。

谷子多到旧仓库放不下。他站在仓门口，看着金黄的粮食，心里不是先想到分给谁，也不是想到来年怎样种得更好。

### 第2页

他想的是：“我要拆掉旧仓库，盖更大的。把我的粮食、我的财物，全都放进去。”

他连对自己的灵魂说话时，也在说“我的”。仓库越大，那个“我”也越胀越大。

### 第3页

他以为，只要仓库够大，就能把安心也锁在里面。

可是故事说，就在那一夜，他的生命走到尽头。满仓的粮食一粒也没有跟他走。

### 第4页

这不是说人不该收粮，也不是说勤劳没有用。

它说的是：东西满了，若心还不知足，人就会把一生变成守仓库。最后守住了仓库，却守不住生命本身。

### 第5页

中国古代的范蠡，走的是另一条路。

他帮助越王勾践打败吴国，越国上下都知道他立了大功。这个时候，功劳像一只满到发亮的杯子，人人都看见。

### 第6页

如果他留下来，可能有高官厚禄，有车马，有门前的拜访。

可范蠡看见另一面：功劳太满，君主和大臣之间就容易变得危险。能一起吃苦的人，不一定能一起分享最亮的功名。

### 第7页

于是他离开朝堂，泛舟远去。

他还写信劝同僚文种：飞鸟射尽了，良弓就会被收起；狡兔捕尽了，猎狗也会有危险。事情做成后，要懂得退。

### 第8页

文种没有像他那样离开，后来果然遭祸。

这段故事有一点冷。它提醒人：功成之后，功劳不只是一朵花，也可能是一根绳子。你抓着它，它也抓着你。

### 第9页

范蠡后来改名陶朱公，经商致富。

更难得的是，他三次聚起很多财富，又三次把财富散给亲友乡邻。能聚，不被聚住；能有，不被“有”捆住。

### 第10页

所以范蠡不是逃避事情的人。

他能把事情做成，也能在做成以后离开；能把财富聚来，也能在财富变成重担前散开。这才叫知止。

### 第11页

古罗马有个人叫辛辛纳图斯。敌人围困罗马时，人们急得没有办法，跑到田里找他。

他那时正在扶犁种地。泥土沾在脚边，国家的危急却已经到了门前。

### 第12页

罗马人把很大的权力交给他，请他救急。

这种权力很诱人：一句话可以调兵，一道命令可以改变许多人的命运。许多人一坐上去，就不愿意下来。

### 第13页

辛辛纳图斯带兵解围，事情很快做成。

然后，他做了更难的一件事：把权力交回去，回到田里，继续扶他的犁。

### 第14页

两千多年后，华盛顿也遇到相似的考验。

他带领军队赢得独立战争。胜利以后，军队听他的话，许多人敬重他。若他想紧紧抓住权力，并不是没有机会。

### 第15页

可是他主动交还军权。后来做了总统，也在两届之后离开位置，回到自己的农庄。

他明白，权力若不肯退，就会从保护大家的工具，慢慢变成压住大家的石头。

### 第16页

这些故事里，“退”都不容易。

富人不肯退一步，所以仓库越盖越大；范蠡退了，才没有被功名困住；辛辛纳图斯和华盛顿退了，权力才没有被一个人攥成私物。

### 第17页

还有一个近代故事，像夜里的一声钟。

泰坦尼克号被许多人看成豪华、巨大、几乎不会沉没的大船。自信满到边上时，人就容易嫌提醒扫兴。

### 第18页

冰山警告没有被足够重视，救生艇也不够。船撞上冰山，沉入北大西洋，许多人没能回家。

“满”的危险，有时不是杯子里的水洒出来，而是骄傲让眼睛看不见前面的冰。

## 回扣

### 第19页

老子说“持而盈之，不如其已”，先讲一个物理道理：太满就端不稳。

接着它变成心理道理：太想拥有，就被拥有牵住；太想保住，就把一生变成守卫。

### 第20页

“金玉满堂，莫之能守”，不是讨厌金玉。

它看见的是：若心无分寸，财富越多，担心越多；仓库越大，夜里越睡不稳。

### 第21页

“富贵而骄，自遗其咎”，说的是更深一层。

富贵让人觉得自己站得高，看得远；骄傲却常常让人低估冰山、听不进劝告、看不见月满会缺。

### 第22页

“功遂身退，天之道”，不是叫人半路逃走。

它先要求“功遂”：事要做成，责任要尽到。然后才说“身退”：事已经能自己往前走，自己就别再把身体压在上面。

### 第23页

退，不是把成果丢掉。

退，是把成果从“我的”手里放出来，让它成为大家的路、大家的灯、大家能继续使用的东西。

### 第24页

阿禾写下：会拿起，是本事；会放下，是更深的本事。

满以前停，成以后退，亮起来以后不挡住光。这不是少做，而是让事情活得更久。

# Chapter 009-2: After Success, What Is the Hand Still Holding?

## Allusion

### Page 1

An old parable tells of a rich man whose fields produced a great harvest.

There was so much grain that his old barns could not hold it. He stood at the barn door, looking at the golden grain. His first thought was not whom to share with, nor how to plant better next year.

### Page 2

He thought, "I will tear down the old barns and build bigger ones. I will store all my grain and all my goods there."

Even when he spoke to his own soul, he kept saying "my." The larger the barns became, the larger that "me" swelled.

### Page 3

He thought that if the barns were large enough, peace could be locked inside them too.

But the story says that very night his life came to its end. Not one grain from the full barns went with him.

### Page 4

This does not mean people should not harvest grain, and it does not mean hard work is useless.

It means this: when things are full but the heart still has no enough, a person's life becomes guarding barns. In the end, the barns may be kept, but life itself cannot be kept.

### Page 5

In ancient China, Fan Li took another road.

He helped King Goujian of Yue defeat Wu, and everyone in Yue knew he had done great work. At that moment, his credit was like a cup filled to the shining edge.

### Page 6

If he stayed, he might have received high office, rich gifts, carriages, and visitors at his gate.

But Fan Li saw the other side: when credit is too full, the space between ruler and minister can become dangerous. People who share hardship do not always share bright fame well.

### Page 7

So he left the court and sailed away.

He also wrote to his colleague Wen Zhong: when the birds are gone, the good bow is put away; when the hares are gone, the hunting dog is in danger. When the work is done, one must know how to withdraw.

### Page 8

Wen Zhong did not leave as Fan Li did, and later disaster came to him.

This part of the story is cold. It reminds us: after success, credit is not only a flower. It can also become a rope. If you hold it, it may hold you.

### Page 9

Fan Li later changed his name to Tao Zhu Gong and became a wealthy merchant.

What is rarer is that three times he gathered great wealth, and three times he gave it away to relatives, friends, and neighbors. He could gather without being trapped by gathering; he could have without being bound by having.

### Page 10

So Fan Li was not a person who escaped work.

He could complete the work and then leave. He could gather wealth and then scatter it before it became a weight. This is knowing where to stop.

### Page 11

In ancient Rome, there was a man named Cincinnatus. When enemies trapped Rome, the people had no better choice and came to find him in the fields.

He was holding a plough. Soil lay near his feet, while the country's danger had reached his door.

### Page 12

The Romans gave him great power and asked him to save them.

Such power is tempting: one word can move armies, and one order can change many lives. Many people, once seated there, do not wish to rise.

### Page 13

Cincinnatus led the army and relieved the danger.

Then he did the harder thing: he returned the power, went back to his field, and took up his plough again.

### Page 14

More than two thousand years later, George Washington met a similar test.

He led an army to win independence. After victory, soldiers listened to him and many people honored him. If he had wanted to hold power tightly, there was a chance.

### Page 15

But he gave back military power. Later, after serving two terms as president, he left office and returned to his farm.

He understood that power which refuses to withdraw can slowly change from a tool that protects people into a stone that presses on them.

### Page 16

In these stories, withdrawing is never easy.

The rich man would not step back, so his barns grew larger and larger. Fan Li withdrew, so credit did not trap him. Cincinnatus and Washington withdrew, so power was not clenched into one person's possession.

### Page 17

There is also a modern story, like a bell in the night.

The Titanic was seen by many as luxurious, enormous, almost unable to sink. When confidence is filled to the rim, warnings can feel annoying.

### Page 18

Ice warnings were not taken seriously enough, and there were not enough lifeboats. The ship struck an iceberg and sank in the North Atlantic, and many people did not come home.

The danger of fullness is not always water spilling from a cup. Sometimes pride keeps the eyes from seeing the ice ahead.

## Return to the Original

### Page 19

When Laozi says it is better not to carry what is full to the brim, he begins with a physical truth: what is too full cannot be carried steadily.

Then it becomes a truth of the heart: wanting to possess too much makes possession pull us; wanting to guard too much turns life into guarding.

### Page 20

"When gold and jade fill the hall, their possessor cannot keep them safe" does not hate gold and jade.

It sees this: without measure in the heart, more wealth means more worry; larger barns mean less sleep.

### Page 21

"When wealth and honors lead to arrogance, this brings its evil on itself" goes one layer deeper.

Wealth and honor can make people feel high and far-seeing; arrogance often makes them underestimate icebergs, ignore advice, and forget that the full moon must wane.

### Page 22

"When the work is done, withdraw" does not tell people to run away halfway.

First comes "the work is done": the task must be completed and responsibility fulfilled. Only then comes withdrawal: when the matter can move forward, do not keep pressing your body on top of it.

### Page 23

Withdrawing does not throw away the result.

It releases the result from the hand of "mine," so it can become a road, a lamp, and a useful thing for many people.

### Page 24

Ahe wrote: To pick something up is ability. To put it down is a deeper ability.

Stop before fullness spills. Step back after success. Do not stand in front of the light once it is shining. This is not doing less; it lets the work live longer.
