# 第008-1章：水往低处去

## 原文

### 第1页

上善若水。
水善利万物而不争，处众人之所恶，故几于道。

居善地，心善渊，与善仁，言善信，正善治，事善能，动善时。
夫唯不争，故无尤。

## 释义

### 第2页

第八章说，最高的善，像水。

水不是因为响亮才好，也不是因为站得高才好。它好在能让许多生命继续活下去，却不把功劳挂在自己身上。

### 第3页

河上公说，水在天上是雾露，在地上是泉源。草木得水而生，人也靠水而活。

可是水常去低处、湿处、浑处。大家嫌那里脏、嫌那里不好看，水却不嫌。

### 第4页

这不是水“没出息”。低处能接住水，水也能接住低处的需要。

所以“处众人之所恶”，不是故意找苦吃，而是不把好看、显眼、被夸放在最前面。

### 第5页

“居善地”，是待在合适的位置；“心善渊”，是心里有深处，不被一点响动搅乱。

“与善仁”，是给出去，像水润物；“言善信”，是说话像清水照影，不把真相照歪。

### 第6页

“正善治”，是把乱处慢慢洗平；“事善能”，是方杯里成方，圆碗里成圆，事情需要怎样就怎样做。

“动善时”，是该流时流，该停时停。冬天结冰，春天化开，水不和时节硬拧。

## 难词注解

### 第7页

**上善**：很高很高的善，不是做给人看的好，而是让生命真正受益。

**几于道**：很接近“道”。不是完全说尽了道，而是很像道的样子。

**善利万物**：善于帮助许多生命，不只帮助自己喜欢的那一个。

### 第8页

**处众人之所恶**：待在别人嫌低、嫌湿、嫌脏的地方。

**善渊**：像深水潭一样安静、有深度。

**无尤**：少有怨怪。因为不争功、不抢位，事情反而少生麻烦。

## 正文

### 第9页

雨停以后，空空村后山多了一股水。

它从石缝里钻出来，先绕过一块青石，又往坡下的泥洼里去。泥洼低低的，积着落叶，还有一点不好闻的烂草味。

### 第10页

小石头捂住鼻子：“它为什么偏要去那里？那边又湿又脏。”

阿禾看见村口新修的石阶，心里一亮：“不如把水引过去。水从石阶旁边流，一定很漂亮。”

### 第11页

孩子们搬来小石子，排出一条直直的水道。

水被拦住，先停了一会儿。后面的水继续来，前面的水没地方走，就在石子后面慢慢涨高。

### 第12页

“再堵紧一点！”小石头喊。

他们把缝也塞住。水终于被挤进直道，可走了没几步，就从边上漫出来，冲散了刚摆好的泥炉子。

### 第13页

阿禾急了，拿树枝去拨水：“这边，这边！”

水不听他的话。它一会儿钻进草根，一会儿绕到石头背后，像是不肯参加孩子们安排好的队伍。

### 第14页

老人走过来，没有立刻说教。他只问：“你们想让水好看，还是想让水有用？”

孩子们安静下来。高处的石阶干干净净，旁边的花已经喝足了雨；坡下那片泥洼里，却有几株菜苗蔫着叶子。

### 第15页

老人拨开烂草，露出细细的根。

“低处不是没人要的地方。”他说，“只是大家常常不愿意低头看。”

### 第16页

阿禾把堵住的石子一颗一颗移开，又顺着坡势挖了一条浅浅的小沟。

水先浑了一阵，带着泥沙往下走。过了一会儿，沙沉住了，清水慢慢贴着菜苗根边流。

### 第17页

小石头蹲下来，看见一只小虫从湿土里爬出来，又看见菜叶一点一点挺起。

“它不是怕高。”他说，“它是知道哪里渴。”

### 第18页

傍晚，水流到村口，先洗过孩子们踩脏的木屐，又流进菜畦旁的小沟。

谁也没有给它挂一块牌子，写“今天辛苦了”。可晚饭时，桌上多了一把水灵灵的青菜。

## 回扣

### 第19页

第八章说“水善利万物而不争”。水不和孩子争“该走哪条漂亮的路”，可它也没有停下不走。

它顺着地势，找到真正需要它的地方。低处、弯处、暗处，它都不嫌，因为那里能接住它，也等着它。

### 第20页

不争，不是没力量。水的力量藏在能看见需要、能顺势而行、能一直滋养里。

阿禾写下：小溪不抢石阶旁的好看，所以能到菜苗身边。真正好的东西，有时就是这样，把自己放低，把生命扶起来。

# Chapter 008-1: Water Goes Low

## Original Text

### Page 1

The highest excellence is like (that of) water.  The excellence of water appears in its benefiting all things, and in its occupying, without striving (to the contrary), the low place which all men dislike.  Hence (its way) is near to (that of) the Tao.

The excellence of a residence is in (the suitability of) the place; that of the mind is in abysmal stillness; that of associations is in their being with the virtuous; that of government is in its securing good order; that of (the conduct of) affairs is in its ability; and that of (the initiation of) any movement is in its timeliness.

And when (one with the highest excellence) does not wrangle (about his low position), no one finds fault with him.

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Chapter Eight says that the highest goodness is like water.

Water is not good because it is loud, and not because it stands high. It is good because it lets many lives continue, without hanging the credit on itself.

### Page 3

Heshang Gong says water becomes mist and dew in the sky, and springs on the earth. Plants live by water, and people live by water too.

Yet water often goes to low, wet, muddy places. People may dislike those places because they are dirty or plain, but water does not.

### Page 4

This does not mean water has "no future." Low places can receive water, and water can receive the needs of low places.

So occupying the place others dislike does not mean looking for suffering. It means not placing beauty, attention, and praise first.

### Page 5

"A good residence" means being in the fitting place. "A good mind" is deep, like a pool not disturbed by every noise.

"Good giving" nourishes like water. "Good words" are trustworthy, like clear water that does not bend the truth of a reflection.

### Page 6

"Good rule" slowly washes disorder into order. "Good work" can become square in a square cup and round in a round bowl, doing what the matter needs.

"Good movement" moves at the right time. Water freezes in winter and melts in spring. It does not argue with the season.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 7

**highest excellence** — Very high goodness: not goodness for show, but goodness that truly helps life.

**benefiting all things** — Helping many living things, not only the one we happen to like.

**low place** — A place others may avoid, but where help can be received.

### Page 8

**abysmal stillness** — Deep stillness, like a quiet pool.

**timeliness** — Doing something at the fitting time.

**wrangle** — Argue and fight over position or credit.

## Main Text

### Page 9

After the rain, a thread of water appeared behind Kongkong Village.

It slipped out of a crack in the stone, curved around a green rock, and headed toward a muddy hollow. The hollow was low, full of fallen leaves and a sour smell of rotting grass.

### Page 10

Little Stone covered his nose. "Why does it have to go there? That place is wet and dirty."

Ahe saw the new stone steps at the village gate and had an idea. "Let's guide the water there. It will look beautiful flowing beside the steps."

### Page 11

The children carried small stones and laid out a straight channel.

The water was blocked and paused. More water came from behind; the front water had nowhere to go, so it slowly rose behind the stones.

### Page 12

"Block it tighter!" Little Stone called.

They closed even the gaps. At last the water was squeezed into their straight channel, but after a few steps it spilled over the side and washed apart a small mud stove they had just made.

### Page 13

Ahe grew anxious and poked at the stream with a branch. "This way, this way!"

The water did not obey him. It slipped into grass roots, then curved behind a stone, as if refusing to join the line the children had arranged.

### Page 14

The old man came over, but he did not begin with a lesson. He only asked, "Do you want the water to look useful, or to be useful?"

The children fell quiet. The high stone steps were clean and dry, and the flowers nearby had drunk enough rain. Down in the hollow, several vegetable shoots hung their leaves.

### Page 15

The old man lifted away the rotting grass and showed the thin roots beneath.

"A low place is not a useless place," he said. "It is only a place people often do not lower their heads to see."

### Page 16

Ahe moved the blocking stones away one by one, then dug a shallow ditch along the slope.

The water ran muddy for a while, carrying silt downward. After a time, the sand settled, and clear water flowed beside the roots of the shoots.

### Page 17

Little Stone crouched and saw a small insect crawl out of the damp soil. He saw the vegetable leaves slowly lift.

"It wasn't afraid of being high," he said. "It knew where the thirst was."

### Page 18

By evening, the water reached the village gate. It washed the mud from the children's wooden clogs, then flowed into the ditch beside the vegetable beds.

No one hung a sign over it saying, "You worked hard today." But at supper, a fresh green handful appeared on the table.

## Return to the Original

### Page 19

Chapter Eight says water benefits all things and does not strive. Water did not argue with the children about which pretty road it should take, but it did not stop moving either.

It followed the land and found the place that truly needed it. Low places, curved places, dim places: it did not dislike them, because they could receive it and were waiting for it.

### Page 20

Not striving is not having no strength. Water's strength is hidden in seeing need, moving with the shape of things, and continuing to nourish.

Ahe wrote: The stream did not fight for the beautiful place beside the steps, so it reached the vegetable shoots. What is truly good may lower itself and help life stand up.
