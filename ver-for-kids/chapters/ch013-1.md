# 第013-1章：被夸和被笑，都别把心交出去

## 原文

### 第1页

宠辱若惊，贵大患若身。

何谓宠辱若惊？
宠为下，得之若惊，失之若惊，是谓宠辱若惊。

何谓贵大患若身？
吾所以有大患者，为吾有身，及吾无身，吾有何患？

故贵以身为天下，若可寄天下；
爱以身为天下，若可托天下。

## 释义

### 第2页

第十三章讲两件容易搅动人的事：被宠爱，和被羞辱。

老子说，这两件事看起来一高一低，其实都可能让人惊慌。得到宠爱怕失去，失去宠爱怕被看轻，心就被外面的眼光牵来牵去。

### 第3页

“宠为下”，不是说被喜欢一定不好。

它提醒我们：如果一个人要靠别人的宠爱才觉得自己有价值，那他其实把自己放在了很低、很不稳的位置。

### 第4页

“贵大患若身”，可以先这样理解：把大的忧患看得像自己的身体一样真实。

有身体，就会饿、会累、会疼、会怕失去。所以人很容易被得失惊动。

### 第5页

可是老子又说，真正能珍惜自身的人，才可以把天下托付给他。

因为他知道生命珍贵，就不会为了掌声乱用自己，也不会为了面子伤害别人。

### 第6页

把自己看得太重，会处处受惊。

把生命看得珍贵，却不把名声和角色当成自己，反而能稳。第十三章的难处，就在这里。

## 难词注解

### 第7页

“宠”指的是被喜欢、被重视、被推到亮处。

“辱”指的是被轻看、被羞辱、被推到低处。

“若惊”的意思是像受惊一样，心一下子乱了。

### 第8页

“大患”指的是很大的忧患、麻烦或危险。

“有身”不只指有这个身体，也指对“我”的执着。

“寄天下”和“托天下”的意思，都是把天下托付给一个人治理或照看。

## 正文

### 第9页

灯会那天，空空村要排一场小鼓舞。

老人把红绸带递给阿禾：“你来领第一声鼓。大家听你的鼓点起步。”

### 第10页

阿禾的耳朵一下热了。

小石头拍手：“阿禾当领鼓人！”几个孩子也围过来，看那条红绸带。阿禾觉得胸口像塞进一只会跳的小鸟。

### 第11页

练习时，他把鼓槌举得很高。

别人慢一点，他就皱眉；别人敲错一下，他就叹气。他嘴上说“别出错”，心里却在想：“大家都在看我。”

### 第12页

晚上，灯笼亮起来，鼓舞开始。

第一声鼓应该沉沉落下。可是阿禾看见台下那么多脸，一紧张，鼓槌打偏了，声音“啪”地散开。

### 第13页

队伍乱了一下。

小石头赶紧接了一声鼓，把大家带回来。台下有两个小孩笑出声。阿禾的脸烧起来，比灯笼还红。

### 第14页

鼓舞结束后，有人夸小石头反应快。

也有人说：“阿禾今天怎么慌了？”这句话不重，却像小刺扎进阿禾心里。他把红绸带塞进袖子，连饭也不想吃。

### 第15页

老人坐到他旁边，问：“早上大家夸你时，你是不是也吃不下饭？”

阿禾想了想：“那时不是吃不下，是太高兴了，忘了饿。”

### 第16页

老人说：“你看，夸你时，你的肚子也被带走；笑你时，你的肚子也被带走。”

“一条红绸带，早上把你抬起来，晚上又把你摔下去。它到底有多大力气？”

### 第17页

阿禾低头看那条红绸带。

它只是一条布。真正把他抬高又摔低的，是他自己把心交给了别人眼里的“领鼓人”。

### 第18页

老人把一碗热饭推到他面前。

“先吃饭。”老人说，“身体饿了，别让面子替它做主。一个连自己这碗饭都照看不好的人，怎么照看一队人的鼓点？”

### 第19页

阿禾慢慢吃完饭，又去找小石头。

他说：“谢谢你刚才接住鼓点。”小石头笑：“你明天也会接住我的。”

### 第20页

第二天，孩子们再练鼓。

阿禾还是认真，却不再把每一次目光都当成判决。有人敲错，他先把鼓点稳住，再教他慢慢跟上。

## 回扣

### 第21页

第十三章说“宠辱若惊”。

阿禾懂了一点：被夸时心飞起来，被笑时心摔下去，看似一个高一个低，其实都让自己离开了脚下。

### 第22页

“得之若惊，失之若惊”，不只发生在大人做官、得奖、失势时。

孩子也会这样：被老师表扬会怕下次不够好，被朋友笑一下会觉得自己全坏了。

### 第23页

“贵以身为天下”，先从珍惜自己的身体开始。

该吃饭时吃饭，该睡觉时睡觉，该认错时认错。身体和心稳一点，才不容易被宠和辱牵走。

### 第24页

阿禾写下：红绸带可以戴在身上，不要戴进心里。

别人夸我，我好好做事；别人笑我，我也好好做事。鼓点要稳，心也要稳。

# Chapter 013-1: Do Not Hand Your Heart to Praise or Laughter

## Original Text

### Page 1

Favour and disgrace would seem equally to be feared; honour and great calamity, to be regarded as personal conditions (of the same kind).

What is meant by speaking thus of favour and disgrace?  Disgrace is being in a low position (after the enjoyment of favour).  The getting that (favour) leads to the apprehension (of losing it), and the losing it leads to the fear of (still greater calamity):--this is what is meant by saying that favour and disgrace would seem equally to be feared.

And what is meant by saying that honour and great calamity are to be (similarly) regarded as personal conditions?  What makes me liable to great calamity is my having the body (which I call myself); if I had not the body, what great calamity could come to me?

Therefore he who would administer the kingdom, honouring it as he honours his own person, may be employed to govern it, and he who would administer it with the love which he bears to his own person may be entrusted with it.

## Interpretation

### Page 2

Chapter Thirteen speaks of two things that easily shake people: being favored and being disgraced.

Laozi says these two look high and low, yet both can frighten the heart. When favor comes, we fear losing it. When favor leaves, we fear being looked down on. The heart is pulled by outside eyes.

### Page 3

"Favor is low" does not mean being liked is always bad.

It warns us: if a person needs another's favor in order to feel valuable, he has placed himself in a low and unstable position.

### Page 4

"Great calamity as the body" can first mean this: great trouble feels as real and close as one's own body.

Because we have a body, we hunger, tire, hurt, and fear loss. So gain and loss easily startle us.

### Page 5

Yet Laozi also says that one who truly cherishes the body may be entrusted with the world.

Because he knows life is precious, he will not spend himself wildly for applause, and he will not harm others for face.

### Page 6

Taking the self too heavily makes one frightened at every turn.

Cherishing life, while not mistaking fame and roles for the self, brings steadiness. This is the difficulty of Chapter Thirteen.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 7

The word “favor” means being liked, honored, or placed in the bright spot.

The word “disgrace” means being looked down on, shamed, or pushed low.

Here, the word “feared” means being startled and made restless.

### Page 8

The word “calamity” means a great trouble or danger.

The phrase “having the body” means having this body, and also clinging to the idea of “me.”

The word “entrusted” means being given something important to care for.

## Main Text

### Page 9

On the lantern festival night, Kongkong Village planned a small drum dance.

The old man handed a red silk band to Ahe. "You will lead the first drumbeat. Everyone will begin by listening to you."

### Page 10

Ahe's ears grew hot.

Little Stone clapped. "Ahe is the lead drummer!" Several children gathered to look at the red band. Ahe felt as if a hopping bird had been placed in his chest.

### Page 11

During practice, he lifted the drumstick very high.

If someone was slow, he frowned. If someone missed a beat, he sighed. He said, "Do not make mistakes," but inside he thought, "Everyone is watching me."

### Page 12

At night, the lanterns lit, and the drum dance began.

The first beat should have fallen deep and steady. But Ahe saw all the faces below, grew tense, and struck off-center. The sound cracked and scattered.

### Page 13

The line faltered.

Little Stone quickly gave another beat and brought everyone back. Two small children below laughed. Ahe's face burned redder than the lanterns.

### Page 14

After the dance, someone praised Little Stone's quick response.

Someone else said, "Why did Ahe panic today?" The words were not heavy, but they pricked Ahe's heart. He stuffed the red band into his sleeve and did not want supper.

### Page 15

The old man sat beside him. "This morning, when everyone praised you, did you also lose your appetite?"

Ahe thought. "Not exactly. I was so happy I forgot I was hungry."

### Page 16

The old man said, "Look. When they praised you, your belly was carried away. When they laughed at you, your belly was carried away too."

"A strip of red silk lifted you in the morning and dropped you at night. How strong is it really?"

### Page 17

Ahe looked down at the red band.

It was only cloth. What had lifted and dropped him was his own heart, handed to the "lead drummer" in other people's eyes.

### Page 18

The old man pushed a bowl of warm rice toward him.

"Eat first," he said. "Your body is hungry. Do not let face make its decisions. If a person cannot care for this bowl of rice, how can he care for a line of drumbeats?"

### Page 19

Ahe slowly finished the rice and went to find Little Stone.

"Thank you for catching the beat," he said. Little Stone smiled. "Tomorrow you will catch mine."

### Page 20

The next day, the children practiced again.

Ahe was still serious, but he no longer treated every glance as a judgment. When someone missed a beat, he first steadied the rhythm, then helped the child follow it.

## Return

### Page 21

Chapter Thirteen says that favor and disgrace can both startle.

Ahe understood a little. When praise made his heart fly, and laughter made it fall, both carried him away from the ground beneath his feet.

### Page 22

"Getting it is frightening, losing it is frightening" does not only happen when adults gain office, awards, or power.

Children know it too: being praised by a teacher can make one fear next time; being laughed at by a friend can make one feel entirely bad.

### Page 23

"Honoring the body as the world" begins with cherishing one's own body.

Eat when it is time to eat, sleep when it is time to sleep, and admit a mistake when it is time. When body and heart are steadier, favor and disgrace cannot drag them away so easily.

### Page 24

Ahe wrote: the red band may be worn on the body, but not inside the heart.

If others praise me, I do the work well. If others laugh at me, I still do the work well. The drumbeat should be steady, and so should the heart.
