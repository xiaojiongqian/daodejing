# 第014-1章：摸不着的一

## 原文

### 第1页

视之不见名曰夷，听之不闻名曰希，搏之不得名曰微。
此三者，不可致诘，故混而为一。

其上不皦，其下不昧。
绳绳不可名，复归于无物。

### 第2页

是谓无状之状，无物之象，是谓惚恍。

迎之不见其首，随之不见其后。

执古之道，以御今之有。
能知古始，是谓道纪。

## 释义

### 第3页

第十四章讲一种很难抓住的东西。

看，看不见；听，听不到；伸手去抓，也抓不住。可它不是完全没有，因为万物又好像都从它那里得到秩序。

### 第4页

“夷”，可以先理解成看不见形状。

“希”，是听不见声音，或者声音细到像快要没有。

“微”，是小到、深到、细到手抓不住。

### 第5页

老子说，这三样最后“混而为一”。

不是三件奇怪的东西拼在一起，而是说：真正的“道”不一定站成一个物件让你看、让你听、让你拿。

### 第6页

“迎之不见其首，随之不见其后”，像追一阵风。

你迎上去，看不见它从哪里开始；你追在后面，也看不见它在哪里结束。可树叶动了，水面皱了，你知道它来过。

### 第7页

“执古之道，以御今之有”，不是要人死守旧东西。

它是说，有些根本道理很古老，今天仍然能帮我们处理眼前的事。水往低处流，种子要土和时节，人心太满就听不进话，这些古老得像早晨的光。

## 难词注解

### 第8页

**夷**：平而无形，这里指看不见的样子。

**希**：稀少、细微，这里指听不清或听不见。

**微**：细小、幽深，不能用手抓住。

### 第9页

**不可致诘**：不能一层一层追问到像普通物件那样说清。

**惚恍**：若有若无、难以固定的样子。

**道纪**：道的线索、纲纪，像一卷线的头绪。

## 正文

### 第10页

阿禾想画一幅“道”的图。

他铺开纸，磨好墨，先画一条路。画完又摇头：“路只是路，不是道。”

### 第11页

他又画一条溪。

溪水弯弯绕绕，很像前几章说过的水。可他想了想，又摇头：“水像道，却也不是道全部。”

### 第12页

小石头跑来帮忙。

他说：“画风吧。风看不见，最像。”阿禾拿笔点了半天，只画出几片斜斜的叶子。

### 第13页

“你看，”小石头说，“风自己画不出来。”

老人从旁边走过，笑了一下：“画不出风，不等于风没有来。”

### 第14页

老人带他们到晒谷场。

谷粒摊在竹席上，太阳照着，风轻轻过来。阿禾看不见风的头，也抓不到风的尾，可谷壳被吹到一边，稻香从席面浮起来。

### 第15页

他们又走到老水渠旁。

这条水渠是很久以前的村民挖的，贴着地势绕过石头，低处接水，高处不硬冲。去年大雨时，新修的直沟被冲坏，老水渠却还稳稳把水送到田边。

### 第16页

老人说：“这就叫旧道理管今天的事。”

“古人不知道我们的鞋子、灯和纸风车，可他们知道水性、土性、人心。这些东西，今天还是今天。”

### 第17页

阿禾蹲在水渠边，看见水沿着旧痕往前走。

旧痕不是水，水也不是道。可是那种“顺着地势、不硬拧”的道理，从很久以前走到今天，仍然有用。

### 第18页

傍晚，阿禾回到纸前。

他没有再画一个大大的“道”。他画了一只转动的车轮、一口空罐、一扇开着的窗、一条弯曲的水渠，还有几片被风吹起的谷壳。

### 第19页

小石头问：“这就是道吗？”

阿禾说：“不是。可是它们像道留下的脚印。”

## 回扣

### 第20页

第十四章说“视之不见，听之不闻，搏之不得”。

阿禾明白了一点：有些真实，不是因为看不见就不存在。风看不见，谷壳会动；道摸不着，万物却顺着它生长变化。

### 第21页

“混而为一”，不是把眼睛、耳朵、手都弄糊涂。

它提醒我们：不要以为只有被看见、听见、抓住的东西才是真的。世界还有更深的线索，藏在事物怎样运行里面。

### 第22页

“执古之道，以御今之有”，也不是抱着旧东西不放。

是从古老的道理里找到活的线索，用来照看今天的水渠、今天的心、今天要做的事。

### 第23页

阿禾写下：看不见的，不一定空；抓不住的，不一定无用。

有时候，看见脚印，就知道有路；看见万物各归其所，就知道道在悄悄经过。

# Chapter 014-1: The One That Cannot Be Held

## Original Text

### Page 1

We look at it, and we do not see it, and we name it 'the Equable.'  We listen to it, and we do not hear it, and we name it 'the Inaudible.'  We try to grasp it, and do not get hold of it, and we name it 'the Subtle.'  With these three qualities, it cannot be made the subject of description; and hence we blend them together and obtain The One.

Its upper part is not bright, and its lower part is not obscure.  Ceaseless in its action, it yet cannot be named, and then it again returns and becomes nothing.  This is called the Form of the Formless, and the Semblance of the Invisible; this is called the Fleeting and Indeterminable.

### Page 2

We meet it and do not see its Front; we follow it, and do not see its Back.  When we can lay hold of the Tao of old to direct the things of the present day, and are able to know it as it was of old in the beginning, this is called (unwinding) the clue of Tao.

## Interpretation

### Page 3

Chapter Fourteen speaks of something very hard to grasp.

We look, but do not see it. We listen, but do not hear it. We reach out, but cannot hold it. Yet it is not simply nothing, because all things seem to receive order from it.

### Page 4

"Equable" can first mean without a visible shape.

"Inaudible" means soundless, or so fine that it is nearly no sound.

"Subtle" means so small, deep, and fine that the hand cannot seize it.

### Page 5

Laozi says these three blend into one.

This does not mean three strange things are glued together. It means the living Dao does not always stand before us as an object to see, hear, or hold.

### Page 6

"We meet it and do not see its front; we follow it and do not see its back" is like chasing wind.

When you face it, you cannot see where it begins. When you run after it, you cannot see where it ends. Yet leaves move, water wrinkles, and you know it has passed.

### Page 7

"Holding the Tao of old to direct what exists today" does not mean clinging to old things.

It means that some root truths are ancient and still help with present matters. Water flows downward, seeds need soil and season, and a heart too full cannot hear. These are as old as morning light.

## Vocabulary Notes

### Page 8

**Equable** — Even and without a form that can be seen.

**Inaudible** — Unable to be heard.

**Subtle** — Fine, deep, and hard to grasp.

### Page 9

**cannot be made the subject of description** — Cannot be explained like an ordinary object piece by piece.

**Fleeting and indeterminable** — Seeming to be there and not there, impossible to fix.

**clue of Tao** — A guiding thread, like the loose end of a spool.

## Main Text

### Page 10

Ahe wanted to draw a picture of the Dao.

He spread paper, ground ink, and first drew a road. When he finished, he shook his head. "A road is only a road, not the Dao."

### Page 11

Then he drew a stream.

The stream curved and turned, much like the water from earlier chapters. But after thinking, he shook his head again. "Water is like the Dao, but not the whole Dao."

### Page 12

Little Stone ran over to help.

"Draw wind," he said. "Wind cannot be seen. It is most like it." Ahe dotted the brush for a long time and drew only a few slanting leaves.

### Page 13

"See?" Little Stone said. "Wind itself cannot be drawn."

The old man passed by and smiled. "Not being able to draw wind does not mean wind has not come."

### Page 14

He brought them to the grain-drying ground.

Grains lay spread on bamboo mats. Sunlight shone, and wind came softly. Ahe could not see the wind's head or grasp its tail, but husks blew aside and the scent of rice rose from the mat.

### Page 15

They walked on to the old irrigation ditch.

Long ago, villagers had dug it along the land's slope. It curved around stones, received water in low places, and did not force itself through high places. Last year, heavy rain broke a new straight ditch, but the old ditch still carried water to the field.

### Page 16

The old man said, "This is an old truth guiding today's matter."

"The old villagers did not know our shoes, lamps, or paper pinwheels. But they knew water, soil, and the human heart. These are still themselves today."

### Page 17

Ahe crouched by the ditch and watched the water follow the old marks.

The marks were not water, and water was not the Dao. Yet the principle of following the land and not forcing it had walked from long ago into today, still useful.

### Page 18

At dusk, Ahe returned to his paper.

He did not draw one large thing called "Dao." He drew a turning wheel, an empty jar, an open window, a curving ditch, and a few husks lifted by wind.

### Page 19

Little Stone asked, "Is this the Dao?"

Ahe said, "No. But they are like footprints it leaves."

## Return

### Page 20

Chapter Fourteen says that we cannot see it, hear it, or grasp it.

Ahe understood a little: some realities do not stop existing because they are unseen. Wind is unseen, yet husks move. The Dao cannot be held, yet things grow and change by it.

### Page 21

"Blending into one" does not mean confusing eyes, ears, and hands.

It reminds us not to think that only what can be seen, heard, and held is real. The world has deeper threads, hidden in how things work.

### Page 22

"Holding the Tao of old to direct what exists today" does not mean clutching old objects.

It means finding living clues in ancient truth and using them to care for today's ditch, today's heart, and today's work.

### Page 23

Ahe wrote: what cannot be seen is not always empty; what cannot be held is not always useless.

Sometimes, seeing footprints tells us there is a road. Seeing things find their place tells us the Dao has quietly passed.
