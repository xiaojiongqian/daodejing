# 第000-1章：一个爱看水的老人

## 第1页

这一天，阿禾比平常来得早。

村口的大树还带着清晨的凉气。他把"名字本"放在膝盖上，手指压着第一页，想了想，才问老人：

"那句'道可道，非常道'，最早是谁留下来的？"

## 第2页

老人没有马上回答。

他先望了望远处的山，又低头看树根旁的一小股水。那点水慢慢往前流，遇到细草，就绕一下；遇到土块，就停一停，再找路过去。

"人们通常会说：老子。"老人说，"不过，讲老子，先别把他想成高高在上的神仙。你可以先记住他这个样子：一位很爱看水的老人。"

阿禾在本子上写下：老子。

## 第3页

"老子是他的真名吗？"小兔问。

"不像我们今天这样写在户口本上的名字。"老人说，"有的书说他姓李，名耳，也叫老聃。'老子'更像一种尊称，意思是很有智慧的老先生。"

史书里关于他的记录不多，后人讲的故事却很多。读老子的时候，要像分豆子一样，把"大概可信的记录"和"后来长出来的传说"轻轻分开。

## 第4页

老子大约生活在两千五百年前，比秦始皇统一天下还早很久。

那时是东周后期，天下被许多诸侯国分着。今天这个国家和那个国家结盟，明天又可能翻脸。路上有战车，城门口有士兵，种地的人一边扶着犁，一边担心远处又要打仗。

在这样的年代，有人只想着怎么赢；也有人开始问：人能不能少争一点？天下能不能安稳一点？

## 第5页

老人捡起一根树枝，在地上画了许多小圈。

"这些小圈，就是当时的诸侯国。齐、晋、楚、秦，还有后来的韩、赵、魏、燕，都想让别人听自己的。"

"像我们上次争大象叫什么？"小鸟问。

老人摇摇头："比那个严重得多。他们争的不是一个名字，是土地、粮食、城池，还有谁说了算。"

## 第6页

"那老子做什么工作呢？"阿禾问。

"传说他在周王室管理藏书和档案。"老人说，"有点像今天看管图书馆和档案馆的人。只是那时候没有纸书，字写在一片片竹简上，再用绳子穿起来。一本书拿在手里，沉甸甸的。"

有人来查旧典籍，他帮忙找；没人来时，他就坐在安静处读。

## 第7页

老子读历史、诗歌、礼法，也读战争和兴亡的记录。

可他看的不只是竹简。窗外的树怎样长，河水怎样绕过石头，赶路的人怎样低着头走过，马蹄声怎样从远处响到门前，他都看。

老人说："只盯着字，心里容易变窄。真正会读书的人，也会抬头看看活着的世界。"

## 第8页

老人带孩子们走到小溪边。

溪水很浅，青灰色的石头露出水面。水碰到石头，没有停下来，也没有发脾气，只是分成两股，从石头两边绕过去，又在后面合到一起。

"你们看，水从来不喊自己厉害。"老人说，"可它一直往前。高处落下来，它就是瀑布；低处等着它，它就是池塘。"

## 第9页

老人又指了指村口那棵大树。

"老子也会看树。你们看，这么大的树，一开始只是软软的小芽。它没有急着证明自己强大，只是一年一年往下扎根，往上长叶。风来了，它先弯一弯；雨过了，它又继续长。"

阿禾写下两个字：水，树。他觉得这两个字放在一起，比一大串解释还清楚。

## 第10页

老人压低声音："关于老子的出生，还有一个很神奇的传说。"

"先说好，传说不等于历史。传说更像人们给一个人挂上的灯笼，不一定照出真实的模样，却能照出后来的人多么敬重他。"

小鸟马上说："我知道不一定是真的，但我还是想听。"

## 第11页

传说里，老子的母亲怀了他八十一年。

他出生时，头发和胡子已经白了；又因为生在李树下，所以姓李，人们叫他老子。

小兔笑得眼睛弯弯的。老人也笑："这大概不是事实。可是它让我们知道，后人觉得他的智慧很古老，好像一出生就带着很长很长的时间。"

## 第12页

"他见过孔子吗？"小石头问。

老人说："《史记》里有这样的记载：孔子曾经去向老子请教'礼'。"

老子提醒他：旧规矩留下了文字，可制定规矩的人已经远去。学礼，不只是学动作和说法，也要想一想，当初为什么需要这些规矩。

## 第13页

孔子回来后，说自己见到的人像龙。

"鸟会飞，鱼会游，兽会跑，我都知道。可是龙乘着风云上天，我不知道它怎样做到。"

阿禾听得出神。他想：有些深的人和深的道理，只能看见一点影子，没法一下子装进一个名字里。

## 第14页

后来，老子看见周朝越来越乱。

书，他读过很多；水，他看过很多；人世间的争夺，他也看过很多。也许他觉得，再留下去，只会听见更多争吵和马蹄声。

传说有一天，他骑上一头青牛，慢慢往西边走去。

## 第15页

"为什么骑牛，不骑马？"小兔问。

老人笑了："马快，像急着赶路；牛慢，像不再跟谁比赛。"

阿禾点点头："所以老子不是慌慌张张地逃走。他只是决定离开，不再跟乱世一起着急。"

傍晚，他在本子上写：

*老子，一个爱看水的老人。史书留下的不多，传说留下的很多。他像龙，我们看得见几片鳞，看不见整个身子。*

老人说："明天，我们跟着那头青牛，走到关口去。"

# Chapter 000-1: An Old Man Who Loved to Watch Water

## Page 1

That day, Ahe came earlier than usual.

The big tree by the village gate still held the coolness of morning. He set his "book of names" on his knees, pressed one finger on the first page, and thought for a moment before asking the old man:

"Who first left us the line, 'The Dao that can be spoken of is not the constant Dao'?"

## Page 2

The old man did not answer right away.

He looked first toward the hills, then down at a thin trickle of water near the tree roots. The water moved slowly. When it met grass, it slipped around it. When it met a lump of earth, it paused, then found a way past.

"People usually say Laozi," the old man said. "But before we make him into someone far above us, remember him like this: an old man who loved to watch water."

Ahe wrote in his notebook: Laozi.

## Page 3

"Was Laozi his real name?" Little Rabbit asked.

"Not like a name we would write on a form today," said the old man. "Some books say his family name was Li and his given name was Er. Some call him Lao Dan. 'Laozi' is more like a respectful title. It means an old master with great wisdom."

History tells us only a little about him. Later people told many stories. When we read about Laozi, we have to sort them gently, like sorting beans: the records that may be true on one side, the legends that grew later on the other.

## Page 4

Laozi lived about two thousand five hundred years ago, long before the First Emperor united China.

It was late in the Eastern Zhou. The land was divided among many states. One day two states might be allies; the next day they might turn against each other. War chariots moved along roads. Soldiers stood at city gates. Farmers held their plows and worried that fighting might begin again far away.

In a time like that, some people only thought about how to win. Others began to ask: can people struggle less? Can the world become steadier?

## Page 5

The old man picked up a twig and drew many small circles in the dirt.

"These are the states of that time. Qi, Jin, Chu, Qin, and later Han, Zhao, Wei, and Yan all wanted others to listen to them."

"Like when we argued about what to call the elephant?" Little Bird asked.

The old man shook his head. "Much more serious. They were not fighting over a name. They were fighting over land, grain, cities, and who got to decide."

## Page 6

"What work did Laozi do?" Ahe asked.

"Tradition says he kept the books and records for the Zhou royal house," said the old man. "A little like someone who cares for a library and an archive today. But there were no paper books then. Words were written on bamboo slips and tied together with cords. One book could feel heavy in your hands."

When people came looking for old records, he helped them find what they needed. When no one came, he sat quietly and read.

## Page 7

Laozi read histories, poems, rules of ritual, and records of wars and fallen states.

But bamboo slips were not the only things he studied. He watched how trees grew outside the window, how water turned around stones, how travelers passed with lowered heads, and how hoofbeats sounded from far down the road before reaching the door.

"If you stare only at words, your heart can become narrow," said the old man. "A true reader also lifts his head and notices the living world."

## Page 8

The old man led the children to the stream.

The water was shallow, and gray-green stones showed above the surface. When the stream met a stone, it did not stop or lose its temper. It divided into two lines, slipped around both sides, and joined again behind it.

"Water never shouts about being strong," said the old man. "But it keeps going. When it falls from a high place, it becomes a waterfall. When a low place receives it, it becomes a pool."

## Page 9

The old man pointed back at the great tree by the village gate.

"Laozi watched trees too. Look at this one. A tree this large began as a soft sprout. It did not hurry to prove its strength. Year after year, it sent roots downward and leaves upward. When wind came, it bent a little. After rain, it kept growing."

Ahe wrote two words: water, tree. Together, they felt clearer than a long explanation.

## Page 10

The old man lowered his voice. "There is also a strange legend about Laozi's birth."

"Remember, legend is not the same as history. A legend is more like a lantern people hang for someone. It may not show exactly what happened, but it shows how much later people respected him."

Little Bird said at once, "I know it may not be true, but I still want to hear it."

## Page 11

In the legend, Laozi's mother carried him for eighty-one years.

When he was born, his hair and beard were already white. Because he was born under a plum tree, people said his family name was Li and called him Laozi.

Little Rabbit laughed until her eyes curved. The old man laughed too. "This probably is not fact. But it tells us something: later people felt his wisdom was ancient, as if he had been born carrying a very long time inside him."

## Page 12

"Did he meet Confucius?" Little Stone asked.

"The *Records of the Grand Historian* has such a story," said the old man. "It says Confucius once asked Laozi about ritual."

Laozi reminded him that old rules leave words behind, but the people who made those rules are already gone. To learn ritual is not only to copy the motions and phrases. It is also to ask why those rules were needed in the first place.

## Page 13

Afterward, Confucius said the person he had met was like a dragon.

"Birds fly, fish swim, and beasts run. I understand those. But a dragon rides wind and clouds into the sky. I do not know how it does that."

Ahe listened without moving. He thought: some deep people, and some deep truths, can show us a shadow, but they cannot be packed neatly into one name.

## Page 14

Later, Laozi saw the Zhou world grow more and more troubled.

He had read many books, watched much water, and seen plenty of human struggle. Maybe he felt that if he stayed, he would only hear more quarrels and more hoofbeats.

Legend says that one day he mounted a blue-green ox and slowly traveled west.

## Page 15

"Why an ox, not a horse?" Little Rabbit asked.

The old man smiled. "A horse is fast, as if it is rushing somewhere. An ox is slow, as if it has stopped racing anyone."

Ahe nodded. "So Laozi was not running away in a panic. He had simply decided to leave, and not to hurry along with a hurried world."

At dusk, he wrote in his notebook:

*Laozi, an old man who loved to watch water. History left only a little of him. Legend left much more. He was like a dragon: we can glimpse a few scales, but not the whole body.*

"Tomorrow," said the old man, "we will follow that ox to the pass."
