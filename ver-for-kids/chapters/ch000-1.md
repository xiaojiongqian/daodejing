# 第000-1章：一个爱看水的老人

## 第1页

这一天，阿禾早早来到村口的大树下。

他把"名字本"放在膝盖上，手指夹着第一页，却没有立刻翻开。

他问老人："道可道，非常道，这句话最早是谁说的？"

## 第2页

老人没有急着回答。他望了望远处的山，又看了看树根旁慢慢爬过的一小股水。

"人们通常说，是老子。"老人说，"如果把他想成一个人，我愿意先叫他：一个爱看水的老人。"

阿禾在本子上写下：老子。

## 第3页

"老子是他的真名吗？"小兔问。

"不太像我们今天的名字。"老人说，"有的书说他姓李，名耳，也叫老聃。'老子'更像尊称，意思是很有智慧的老先生。"

关于他，史书记得不多，后人讲的故事却很多。读的时候，要把这两样轻轻分开。

## 第4页

老子大约生活在两千五百年前，比秦始皇统一天下还早很久。

那时是东周后期，天下并不安静。路上有兵车，城门口有士兵，田边的人一边种地，一边担心远处又打起来。

越吵的年代，越有人想问：有没有一种办法，能让人少争一点，天下稳一点？

## 第5页

老人捡起一根树枝，在地上画了许多小圈。

"这些小圈，是当时的诸侯国。齐、晋、楚、秦，还有后来的韩、赵、魏、燕，都想让别人听自己的。"

小鸟说："像我们上次争大象叫什么？"

老人摇摇头："更严重。他们争的不是一个名字，是土地、粮食、城池和天下。"

## 第6页

"那老子做什么工作？"阿禾问。

"传说他在周朝管理藏书。"老人说，"那时没有纸书，书写在一片片竹简上，再用绳子穿起来。一本书拿在手里，沉甸甸的。"

有人来查典籍，他帮忙寻找；没有人来时，他就在安静处读。

## 第7页

老子读历史、诗歌、礼法，也读战争和兴亡的记录。

可是他看的不只是竹简。窗外的树怎样长，河水怎样绕过石头，赶路的人怎样低头走过，马蹄声怎样从远到近，他都看。

老人说："只盯着字，容易把道理看窄。懂道的人，还要看见活着的世界。"

## 第8页

老人带孩子们走到小溪边。

溪水很浅，石头露出青灰色的背。水碰到石头，没有停下来，也没有发脾气，只是分成两股，从两边绕过去。

"你们看，水不喊自己厉害。"老人说，"可它一直往前。高处落下来，它就成瀑布；低处等着它，它就成池塘。"

## 第9页

"老子也看树。"老人又指着大树。

"这么大的树，一开始只是软软的小芽。它不急着证明自己强大，只是一年一年扎根、长叶、伸枝。"

阿禾写下两个字：水，树。他觉得这两个字放在一起，比一大串解释还明白。

## 第10页

老人压低声音："关于老子的出生，还有一个很神奇的传说。"

"先说好，传说不等于历史。它更像一件发亮的外衣，披在真实的人身上，让人记住他。"

小鸟马上说："我知道不一定是真的，但我还是想听。"

## 第11页

传说里，老子的母亲怀了他八十一年。

他出生时，头发和胡子已经白了，又生在李树下，所以姓李，人们叫他老子。

小兔笑弯了眼睛。老人也笑："这大概不是事实。可是它告诉我们，后人觉得他的智慧很古老，好像一出生就带着很长很长的时间。"

## 第12页

"他见过孔子吗？"小石头问。

老人说："《史记》里记着，孔子曾去向老子请教礼。"

老子提醒他：旧规矩留下了文字，可制定规矩的人已经远去。读规矩时，不能只抓住外壳，也要想一想它为什么出现。

## 第13页

孔子回来后，说自己见到的人像龙。

"鸟会飞，鱼会游，兽会跑，我都知道。可是龙乘着风云上天，我不知道它怎样做到。"

阿禾听得出神。他想：真正深的东西，能看见一点影子，却不能一下子装进一个名字。

## 第14页

后来，老子看见周朝越来越乱。

书他读过很多，水他看过很多，人世间的争夺他也看过很多。

传说有一天，他骑上一头青牛，慢慢往西边走去。

## 第15页

"为什么骑牛，不骑马？"小兔问。

老人笑了："马快，像急着赶路；牛慢，像不再跟谁比赛。"

阿禾点点头："所以老子不是慌慌张张地逃走。他只是决定离开。"

傍晚，他在本子上写：

*老子，一个爱看水的老人。史书留下的不多，传说留下的很多。他像龙，我们看得见鳞片，看不见全身。*

老人说："明天，我们讲那些字怎样留下来，又怎样走到今天。"

# Chapter 000-1: An Old Man Who Loved to Watch Water

## Page 1

That day, Ahe came early to the big tree by the village gate.

He set his "book of names" on his knees. His finger held the first page, but he did not open it yet.

"Who first said, 'The Dao that can be spoken of is not the lasting Dao'?" he asked the old man.

## Page 2

The old man did not answer at once. He looked toward the hills, then down at a thin trickle of water near the roots of the tree.

"People usually say Laozi," he said. "If we picture him as a person, I like to begin this way: he was an old man who loved to watch water."

Ahe wrote in his notebook: Laozi.

## Page 3

"Was Laozi his real name?" Little Rabbit asked.

"Not like a name we use today," said the old man. "Some books say his family name was Li and his given name was Er. Some call him Lao Dan. 'Laozi' is more like a title of respect. It means a wise old master."

History remembers only a little about him. Later people told many stories. When we read, we should gently keep those two apart.

## Page 4

Laozi lived about two thousand five hundred years ago, long before the First Emperor united China.

It was late in the Eastern Zhou, and the world was not peaceful. War carts moved along the roads. Soldiers stood at city gates. Farmers worked their fields while worrying that fighting might begin again somewhere far away.

In a noisy age, some people began to ask: is there a way for people to struggle less, and for the world to become steadier?

## Page 5

The old man picked up a twig and drew many small circles in the dirt.

"These are the states of that time. Qi, Jin, Chu, Qin, and later Han, Zhao, Wei, and Yan all wanted others to obey them."

"Like when we argued about what to call the elephant?" Little Bird asked.

The old man shook his head. "More serious than that. They were not fighting over a name. They were fighting over land, grain, cities, and all under heaven."

## Page 6

"What work did Laozi do?" Ahe asked.

"Tradition says he cared for the royal archives of Zhou," said the old man. "There were no paper books then. Words were written on strips of bamboo, then tied together with cords. A single book could feel heavy in your hands."

When people came to look up old records, he helped them find what they needed. When no one came, he read quietly.

## Page 7

Laozi read histories, poems, rites, and records of wars and fallen states.

But bamboo slips were not the only things he studied. He watched trees growing outside the window, water turning around stones, travelers passing with lowered heads, and hoofbeats moving from far away to near.

"If you stare only at words, understanding can become too small," said the old man. "A person who seeks Dao must also notice the living world."

## Page 8

The old man led the children to the stream.

The water was shallow. Gray-green stones showed their backs. When the stream met a stone, it did not stop or lose its temper. It simply divided into two thin lines and slipped around both sides.

"Water never shouts about being strong," said the old man. "But it keeps going. When it falls from a high place, it becomes a waterfall. When a low place receives it, it becomes a pool."

## Page 9

"Laozi watched trees too," the old man said, pointing back at the great tree.

"A tree this large begins as a soft sprout. It does not hurry to prove its strength. Year after year, it roots itself deeper, opens leaves, and reaches out new branches."

Ahe wrote two words: water, tree. Together, they felt clearer than a long explanation.

## Page 10

The old man lowered his voice. "There is also a strange legend about Laozi's birth."

"Remember, a legend is not the same as history. It is more like a shining coat placed on a real person so people will remember him."

Little Bird said at once, "I know it may not be true, but I still want to hear it."

## Page 11

In the legend, Laozi's mother carried him for eighty-one years.

When he was born, his hair and beard were already white. He was born under a plum tree, so people said his family name was Li and called him Laozi.

Little Rabbit laughed until her eyes curved. The old man laughed too. "This probably is not fact. But it tells us something: later people felt his wisdom was ancient, as if he had been born carrying a very long time inside him."

## Page 12

"Did he meet Confucius?" Little Stone asked.

"The *Records of the Grand Historian* says Confucius once asked Laozi about ritual," said the old man.

Laozi reminded him that old rules leave words behind, but the people who made them are already gone. When we read a rule, we should not hold only the shell. We should ask why it came into being.

## Page 13

Afterward, Confucius said the person he had met was like a dragon.

"Birds fly, fish swim, and beasts run. I understand these. But a dragon rides wind and clouds into the sky. I do not know how it does that."

Ahe listened without moving. He thought: the deepest things can show us a shadow, but they cannot be packed neatly into one name.

## Page 14

Later, Laozi saw the Zhou world grow more and more troubled.

He had read many books, watched much water, and seen plenty of human fighting.

Legend says that one day he mounted a blue-green ox and slowly traveled west.

## Page 15

"Why an ox, not a horse?" Little Rabbit asked.

The old man smiled. "A horse is fast, as if it is rushing somewhere. An ox is slow, as if it has stopped racing anyone."

Ahe nodded. "So Laozi was not running away in panic. He had simply decided to leave."

At dusk, he wrote in his notebook:

*Laozi, an old man who loved to watch water. History left only a little of him. Legend left much more. He was like a dragon: we can glimpse the scales, but not the whole body.*

"Tomorrow," said the old man, "we will talk about how those words were left behind, and how they reached us."
