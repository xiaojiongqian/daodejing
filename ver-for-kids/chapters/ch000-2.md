# 第000-2章：青牛走到关口

## 第1页

第二天一早，阿禾又坐到大树下。

昨晚那头青牛还在他脑子里慢慢走。他翻开本子问："老子骑着青牛离开以后，那些话是怎么留下来的？"

老人说："要回答这个问题，我们得先走到一个关口。"

## 第2页

老人先把话说清楚："今天前半段，还是传说。后半段，我们再讲书怎样一代一代传下来。"

"传说不能直接当历史，可它常常藏着人们的愿望、敬意和想象。听传说的时候，耳朵可以靠近一点，脚也要站稳一点。"

孩子们点点头，围得更近了。

## 第3页

传说里，老子骑着青牛，来到函谷关。

函谷关在周朝西边，是很重要的关口。过了这里，就像走出熟悉的家门，前面的路更远，也更难知道。

守关的人叫尹喜。后来，人们也尊称他为关尹子。

## 第4页

尹喜不是只会开门、关门的人。

传说他懂得"望气"，会看云气的颜色和形状，猜测将要发生的事。我们今天听起来会觉得神奇，可在古人的故事里，这是一种特别的本领。

有一天，他看见东方有紫色的云气慢慢飘来，心里一动：也许有一位了不起的人要到了。

## 第5页

"紫气东来！"阿禾脱口而出，"这个词我听过。"

老人点点头："后来它成了成语，常用来祝福好事快来了，贵客快到了。"

小鸟抬头看了半天。天上只有白云。她小声说："我怎么一点紫色也没看见？"

## 第6页

老人笑了笑："传说里的云，常常不只在天上，也在人心里。一个人真心等待好事，普通的云也会变得有意思。"

果然，过了一会儿，一位白发老人骑着青牛来到关前。

尹喜没有为难他。他恭恭敬敬地请求："先生要出关远行，能不能把最重要的话留下来？"

## 第7页

老子停住了。

老人说："老子不像一个爱长篇大论的人。"

"他知道，真正重要的道理，不是靠嘴巴说一大堆就能说完的。话语像手指，能帮我们指向月亮；可是手指不是月亮。"

## 第8页

"可是尹喜问得诚心，老子就写了。"

"写了多少？"小石头问。

老人伸出五根手指："大约五千个字。后人把这些话称为《老子》，也叫《道德经》，又分成八十一章，方便一章一章读。"

## 第9页

写完以后，老子重新骑上青牛，出了关。

"后来他去哪儿了？"小兔问。

老人摇摇头："没有人能确定。有人说他继续往西走，有人说他就像云一样，慢慢散进传说里。"

"能确定的是，那五千个字留下来了。一个人走远了，一本书的路却刚刚开始。"

## 第10页

阿禾想象黄昏里的关门。

青牛的蹄声越来越轻，最后混进风里。尹喜抱着竹简站在关口，怀里那一卷并不厚，却像装着很长的路。

风吹过木门，也吹过竹简上的字。

## 第11页

那时没有印刷机，也没有现在这样的书店。

竹简会断，穿竹简的绳子会松，潮气会让字迹变淡，虫子也可能咬掉几片。一本书想留得久，并不容易。

如果想多留一份，就要有人坐下来，一字一字抄。

## 第12页

"抄书会不会抄错？"阿禾问。

"会。"老人说，"眼睛一花，可能多看一行；手一快，可能少写一句。有的人还会根据自己的理解，换掉一两个字。"

"一本书走得越远，留下的样子就越多。我们今天读古书，常常要把这些样子放在一起比较。"

## 第13页

有时候，连皇帝的名字也会让字改变。

古人讲避讳。如果皇帝名字里有某个字，很多人写书时就尽量不用它，换成意思相近的字。

比如早期有些本子写"恒"，后来为了避汉文帝刘恒的名讳，常写成"常"。所以我们今天读到的《道德经》，像一条流过许多年代的河，河水还是那条河，水色却会有细小的变化。

## 第14页

"那我们还能找到更早的样子吗？"小石头问。

老人说："很难找到最早的那一滴水，但考古让我们看见了很早的河段。"

"一九七三年，长沙马王堆汉墓出土了写在丝帛上的《老子》。丝帛比竹简轻，像布一样。那些字在地下睡了两千多年，重新被人看见。"

## 第15页

"后来，湖北郭店又出土了更早的竹简本。不过它只留下部分章节，顺序和文字也和今天常见的本子不完全一样。"

阿禾写下：

*同一条河，流过泥土、石头和草地，颜色会有一点不同。但只要静下来，我们仍然能听见水声。*

老人看了看天色："明天，我们再讲讲，后来的人怎样听懂这些水声。"

# Chapter 000-2: The Blue-Green Ox Reaches the Pass

## Page 1

Early the next morning, Ahe sat beneath the big tree again.

The blue-green ox from last night's story was still walking slowly through his mind. He opened his notebook and asked, "After Laozi rode away on the ox, how did his words stay in the world?"

"To answer that," said the old man, "we first have to walk to a pass."

## Page 2

The old man began by making things clear. "The first half of today's story is still legend. After that, we will talk about how the book was passed down from age to age."

"A legend is not the same as history. But it often carries people's hopes, respect, and imagination inside it. When we listen to legend, our ears can lean closer, but our feet should still stand steady."

The children nodded and leaned in.

## Page 3

In the legend, Laozi rode his blue-green ox to Hangu Pass.

Hangu Pass stood on the western side of the Zhou realm. Passing through it was like stepping out of a familiar home. The road ahead was farther, and harder to know.

The keeper of the pass was named Yin Xi. Later people respectfully called him Master Yin of the Pass.

## Page 4

Yin Xi was not only someone who opened and closed a gate.

Legend says he could "watch the vapors." He studied the colors and shapes of clouds and guessed what might be coming. It sounds magical to us now, but in old stories it was a special skill.

One day, he saw purple clouds drifting slowly from the east. His heart stirred: perhaps a remarkable person was on the way.

## Page 5

"Zi qi dong lai!" Ahe blurted out. "Purple clouds from the east. I have heard that phrase."

The old man nodded. "Later it became an idiom. People use it to wish that good fortune is near, or that an honored guest is arriving."

Little Bird stared up for a long while. There were only white clouds in the sky. "I cannot see any purple," she whispered.

## Page 6

The old man smiled. "In legends, clouds are often not only in the sky. They are also in people's hearts. When someone truly waits for good news, even ordinary clouds can begin to matter."

Sure enough, after a while, an old man with white hair arrived before the pass, riding a blue-green ox.

Yin Xi did not trouble him. He bowed and asked, "Sir, you are going beyond the pass. Could you leave your most important words behind?"

## Page 7

Laozi stopped.

"Laozi does not seem like a man who loved long speeches," said the old man.

"He knew that the most important truths cannot be finished just by speaking more and more. Words are like a finger pointing at the moon. They help us look, but they are not the moon itself."

## Page 8

"But Yin Xi asked sincerely, so Laozi wrote."

"How much did he write?" Little Stone asked.

The old man held up five fingers. "About five thousand characters. Later people called those words the *Laozi*, or the *Dao De Jing*, and divided them into eighty-one chapters so readers could take them one chapter at a time."

## Page 9

When he had finished, Laozi mounted the ox again and went through the pass.

"Where did he go after that?" Little Rabbit asked.

The old man shook his head. "No one knows for certain. Some say he kept traveling west. Some say he slowly faded into legend like a cloud."

"What we do know is this: the five thousand characters remained. One person had gone far away, but the journey of a book had just begun."

## Page 10

Ahe imagined the gate at dusk.

The ox's hoofbeats grew lighter and lighter until they mixed with the wind. Yin Xi stood at the pass holding the bamboo slips. The bundle was not very thick, but it seemed to hold a very long road.

Wind moved across the wooden gate and across the words on the bamboo.

## Page 11

There were no printing presses then, and no bookstores like the ones we know.

Bamboo slips could crack. The cords that tied them could loosen. Damp air could fade the writing. Worms might bite away a few strips. It was not easy for a book to last.

If people wanted another copy, someone had to sit down and copy it character by character.

## Page 12

"Could copying make mistakes?" Ahe asked.

"Yes," said the old man. "A tired eye might jump to the wrong line. A quick hand might skip a sentence. Some readers might even change a word or two because of how they understood it."

"The farther a book travels, the more shapes it can leave behind. When we read ancient books today, we often compare those shapes."

## Page 13

Sometimes even an emperor's name could change a word.

In ancient China, people practiced name taboo. If a character appeared in an emperor's name, writers often avoided it and used another character with a similar meaning.

For example, some early copies used *heng*. Later, to avoid the personal name of Emperor Wen of Han, Liu Heng, many copies wrote *chang* instead. So the *Dao De Jing* we read today is like a river that has flowed through many ages. It is still the same river, but its color can shift a little.

## Page 14

"Can we still find an earlier shape of it?" Little Stone asked.

"It is hard to find the very first drop of water," said the old man. "But archaeology lets us see very early stretches of the river."

"In nineteen seventy-three, the Mawangdui Han tombs in Changsha yielded silk manuscripts of the *Laozi*. Silk is lighter than bamboo and more like cloth. Those words had slept underground for more than two thousand years before people saw them again."

## Page 15

"Later, even older bamboo-slip texts were found at Guodian in Hubei. They preserve only parts of the book, and their order and wording are not exactly the same as the common versions we read today."

Ahe wrote:

*The same river may pass through mud, stone, and grass, so its color changes a little. But if we grow quiet, we can still hear the water.*

The old man looked at the sky. "Tomorrow, we will talk about how later readers learned to hear that water."
