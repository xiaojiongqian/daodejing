# 第015-2章：雪把路藏起来

## 典故引入

### 第1页

关于拿破仑，西方流传着一句很有名的话，大意是：他的字典里没有“不可能”。

这句话听起来像一面亮亮的旗。可第十五章问的，偏偏是另一件事：冬天过河的人，为什么不敢把话说得太满？

### 第2页

拿破仑年轻时很会打仗。

一次次胜利，让许多人相信他，也让他越来越相信自己。地图摊开时，山河像线，城池像点，远方好像只要一声命令就会靠近。

### 第3页

最危险的，不一定是失败。

有时，连胜也危险。失败会让人低头看脚下，连胜却容易让人只看前方。

### 第4页

1812年，他带着大军进入俄国。

地图上的路很细，也很干净。可真正的路不在纸上。真正的路有泥，有空村庄，有越拉越长的队伍，还有慢慢压低的天色。

### 第5页

一开始，旗帜还多，马蹄还响。

士兵们相信他们会像从前那样赢。可是走得越深，粮食越难找，马越疲惫，夜里的寒气越来越像一只看不见的手。

### 第6页

俄国的冬天没有大声说话。

它只是一天比一天冷。河面结住，路冻硬，雪落下来，把车辙盖住，也把人的判断一点一点盖住。

### 第7页

如果心里还留着“冬涉川”的敬畏，人会停下来问：

粮够不够？马还能走多久？退路在哪里？现在往前，是为了胜利，还是为了不肯承认自己该停？

### 第8页

可是太满的自信不喜欢这些问题。

它觉得问题扫兴，提醒碍耳，停一步就像输了。它只听见“向前”，听不见雪落在帐篷上的声音。

### 第9页

后来，队伍往回走。

来时的路已经不是来时的路。白雪盖住地面，寒冷钻进衣缝，许多人再也看不见故乡。

### 第10页

老人讲到这里，停了一会儿。

屋外也在下小雪。阿禾听见雪落在竹叶上，轻轻一声，又轻轻一声。

### 第11页

小石头问：“那是不是不能说‘我一定行’？”

老人说：“可以有志气。没有志气，船不开，桥不搭，路也没人走。”

### 第12页

“可是志气要带着耳朵。”

“听得见风，听得见水，听得见别人说‘慢一点’，也听得见自己心里那句‘我是不是太满了’。”

### 第13页

阿禾想起自己做小木桥。

他觉得两块木板就够，小石头提醒要垫石头，他嫌麻烦。第一场雨来，一头木板陷进泥里，桥歪了，大家只好绕路。

### 第14页

那不是打仗，也不是雪原。

可心里的毛病一样：一旦觉得“我想到了，就一定行”，耳朵就关上一半；一旦嫌提醒麻烦，路就开始在脚下变软。

### 第15页

老人说：“真正有本事的人，不急着显得什么都不怕。”

“他怕错一步会害人，怕看漏一点会误事。这个怕，不把他吓住，只把他的脚步放稳。”

### 第16页

阿禾看着窗外的雪。

雪落得很轻，却能把整条路盖住。他忽然懂了：有些危险不是因为吵，才危险；有些危险正因为安静，才更要小心听。

## 回扣

### 第17页

第十五章说“豫焉若冬涉川”。

这不是胆小，是知道天地比自己的口号大。冬天的河不会因为你勇敢，就变暖；雪路也不会因为你自信，就自己让开。

### 第18页

“犹兮若畏四邻”，不是疑神疑鬼。

它是说，一个人做大事时，四周的天气、地势、人心、粮草、退路，都要看见。看见这些，才是真勇敢。

### 第19页

“保此道者不欲盈”，在这个故事里最重。

心太满，就装不下提醒；话太满，就听不见雪声。能留一点不满，才有地方让真实进来。

### 第20页

阿禾写下：勇敢不是把“不可能”从字典里撕掉。

勇敢是带着敬畏往前走。能进，也能停；能看地图，也能低头看雪。

# Chapter 015-2: Snow Hides the Road

## Allusion

### Page 1

A famous saying is often connected with Napoleon: in his dictionary, there was no "impossible."

The saying sounds like a bright flag. But Chapter Fifteen asks another question: why does someone crossing a winter river not speak too fully?

### Page 2

Napoleon was very gifted in war when young.

Victory after victory made many people believe in him, and made him believe more and more in himself. When a map opened, mountains and rivers seemed like lines, cities like dots, and distance as if it could be brought near by one command.

### Page 3

The most dangerous thing is not always failure.

Sometimes repeated victory is dangerous too. Failure makes people lower their heads and look at their feet. Repeated victory can make them look only ahead.

### Page 4

In 1812, he led a great army into Russia.

On paper, the road was thin and clean. But the real road was not on paper. The real road had mud, empty villages, a line of soldiers stretching longer and longer, and a sky slowly pressing lower.

### Page 5

At first, there were many flags, and hoofbeats were strong.

The soldiers believed they would win as before. But the deeper they went, the harder food was to find, the more tired the horses became, and the night's cold felt more and more like an invisible hand.

### Page 6

The Russian winter did not speak loudly.

It simply grew colder day by day. Rivers froze, roads hardened, and snow fell, covering wheel tracks and slowly covering human judgment too.

### Page 7

If the heart still keeps the caution of crossing a river in winter, it stops and asks:

Is there enough food? How long can the horses walk? Where is the way back? Am I moving forward for victory, or because I refuse to admit I should stop?

### Page 8

Overfull confidence dislikes such questions.

It thinks questions spoil the mood, reminders grate on the ear, and one step of stopping feels like defeat. It hears only "forward" and not snow falling on the tents.

### Page 9

Later, the army turned back.

The road they had taken was no longer the same road. White snow covered the ground, cold entered through seams, and many people never saw home again.

### Page 10

The old man stopped for a while here.

Outside, light snow was falling too. Ahe heard it touch bamboo leaves, softly once, then softly again.

### Page 11

Little Stone asked, "Does that mean we should not say, 'I can do it'?"

The old man said, "Ambition is allowed. Without ambition, boats do not launch, bridges are not built, and roads go untraveled."

### Page 12

"But ambition needs ears."

"It must hear wind, water, another person saying 'slow down,' and the sentence inside: 'Am I too full?'"

### Page 13

Ahe remembered a small wooden bridge he had made.

He thought two planks were enough. Little Stone reminded him to set stones underneath, but he found it troublesome. The first rain came, one end sank into mud, the bridge tilted, and everyone had to go around.

### Page 14

That was not war, and not a snowfield.

But the heart's sickness was similar. Once we think, "I thought of it, so it must work," the ears half close. Once reminders feel troublesome, the road begins to soften underfoot.

### Page 15

The old man said, "A truly capable person does not hurry to look fearless."

"He fears that one wrong step may harm others, and one missed detail may spoil the matter. That fear does not freeze him; it steadies his feet."

### Page 16

Ahe watched the snow outside.

Snow fell lightly, yet it could hide a whole road. He suddenly understood: some dangers are dangerous not because they are noisy, but because they are quiet. That is why one must listen carefully.

## Return

### Page 17

Chapter Fifteen says, "like those who wade through a stream in winter."

This is not cowardice. It knows that heaven and earth are larger than one's slogans. A winter river does not become warm because you are brave, and a snow road does not move aside because you are confident.

### Page 18

"Like those afraid of all around them" does not mean suspecting danger everywhere.

It means that when a person does great work, weather, land, people's hearts, supplies, and the road back must all be seen. Seeing these is part of true courage.

### Page 19

"Those who preserve this method do not wish to be full" is the heaviest line in this story.

When the heart is too full, there is no room for reminders. When words are too full, one cannot hear snow. Leaving a little unfilled space gives reality a place to enter.

### Page 20

Ahe wrote: courage is not tearing "impossible" out of the dictionary.

Courage is walking forward with respect. It can advance, and it can stop. It can read the map, and it can lower its head to look at the snow.
