# 第013-2章：两只手臂和一张契书

## 典故引入

### 第1页

《庄子》里有一个故事，讲子华子去见韩国的国君。

那时，韩国和魏国为了争一块土地，弄得人心不安。国君脸上带着忧色，像把那块地装进了自己的胸口。

### 第2页

子华子没有先说“别争了”，也没有责备国君贪心。

他先拿出一个比方，好像把一把秤摆在国君面前。

### 第3页

他说：“假如有一张契书写着：你用左手去拿它，右手就废掉；你用右手去拿它，左手就废掉。”

“可是只要拿到它，你就能得到整个天下。你拿不拿？”

### 第4页

这个问题很大。

天下，不是一座房子，也不是一车粮食。天下像所有城门、河流、田地和道路加在一起。听起来，谁不心动？

### 第5页

可是国君立刻说：“不拿。”

没有手臂，怎么穿衣、吃饭、写字、扶人、抱孩子？天下再大，也不能替一只废掉的手拿起碗。

### 第6页

子华子这才往下说：“两只手臂，比天下还贵重。”

“人的身体，又比两只手臂更贵重。”

### 第7页

国君听着，脸上的忧色慢慢变了。

子华子继续说：“天下已经比你的身体轻，韩国又比天下轻。现在你所争的那块地，比韩国还轻。”

### 第8页

“为了一块更轻的地，愁坏更贵重的身，值得吗？”

这句话像把乱成一团的绳子慢慢理开。原来国君一直把小的东西放大，把大的东西忘了。

### 第9页

他以为自己在为国家忧虑。

可忧虑若把身体烧坏，把判断搅乱，最后也未必真的帮到国家。一个被小得失牵走的人，很难照看大的事情。

### 第10页

国君叹了一口气。

他不是马上变成神仙，也不是从此什么都不管。只是那张秤重新摆正了：身体、生命、国家、土地，各有轻重。

### 第11页

阿禾听到这里，先摸了摸自己的两只手。

它们很普通。一只手有一点墨迹，另一只手指甲里还有泥。可是没有它们，他就不能翻书、种豆、扶小石头骑车。

### 第12页

小石头问：“那是不是为了身体，什么都不要做？”

老人说：“不是。子华子不是叫国君躲起来。他是先帮国君看清轻重。”

### 第13页

“若为了面子，把身体气坏；为了争一点东西，把睡眠、饭食、判断全丢了，就叫本末倒置。”

“本末倒置的人，手里抓的也许是地，脚下失的却是路。”

### 第14页

阿禾想起自己为红绸带吃不下饭。

那不是争天下，只是一场鼓舞。可他的心已经像小小国君，守着一块小地，愁得忘了身体。

### 第15页

老人让孩子们做一个选择。

“给你一箱金珠，但换走你今天所有的睡眠，你换不换？给你一面最大的小旗，但让你再也不能跑，你换不换？”

### 第16页

孩子们一开始笑，后来都摇头。

有些东西听起来很亮，一放到身体旁边，分量就轻了。

## 回扣

### 第17页

第十三章说“贵大患若身”。

人有身体，所以会有忧患；但也正因为有身体，才知道生命不能随便拿去换身外之物。

### 第18页

“贵以身为天下，若可寄天下”，不是自私。

真正珍惜身体和生命的人，才不会轻易让战争、面子、贪心把许多人拖进去。

### 第19页

子华子的故事把“轻重”讲得很清楚。

天下很大，两臂更贴身；国家很重，生命更根本；一块争来的地若伤了判断，就未必值得。

### 第20页

阿禾写下：重要的东西，不一定最闪亮。

手臂能抱人，身体能活着，心能清楚地判断。先不把这些弄丢，才有资格照看更大的事。

# Chapter 013-2: Two Arms and a Written Deed

## Allusion

### Page 1

In the Zhuangzi, there is a story of Zihuazi visiting the ruler of Han.

At that time, Han and Wei were uneasy over a piece of land. The ruler's face was troubled, as if he had placed that land inside his chest.

### Page 2

Zihuazi did not begin by saying, "Stop fighting," and he did not scold the ruler for greed.

He first offered an example, as if setting a scale before him.

### Page 3

He said, "Suppose a written deed says this: if you take it with your left hand, your right arm will be ruined; if you take it with your right hand, your left arm will be ruined."

"But if you take it, you will gain the whole world. Would you take it?"

### Page 4

This was a large question.

The world is not one house, or one cart of grain. It is as if all gates, rivers, fields, and roads were added together. Who would not be moved by it?

### Page 5

But the ruler immediately said, "I would not take it."

Without arms, how could one dress, eat, write, help someone, or hold a child? The whole world cannot lift a bowl for a ruined hand.

### Page 6

Only then did Zihuazi continue. "Two arms are more precious than the whole world."

"And a person's body is more precious than the two arms."

### Page 7

The ruler listened, and the worry on his face slowly changed.

Zihuazi went on. "If the world is lighter than your body, then Han is lighter than the world. And the land you are fighting over is lighter still."

### Page 8

"For a lighter piece of land, is it worth damaging the more precious body?"

The words slowly loosened a tangled rope. The ruler had been making the small thing large and forgetting the larger thing.

### Page 9

He thought he was worrying for the state.

But if worry burns the body and muddles judgment, it may not truly help the state. A person dragged by small gain and loss cannot easily care for great matters.

### Page 10

The ruler sighed.

He did not become a sage at once, and he did not stop caring about everything. But the scale was set upright again: body, life, state, and land each had its weight.

### Page 11

When Ahe heard this, he touched his two hands.

They were ordinary. One had a little ink on it; the other had mud under the nails. Yet without them, he could not turn pages, plant beans, or help Little Stone ride a bicycle.

### Page 12

Little Stone asked, "Then should we do nothing except protect the body?"

The old man said, "No. Zihuazi did not tell the ruler to hide. He first helped him see weight and measure clearly."

### Page 13

"If you ruin your body for face, or lose sleep, food, and judgment over a small thing, that is putting root and branch upside down."

"Such a person may be holding land in his hand, but losing the road under his feet."

### Page 14

Ahe remembered losing his appetite over the red drum band.

That was not a struggle for the world; it was only a drum dance. Yet his heart had become like a small ruler, guarding a small field and forgetting the body.

### Page 15

The old man asked the children to choose.

"If I give you a box of gold beads but take away all your sleep tonight, will you trade? If I give you the biggest flag but you can never run again, will you trade?"

### Page 16

At first the children laughed. Then they all shook their heads.

Some things sound bright, but beside the body, their weight becomes light.

## Return

### Page 17

Chapter Thirteen speaks of great calamity as the body.

Because we have bodies, we have troubles. But because we have bodies, we also know that life should not be exchanged lightly for things outside us.

### Page 18

"Honoring the body as the world" is not selfishness.

One who truly cherishes body and life will not easily let war, face, or greed drag many people into harm.

### Page 19

Zihuazi's story teaches weight and measure clearly.

The world is large, but two arms are closer. A state is weighty, but life is more basic. If a piece of land damages judgment, it may not be worth the cost.

### Page 20

Ahe wrote: the important thing is not always the shiniest.

Arms can hold people, the body can live, and the heart can judge clearly. If these are not lost, one may then care for larger things.
