# 第012-2章：四座城门之外

## 典故引入

### 第1页

古印度有一个关于悉达多太子的故事。

这是佛教传统里非常重要的故事。给孩子讲时，不必急着讲所有教义，只先看见一个问题：一个人如果只被美好的东西围住，会不会反而看不见真实？

### 第2页

悉达多出生在王宫里。

他的父亲希望他一生富贵快乐，便把宫殿修得极美：花园常开，乐声常在，衣服柔软，食物甘美。

### 第3页

父亲还吩咐：不要让太子看见衰老、病痛和死亡。

宫里的人把皱纹藏起来，把哭声压低，把病人和送葬的人都挡在远处。太子看见的，总是年轻的脸、明亮的灯和不会停的歌舞。

### 第4页

这座宫殿像一个巨大的彩色盒子。

盒子里有五色，有五音，有五味。可是盒子太满了，连一条通向真实世界的缝都快没有了。

### 第5页

有一天，悉达多坚持要出城。

马车走到东门外，他看见一位老人。老人背弯了，头发白了，脚步很慢，像每一步都要从地上借力。

### 第6页

太子问车夫：“人为什么会这样？”

车夫沉默了一会儿，说：“每个人都会老。太子也会。”

### 第7页

宫里的音乐好像忽然远了。

悉达多第一次知道，青春不是永远贴在脸上的金箔。它会一点一点退下去。

### 第8页

后来，他从南门出去，看见病人。

那人躺在路旁，脸色苍白，身体疼得发抖。太子又问：“这也是别人家的事吗？”

### 第9页

车夫说：“不是。人有身体，就可能生病。强壮的人也会有脆弱的时候。”

这句话像一阵冷风，吹进太子从没开过的窗。

### 第10页

再后来，他从西门出去，看见一队送葬的人。

人们低着头，脚步沉沉。太子明白了：生命不仅会老，会病，还会走到尽头。

### 第11页

宫殿里的甜味没有变，花也还在开。

可是悉达多的舌头忽然尝出了另一种味道：原来被藏起来的事情，并不会因为看不见就不存在。

### 第12页

最后，他从北门出去，看见一位修行人。

那人衣着朴素，手里没有珠宝，脸上却有一种安静。太子从他身上看见另一条路：不逃避真实，也不被真实吓倒。

### 第13页

这四次出城，像四扇门一扇一扇打开。

老人、病人、送葬队伍、修行人，不是为了吓他，而是把生命原本的样子摆在他面前。

### 第14页

悉达多回到宫里。

乐师还在弹琴，舞者还在旋转，盘子里还有甜果。可是他知道，如果一生只躲在这些美好里，眼睛反而会被美好遮住。

### 第15页

后来，他离开王宫，去寻找关于生老病死的答案。

这不是因为他讨厌花园，也不是因为音乐和饭食有罪。是因为宫殿再华丽，也回答不了他心里那个真正的问题。

### 第16页

孩子们听到这里，有点安静。

小石头问：“那是不是快乐都是假的？”

### 第17页

老人摇头：“不是。花是真的，音乐也是真的。”

“可是如果有人只让你看花，不让你看根；只让你听歌，不让你听哭声；那你看到的就不完整。”

### 第18页

阿禾想起集市上那颗蓝珠子。

它并不是坏东西。坏的是他让那一点亮光挡住了豆种、朋友的呼唤和自己答应过的事。

## 回扣

### 第19页

第十二章说“五色令人目盲”，悉达多的宫殿就是一座五色很多的地方。

美丽本身没有错。可若美丽被用来挡住真实，它就会变成一面很亮的墙。

### 第20页

“五音令人耳聋”，不只是声音太吵。

有时，是因为只听见赞美和歌舞，就听不见世界上真实的痛苦，也听不见自己心里真正的问题。

### 第21页

“圣人为腹不为目”，是回到生命的根本。

眼睛喜欢好看的，耳朵喜欢好听的，舌头喜欢好吃的，这些都可以有。但人不能只靠这些活着。

### 第22页

阿禾写下：真正的清醒，不是把美好的东西全推开。

真正的清醒，是看见美好时也不忘真实；听见热闹时也听得见安静处的呼唤。

# Chapter 012-2: Beyond the Four Gates

## Allusion

### Page 1

Ancient India has a story about Prince Siddhartha.

It is an important story in Buddhist tradition. For children, we need not rush into every teaching. We can first see one question: if a person is surrounded only by pleasant things, might he fail to see what is real?

### Page 2

Siddhartha was born in a palace.

His father wanted him to live in wealth and happiness, so he made the palace beautiful: gardens kept blooming, music kept playing, clothes were soft, and food was sweet.

### Page 3

The father also ordered that the prince should not see aging, sickness, or death.

People in the palace hid wrinkles, lowered crying voices, and kept the sick and funeral processions far away. The prince always saw young faces, bright lamps, and dances that did not seem to end.

### Page 4

The palace was like a great colored box.

Inside were many colors, sounds, and flavors. But the box was so full that almost no crack remained through which the real world could enter.

### Page 5

One day, Siddhartha insisted on leaving the city.

At the eastern gate, he saw an old man. The man's back was bent, his hair was white, and his steps were slow, as if each step had to borrow strength from the ground.

### Page 6

The prince asked the charioteer, "Why does a person become like this?"

The charioteer was silent for a moment. "Everyone grows old. Even the prince will."

### Page 7

The music of the palace suddenly seemed far away.

For the first time, Siddhartha learned that youth is not gold leaf fixed forever to the face. It slowly falls away.

### Page 8

Later, he went out through the southern gate and saw a sick man.

The man lay beside the road, pale and trembling with pain. The prince asked again, "Is this only something that happens to other families?"

### Page 9

The charioteer said, "No. If a person has a body, sickness can come. Even the strong have fragile hours."

The words were like a cold wind entering a window that had never been opened.

### Page 10

Another time, he went out through the western gate and saw a funeral procession.

People lowered their heads and walked heavily. The prince understood that life not only ages and sickens; it also comes to an end.

### Page 11

The sweetness in the palace had not changed, and the flowers still bloomed.

But Siddhartha's tongue tasted something else: what is hidden does not stop existing just because it is unseen.

### Page 12

At last, he went out through the northern gate and saw a wandering seeker.

The man wore simple clothes and carried no jewels, yet his face was quiet. In him, the prince saw another road: one that did not flee from reality and did not panic before it.

### Page 13

These four outings were like four doors opening one by one.

The old man, the sick man, the funeral, and the seeker were not there to frighten him. They set the true shape of life before him.

### Page 14

Siddhartha returned to the palace.

The musicians still played, dancers still turned, and sweet fruits still filled the plates. But he knew that if he hid all his life inside these pleasant things, his eyes would be covered by beauty.

### Page 15

Later, he left the palace to seek an answer to aging, sickness, and death.

This was not because he hated gardens, or because music and food were guilty. It was because even the most beautiful palace could not answer the real question in his heart.

### Page 16

When the children heard this, they grew quiet.

Little Stone asked, "Does that mean happiness is false?"

### Page 17

The old man shook his head. "No. Flowers are real, and music is real too."

"But if someone lets you see only flowers and never roots, only songs and never crying, then what you see is not whole."

### Page 18

Ahe remembered the blue bead from the market.

The bead itself was not bad. What was wrong was letting its brightness hide the bean seeds, his friend's call, and the promise he had made.

## Return

### Page 19

Chapter Twelve says that many colors can blind. Siddhartha's palace was full of many colors.

Beauty itself is not wrong. But if beauty is used to block reality, it becomes a shining wall.

### Page 20

"Many sounds can deafen" does not only mean that noise is too loud.

Sometimes it means that hearing only praise and music makes us unable to hear real suffering in the world or the true question in our own heart.

### Page 21

"For the belly, not for the eyes" means returning to what life truly needs.

The eyes may enjoy beauty, the ears may enjoy music, and the tongue may enjoy good food. But a person cannot live only by these.

### Page 22

Ahe wrote: being awake does not mean pushing away all beautiful things.

Being awake means seeing beauty without forgetting truth, and hearing liveliness while still hearing the call from quiet places.
